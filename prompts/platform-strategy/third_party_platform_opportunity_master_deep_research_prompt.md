# Third-Party Platform Opportunity Master Deep Research Prompt

This document provides a single master deep research prompt for identifying profitable third-party app opportunities across platform ecosystems.

It is designed to investigate:
- platform-specific pain points,
- underserved niches and communities,
- monetizable workflow gaps,
- trust and switching barriers,
- platform-native capability gaps,
- newly released or underexploited platform capabilities,
- marketplace saturation versus open opportunity,
- defensible ways a third-party developer can profit.

The perspective is explicitly that of a **third-party developer** looking for commercially meaningful opportunity inside an existing platform ecosystem.

This prompt is intended to be reusable across multiple ecosystems such as Twitch, HubSpot, Atlassian, Shopify, Monday.com, Webflow, Canva, Kick, and Google Workspace Marketplace.

---

## Master Prompt — Third-Party Platform Opportunity Discovery

```text
Conduct deep research using only publicly available information. Do not use interviews, surveys, invented examples, or unsupported speculation. Use current sources where recency matters. Distinguish clearly between:
1. verified facts,
2. reasoned inference,
3. uncertain or contested conclusions.

For every major claim, cite sources. Prioritize primary sources such as official platform documentation, developer docs, changelogs, marketplace listings, pricing pages, API references, app review ecosystems, public case studies, public support docs, creator or operator discussions, credible industry research, investor or company materials when relevant, and high-quality operator commentary when directly relevant.

Your task is not merely to describe a platform ecosystem. Your task is to identify commercially meaningful third-party app opportunities that a developer could realistically build and profit from.

The analysis must focus especially on:
- painful workflows,
- recurring operational friction,
- underserved niches or communities,
- weak or outdated marketplace solutions,
- monetizable trust and governance problems,
- opportunities created by new or underexploited platform capabilities,
- cases where the platform’s native offering is incomplete, too generic, too late, too shallow, or structurally unlikely to solve the problem well.

Approach the research from the perspective of a serious third-party developer who wants to build a profitable app business inside a platform ecosystem.

For each section of the answer, include:
- what is directly evidenced,
- what appears commercially important,
- what the market may be underestimating,
- what this implies for a third-party developer deciding what to build.

Where evidence is weak, unstable, or platform-specific, say so explicitly.

Now conduct deep research on the platform ecosystem of [INSERT PLATFORM].

Your goal is to identify the highest-value third-party developer opportunities for profitable app creation.

The analysis must cover the following themes in depth:

### 1. Platform Structure and Economic Logic
Analyze:
- the platform’s business model,
- who the platform serves,
- who installs or buys third-party apps,
- whether the ecosystem rewards breadth, specialization, workflow depth, compliance, automation, analytics, collaboration, content tooling, or other categories,
- whether the platform appears friendly, neutral, or hostile to third-party monetization.

### 2. Pain Point Discovery
Identify the most painful, recurring, and monetizable problems in the ecosystem.
Analyze:
- operational pain points,
- coordination pain points,
- workflow inefficiencies,
- reporting or analytics gaps,
- trust and governance gaps,
- admin burden,
- setup and onboarding friction,
- niche user frustrations,
- support complaints,
- review complaints that remain unresolved across multiple tools.

### 3. Underserved Niches and Communities
Identify user groups, business types, creator types, operators, admins, agencies, teams, or specialized communities that appear underserved.
Analyze:
- small but high-pain segments,
- niche roles,
- non-obvious user populations,
- communities ignored by existing tools,
- segments that may support premium pricing despite small size,
- segments that are too specific for the platform itself to prioritize.

### 4. Marketplace Saturation and White Space
Analyze the existing marketplace or ecosystem.
Identify:
- categories that are overcrowded,
- categories that look crowded but still have weak products,
- categories with old, low-quality, shallow, or abandoned apps,
- white-space areas with low competition,
- seemingly solved problems that still generate persistent complaints,
- opportunities where better execution, positioning, or specialization could still win.

### 5. New and Underexploited Platform Capabilities
Research the newest or least-saturated platform capabilities that third-party developers may be able to exploit.
Analyze:
- newly released APIs,
- new marketplace features,
- automation surfaces,
- data access changes,
- workflow primitives,
- AI-related capabilities,
- extensibility features,
- UI extension points,
- embedded experiences,
- eventing or integration opportunities,
- capabilities the platform added but the marketplace has not yet fully capitalized on.

Be explicit about:
- which capabilities appear meaningfully new,
- which seem commercially promising,
- which are likely to become saturated quickly,
- which may create unusually strong first-mover or early-mover opportunities.

### 6. Platform-Native Versus Third-Party Risk
Analyze the risk that the platform itself could absorb or kill the opportunity.
Evaluate:
- whether the pain point is too strategically core for third parties to trust,
- whether the platform has already signaled roadmap movement in that area,
- whether native offerings remain weak for structural reasons,
- whether a niche or depth strategy could remain defendable even if the platform expands.

### 7. Monetization and Profitability Logic
Evaluate how a third-party developer could realistically make money.
Analyze:
- pricing models that appear viable,
- subscription versus usage-based versus one-time models,
- attach rate logic,
- likely customer willingness to pay,
- support burden,
- implementation burden,
- niche economics,
- retention potential,
- expansion potential,
- whether the opportunity is likely to produce healthy or fragile margins.

### 8. Switching, Adoption, and Trust Barriers
Analyze what would make it hard or easy for users to adopt a third-party solution.
Evaluate:
- trust concerns,
- permission or security concerns,
- migration friction,
- setup complexity,
- change-management burden,
- whether the problem is painful enough to justify adoption effort,
- whether users are likely to try, pay, retain, and recommend the solution.

### 9. Priority Opportunity Mapping
Produce a ranked list of the most compelling third-party app opportunities in the ecosystem.
For each opportunity, evaluate:
- severity of pain,
- recurrence of pain,
- willingness to pay,
- saturation level,
- platform risk,
- technical feasibility,
- monetization potential,
- retention potential,
- differentiation potential,
- speed-to-market potential.

### 10. Strategic Recommendations for a Third-Party Developer
Based on the research, determine:
- which opportunity types appear strongest,
- which niches appear most attractive,
- which platform capabilities are most commercially promising,
- which areas should be avoided,
- what kind of third-party developer strategy is most likely to produce profits in this ecosystem.

Before finalizing the answer, perform an explicit internal adversarial review of your own conclusions. Identify at least five ways your reasoning may be weak, incomplete, biased, overly optimistic, or too dependent on limited public evidence, recent platform changes, or unclear monetization signals. Then revise the answer accordingly.

Use this exact output structure:
1. Executive Thesis
2. What the Platform Ecosystem Looks Like Economically
3. The Most Painful and Monetizable Problems
4. The Most Underserved Niches and Communities
5. Marketplace Saturation, Weak Incumbents, and White Space
6. New or Underexploited Platform Capabilities
7. Native-Platform Risk vs Third-Party Defensibility
8. Monetization and Profitability Logic
9. Adoption, Trust, and Switching Barriers
10. Ranked Third-Party App Opportunities
11. Areas to Avoid
12. Strategic Recommendations for a Third-Party Developer
13. Risks, Counterarguments, and Uncertainty
14. Source List
```
