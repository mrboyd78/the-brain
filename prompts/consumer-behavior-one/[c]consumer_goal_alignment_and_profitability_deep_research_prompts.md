# Consumer Goal Alignment and Profitability Deep Research Prompts

These prompts are designed for deep research on consumer goals, desired outcomes, progress-seeking behavior, willingness to pay, profitable segmentation, and customer-driven profitability conducted entirely through public information and LLM-assisted analysis, without interviews or surveys.

Each prompt is copy-and-paste ready and includes:
- master operating instructions
- a rigor add-on
- a consistent output format

## Prompt 1 — What Goals Are Consumers Actually Trying to Achieve in This Category?

```text
Conduct deep research using only publicly available information. Do not use interviews, surveys, invented examples, or unsupported speculation. Use current sources where recency matters. Distinguish clearly between:
1. verified facts,
2. reasoned inference,
3. uncertain or contested conclusions.

For every major claim, cite sources. Prioritize primary sources, official product pages, reviews, public case studies, comparison content, pricing pages, consumer discussions, help centers, credible industry research, marketplace listings, and high-quality operator commentary when directly relevant.

Your task is not merely to list features or complaints. Your task is to identify commercially meaningful consumer goals, desired outcomes, emotional and practical progress, behavioral tradeoffs, unmet needs, and strategic implications.

For each section of the answer, include:
- what is directly evidenced,
- what appears to be a real consumer goal rather than a surface preference,
- what the market may be underestimating,
- what this implies for a founder designing products around outcomes rather than features.

Where evidence is weak, speculative, or segment-specific, say so explicitly.

Now conduct deep research on the question: “What goals are consumers actually trying to achieve in the category of [INSERT PRODUCT OR CATEGORY]?”

Analyze:
- functional goals,
- emotional goals,
- convenience goals,
- social or status goals,
- risk-reduction goals,
- confidence and control goals,
- speed goals,
- cost-saving goals,
- self-improvement goals,
- identity-expression goals,
- frustration-reduction goals.

Then produce:
- a category-specific consumer-goal analysis,
- the strongest recurring goals,
- the weakest or least economically meaningful goals,
- a framework for distinguishing real consumer progress from minor preferences,
- a final recommendation for which goals a founder should optimize around first.

Before finalizing the answer, perform an explicit internal adversarial review of your own conclusions. Identify at least five ways your reasoning may be weak, incomplete, biased, overly optimistic, or too dependent on limited public evidence. Then revise the answer accordingly.

Use this exact output structure:
1. Executive Thesis
2. What the Evidence Shows
3. What Goals Consumers Are Actually Trying to Achieve
4. Weak or Low-Leverage Goals
5. Strategic Implications for a Founder
```
