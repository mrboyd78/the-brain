# Buyer Psychology and Purchase Motivation Deep Research Prompts

These prompts are designed for deep research on buyer psychology, purchase motivation, decision friction, justification logic, perceived risk, and willingness-to-act dynamics conducted entirely through public information and LLM-assisted analysis, without interviews or surveys.

Each prompt is copy-and-paste ready and includes:
- master operating instructions
- a rigor add-on
- a consistent output format

## Prompt 1 — What Actually Motivates Buyers to Purchase in This Category?

```text
Conduct deep research using only publicly available information. Do not use interviews, surveys, invented examples, or unsupported speculation. Use current sources where recency matters. Distinguish clearly between:
1. verified facts,
2. reasoned inference,
3. uncertain or contested conclusions.

For every major claim, cite sources. Prioritize primary sources, official product pages, pricing pages, documentation, review ecosystems, marketplace listings, public case studies, credible industry research, comparison content, buyer-facing materials, and high-quality operator commentary when directly relevant.

Your task is not merely to summarize customer needs. Your task is to identify commercially meaningful purchase motives, emotional and operational drivers, perceived risks, justification patterns, inertia barriers, and strategic implications.

For each section of the answer, include:
- what is directly evidenced,
- what appears to drive real purchase behavior rather than stated preference,
- what the market may be underestimating,
- what this implies for a founder designing product, positioning, pricing, and go-to-market.

Where evidence is weak, speculative, or category-specific, say so explicitly.

Now conduct deep research on the question: “What actually motivates buyers to purchase in the category of [INSERT PRODUCT OR CATEGORY]?”

Analyze motivations such as:
- saving time,
- reducing risk,
- reducing labor,
- increasing control,
- avoiding embarrassment or operational failure,
- satisfying internal stakeholders,
- simplifying complexity,
- improving trust and auditability,
- producing measurable ROI,
- gaining status or professionalism,
- replacing brittle manual work,
- escaping frustration with current tools.

Then produce:
- a category-specific purchase-motivation analysis,
- the strongest real buying motives,
- the weakest or most cosmetic motivations,
- a framework for distinguishing surface-level interest from spend-driving motivation,
- a final recommendation for what a founder should optimize around first.

Before finalizing the answer, perform an explicit internal adversarial review of your own conclusions. Identify at least five ways your reasoning may be weak, incomplete, biased, overly optimistic, or too dependent on limited public evidence. Then revise the answer accordingly.

Use this exact output structure:
1. Executive Thesis
2. What the Evidence Shows
3. What Actually Motivates Purchase in This Category
4. Weak or Cosmetic Motives
5. Strategic Implications for a Founder
6. Risks, Counterarguments, and Uncertainty
```
