# Goldman Sachs / CFO-Grade Solopreneur Finance and Capital Allocation Deep Research Prompts

These prompts are designed for deep research on running a one-person business with **CFO-grade financial discipline and capital allocation rigor**.

They are tailored for the perspective of a **solopreneur founder** who wants to think about money the way serious finance leaders do:
- liquidity before optimism,
- capital allocation before random spending,
- margin structure before vanity revenue,
- downside planning before shock,
- return on investment before expansion,
- and enterprise value creation before mere busyness.

This is not a pack for generic bookkeeping or casual budgeting advice.

This is a pack for asking:
- where cash is actually going,
- which uses of capital produce the best returns,
- how resilient the business is under stress,
- how to protect liquidity while still growing,
- how to increase value without simply increasing workload,
- and how to think like a disciplined finance operator in a business of one.

These prompts are intentionally designed to emulate the rigor, structure, and decision quality associated with CFO-grade and investment-grade thinking, while translating those ideas into a one-person business context.

Each prompt is copy-and-paste ready and includes:
- master operating instructions
- a rigor add-on
- a consistent output format

## What This Pack Is Supposed to Prevent

This pack is designed to prevent the following strategic mistakes:
- treating finance as bookkeeping instead of strategic control
- mistaking bank balance for true liquidity
- allocating capital casually instead of by expected return and survival value
- growing revenue while letting margin quality erode
- using financing to hide weak economics
- extracting too much cash too early through owner draws
- running no serious forecasting or downside scenarios
- reviewing numbers passively instead of using thresholds and triggers
- building current income without building enterprise value
- borrowing enterprise-finance ideas without adapting them to the realities of a business of one

The goal is to force clarity on how a solopreneur should protect cash, allocate capital, preserve resilience, and create financial value with the seriousness of an executive finance function rather than the vagueness of casual small-business advice.
