# McKinsey-Grade Solopreneur Strategy Deep Research Prompts

These prompts are designed for deep research on running a one-person business with **management-consulting-grade strategic rigor**.

They are tailored for the perspective of a **solopreneur founder** who wants advice at the level of:
- structured diagnosis,
- strategy-before-tactics thinking,
- operating-model clarity,
- disciplined resource allocation,
- pricing and margin management,
- customer-journey design,
- productivity improvement,
- risk management,
- and measurable growth logic.

This is not a pack for casual motivational advice.

This is a pack for asking:
- what the business should do,
- what it should stop doing,
- where value is created,
- where time and capital are being wasted,
- which moves actually drive growth and margin,
- and how to think about a solo business with boardroom-level seriousness.

These prompts are intentionally designed to emulate the rigor, structure, and decision quality associated with top-tier strategy consulting work, especially the kinds of themes McKinsey & Company frequently publishes on:
- productivity,
- pricing,
- operating models,
- customer journeys,
- resource allocation,
- growth strategy,
- talent-to-value,
- and transformation discipline.

Each prompt is copy-and-paste ready and includes:
- master operating instructions
- a rigor add-on
- a consistent output format

## Prompt 1 — Build a McKinsey-Grade Strategic Operating Model for a Solopreneur Business

```text
Conduct deep research using only publicly available information. Do not use interviews, surveys, invented examples, unsupported anecdotes, or casual creator advice unless it is clearly labeled as secondary commentary. Use current sources where recency matters.

Distinguish clearly between:
1. verified facts,
2. reasoned inference,
3. uncertain or contested conclusions.

Prioritize sources in this order:
1. McKinsey & Company and McKinsey Global Institute materials,
2. official government or regulatory sources when relevant,
3. high-authority management, accounting, or economic sources,
4. high-quality operator commentary only as secondary support.

Your task is not merely to summarize McKinsey articles. Your task is to synthesize a McKinsey-grade strategic operating model for a solopreneur business.

The analysis must explicitly cover:
- value creation,
- operating model design,
- resource allocation,
- founder role design,
- growth logic,
- productivity levers,
- pricing and margin logic,
- customer value delivery,
- management cadence,
- and strategic simplification.

You must treat the solopreneur business like a serious enterprise with severe resource constraints, not like a lifestyle side project.

For each section of the answer, include:
- what is directly evidenced,
- what appears strategically critical,
- what solopreneurs typically underestimate,
- what this implies for how the business should actually be run.

Now conduct deep research on the question: “What would a McKinsey-grade strategic operating model for a solopreneur business look like if the goal were to maximize value creation, productivity, clarity, and profitable growth?”

Then produce:
- a complete strategic operating-model map,
- the most critical design choices,
- the highest-value management disciplines,
- the strongest simplification moves,
- a final recommendation for how a solopreneur should run the business with consultant-grade rigor.

Before finalizing the answer, perform an explicit internal adversarial review of your own conclusions. Identify at least five ways the reasoning may be weak, incomplete, too generic, too tactical, or insufficiently strategic. Then revise the answer accordingly.

Use this exact output structure:
1. Executive Thesis
2. Strategic Diagnosis
3. The Solopreneur Operating Model
4. Value-Creation Logic
5. Productivity and Resource-Allocation Implications
6. Growth and Margin Implications
7. Strategic Risks and Blind Spots
8. Final Recommendations
9. Source List
```

## What This Pack Is Supposed to Prevent

This pack is designed to prevent the following strategic mistakes:
- treating a solo business like a hustle instead of a serious value-creation system
- confusing tactical busyness with strategy
- underpricing because pricing is treated emotionally rather than structurally
- failing to reallocate time, money, and attention dynamically
- building growth through random initiatives instead of an intentional growth model
- assuming customer experience is “soft” rather than a driver of revenue and efficiency
- ignoring the founder’s role portfolio as a strategic allocation problem
- expanding into too many offers, channels, and activities without pruning
- avoiding downside planning because the business is small
- borrowing enterprise frameworks without adapting them to the realities of a business of one

The goal is to force clarity on how a solopreneur should think, decide, allocate, prune, price, and grow with the seriousness of a strategy firm rather than the vagueness of internet business advice.
