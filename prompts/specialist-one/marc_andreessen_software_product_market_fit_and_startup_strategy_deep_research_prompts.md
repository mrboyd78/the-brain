# Marc Andreessen Software, Product-Market Fit, and Startup Strategy Deep Research Prompts

These prompts are designed for deep research on the **startup, product, venture, and technology insights of Marc Andreessen**.

They are specifically designed for the perspective of a **founder, solopreneur, SaaS builder, technical entrepreneur, operator, or early-stage strategist** who wants to understand:
- what Marc Andreessen actually teaches,
- how he thinks about product-market fit,
- why he argues market matters more than product or team in many startup outcomes,
- how software waves reshape industries,
- how he thinks about startup formation and founder judgment,
- how his build-and-techno-optimist worldview connects to company creation,
- how his ideas apply to software and SaaS businesses,
- and where his frameworks are strongest, most transferable, or context-bound.

The goal is to avoid a common mistake:

**reducing Marc Andreessen to a few famous lines about software or product-market fit, while missing his broader doctrines about startup risk, market power, founder-led company formation, software-driven industry change, and technology-enabled building.**

These prompts are built around his primary and official materials, especially themes reflected across:
- Andreessen Horowitz official materials,
- the Pmarca startup essays preserved at Pmarchive,
- Stanford eCorner talks and clips,
- his “Why Software Is Eating the World” essay,
- “It’s Time to Build,”
- and his broader public technology worldview.

Each prompt is copy-and-paste ready and includes:
- master operating instructions
- a rigor add-on
- a consistent output format

## What This Pack Is Supposed to Prevent

This pack is designed to prevent the following strategic mistakes:
- reducing Marc Andreessen’s work to a few slogans
- confusing PMF with traction alone
- underestimating the role of market power in startup outcomes
- using “software is eating the world” as a cliché rather than a strategic thesis
- ignoring the founder-led nature of early company formation
- misunderstanding his CEO doctrine as generic anti-management advice
- flattening his build and techno-optimist worldview into empty rhetoric
- forcing his venture logic into every business context without adaptation
- borrowing his language without understanding the doctrines behind it
- missing how market, software, founder quality, and build orientation fit together in his worldview

The goal is to force clarity on what Marc Andreessen actually teaches, how his ideas fit together, where they are strongest, and how to apply them without reducing them to generic venture wisdom.
