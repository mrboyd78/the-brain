# Clayton Christensen Disruption, Jobs to Be Done, and Innovation Strategy Deep Research Prompts

These prompts are designed for deep research on the **strategy insights, frameworks, and teachings of Clayton Christensen**.

They are specifically designed for the perspective of a **founder, solopreneur, SaaS builder, operator, strategist, product business, or investor** who wants to understand:
- what Christensen actually taught,
- how disruptive innovation works,
- how Jobs to Be Done fits into innovation strategy,
- how business models shape capabilities and constraints,
- how modularity changes competitive dynamics,
- how incumbents should respond to disruption,
- where Christensen’s theories are strongest,
- and where his work is limited, contested, or misapplied.

The goal is to avoid a common mistake:

**reducing Christensen to the slogan that “disruption means new technology beats incumbents,” while missing the deeper logic of nonconsumption, asymmetric motivation, jobs, business models, and capability fit.**

These prompts are built around Christensen’s real body of work and the current Christensen Institute theory set, especially:
- disruptive innovation,
- Jobs to Be Done,
- business models,
- modularity,
- tools of cooperation,
- and innovation strategy.

Each prompt is copy-and-paste ready and includes:
- master operating instructions
- a rigor add-on
- a consistent output format

## Prompt 1 — Build a Complete Map of Clayton Christensen’s Innovation Philosophy

```text
Conduct deep research using only publicly available information. Do not use invented examples, unsupported anecdotes, or secondhand summaries unless clearly labeled as secondary commentary. Use current official and high-authority sources where relevant.

Distinguish clearly between:
1. verified facts,
2. reasoned inference,
3. uncertain or contested conclusions.

Prioritize sources in this order:
1. Clayton Christensen Institute materials,
2. Christensen’s books and official summaries,
3. Harvard-related primary interviews and talks,
4. high-quality commentary only as secondary support.

Your task is not merely to summarize Christensen. Your task is to synthesize the full architecture of his innovation and strategy philosophy.

The analysis must explicitly cover:
- disruptive innovation,
- Jobs to Be Done,
- business model theory,
- modularity,
- capability constraints,
- managerial incentives,
- incumbent failure,
- and the relationship among these frameworks.

Now conduct deep research on the question: “What is the complete underlying innovation philosophy Clayton Christensen teaches across his major theories and books?”

Then produce:
- a complete philosophy map,
- the core recurring frameworks,
- the strategic logic connecting them,
- the major tradeoffs and caveats,
- and a final recommendation for how to interpret Christensen accurately.

Before finalizing, perform an explicit internal adversarial review identifying at least five ways your reasoning may be weak, incomplete, or overly reductive, then revise.

Use this exact output structure:
1. Executive Thesis
2. Christensen’s Core Innovation Philosophy
3. Main Frameworks and Mental Models
4. Strategic Tradeoffs and Caveats
5. Common Misreadings
6. Strategic Implications for Founders and Strategists
7. Risks, Counterarguments, and Uncertainty
```

## What This Pack Is Supposed to Prevent

This pack is designed to prevent the following strategic mistakes:
- using “disruption” as a synonym for innovation
- applying JTBD superficially
- ignoring business-model asymmetry
- underestimating incumbent capability traps
- missing the role of nonconsumption
- overlooking modularity and architecture shifts
- forcing consumer examples onto enterprise software without adaptation
- treating Christensen’s theories as slogans rather than analytical tools
- overextending his frameworks into contexts where they are weak
- borrowing his language without understanding the doctrine behind it

The goal is to force clarity on what Christensen actually taught, how his frameworks fit together, where they are strongest, and how they should be applied without caricature.
