# 02 — McKinsey-Grade Advice on Solopreneur Resource Allocation (Time, Money, and Attention)

### 1. Executive Thesis
The primary driver of long-term value creation for a solopreneur is not operational efficiency, but **dynamic resource reallocation**. McKinsey research demonstrates that "high reallocators" achieve 30% higher returns than static counterparts. For a solopreneur, where the "firm" and the "founder" are resource-identical, the failure to aggressively shift time, money, and attention away from legacy activities toward high-yield opportunities is the leading cause of "strategic drift"—a state where the business survives but fails to capture its potential economic profit.

### 2. What Resource Allocation Must Actually Optimize
Resource allocation for a solopreneur must optimize for **Return on Management (ROM)**, a framework developed by Robert Simons. ROM is defined as:
$$\text{ROM} = \frac{\text{Productive Energy Released}}{\text{Management Attention Invested}}$$

Unlike a corporation, a solopreneur cannot optimize for ROE (Return on Equity) if the ROM is negative. If an activity requires 80% of founder attention but generates only 20% of revenue (a common "peanut buttering" error), the business is technically insolvent from a resource-utilization perspective, even if it is profitable on paper.

### 3. Time, Money, and Attention Allocation Framework
A solopreneur should deploy resources across **Three Horizons of Value**, adapted from McKinsey’s growth model:

| Horizon | Focus | Resource Split (Target) | Metric of Success |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **H1: Core Engine** | Exploit current proven revenue. | 70% Time / 50% Cash | Cash Flow / Efficiency |
| **H2: Emerging** | Scale validated experiments. | 20% Time / 30% Cash | Conversion Rate / Growth |
| **H3: Options** | High-risk "small bets." | 10% Time / 20% Cash | Optionality / Learning |

*   **Founder Attention as Capital:** Treat every hour of focus as a "unit of capital." H3 activities should be "attention-light" (automated or outsourced) to protect the founder's "Deep Work" capacity for H1 and H2.
*   **Capital Deployment:** Cash should follow the **Barbell Strategy**: 80% in "Safe/Yield" assets (proven marketing, core tools) and 20% in "Aggressive/Growth" (unproven channels, R&D).

### 4. Dynamic Reallocation Doctrine
The "sluggish reallocator" trap is the solopreneur’s greatest risk. A dynamic doctrine requires:
*   **The 10% Reallocation Rule:** At minimum, 10% of time and budget must be moved from the lowest-performing H1 activities to H2 or H3 every quarter.
*   **Zero-Based Attention Budgeting:** Every 90 days, the founder must "re-hire" themselves. Instead of asking "What do I do next week?", ask "If I were starting today with my current cash and reputation, how would I spend my next 40 hours?"
*   **The Power Curve Pivot:** Identify the 20% of clients or activities driving 80% of economic profit. Aggressively "fire" or automate the bottom 20% of "squeaky wheel" tasks that consume disproportionate ROM.

### 5. Failure Modes and Warning Signs
*   **"Peanut Buttering":** Spreading attention evenly across five projects so that none achieve the "critical mass" required to move up the Power Curve.
*   **Liquidity Mismatch:** Allocating cash to fixed assets (high-end branding, permanent software seats) before the revenue model is validated.
*   **The "Social Gravity" of Habits:** Continuing a low-ROI marketing channel simply because "it's what I've always done."
*   **Sleepy Allocation Warning Signs:**
    *   Next month's calendar looks 90% identical to last month's.
    *   Cash is accumulating in a low-yield checking account while H2 opportunities are unfunded.
    *   The founder feels "busy" (high denominator in ROM) but "stagnant" (low numerator).

### 6. Strategic Implications for a Solopreneur Founder
For the solopreneur, **simplification is a form of capital injection.** Every project eliminated releases "Attention Capital" back into the system. In a resource-constrained environment, expansion is often a "hidden tax" on the core engine. A solopreneur should prioritize "Workflow Depth" (higher margin per unit of attention) over "Market Breadth" (more products/services).

### 7. Risks, Counterarguments, and Uncertainty
**Internal Adversarial Review (5 Weaknesses):**
1.  **Over-indexing on McKinsey Corporate Data:** TRS data for 1,500 firms may not scale down perfectly to a one-person business where survival depends on diversification more than "bold moves."
2.  **Context-Switching Tax:** The "Three Horizons" model assumes separate teams. For a solopreneur, moving between H1 and H3 creates a significant "switching cost" that the framework may underestimate.
3.  **The "10% Rule" may be too low:** For a solopreneur, a 10% annual shift might be too slow to survive AI-driven market volatility. 25-30% may be the necessary "Survival Reallocation Rate."
4.  **Optionality vs. Focus:** High-risk H3 "bets" can easily become "shiny object syndrome," diluting the core engine's ROM.
5.  **Capital Scarcity:** Large firms reallocate *excess* capital; solopreneurs often reallocate *vital* capital, where one bad H3 bet could end the firm.

### 8. Final Recommendations
1.  **Conduct an ROM Audit:** Calculate your "Productive Energy" versus "Attention Invested" for your top three projects. If any project has a low ROM, it is a candidate for immediate divestment.
2.  **Implement a Rolling 90-Day Reallocation:** Set a hard requirement to shift 15% of your time/money toward a new H2 or H3 initiative every quarter.
3.  **Prioritize "Attention Filters":** Use AI and automation not just for speed, but as a "Simon Filter" to protect your serial-processing bottleneck from low-value data.
4.  **Aggressively Cut "Peanut Butter" Projects:** If you are pursuing more than two major growth initiatives, you are likely spreading your attention too thin to achieve escape velocity.
