# Fact Preservation and Semantic Integrity in Context Compression

## Introduction

Every context compression technique — from token pruning and prompt compression to hierarchical summarization and continuous embedding compression — faces a common critical challenge: ensuring that the information that survives compression is factually accurate, semantically faithful, and sufficient for downstream reasoning. A compression system that reduces token count by 10x but corrupts a critical fact, omits a key constraint, or subtly distorts the meaning of a passage may produce worse outcomes than no compression at all. This report provides a comprehensive analysis of the fact preservation and semantic integrity challenges inherent in context compression, examining failure modes, evaluation methodologies, and state-of-the-art techniques for detecting and preventing information corruption during compression. The analysis spans token-level pruning, abstractive summarization, and embedding-based compression, with actionable frameworks for measuring and ensuring compression fidelity in production LLM systems.

---

## 1. Theoretical Foundations and Historical Context

### 1.1 The Information-Fidelity Trade-off

Context compression fundamentally operates on the rate-distortion curve from information theory. Given a source (the original context) with entropy H, any compression to a representation of fewer than H bits will necessarily lose some information [1]. The design challenge is to ensure that the lost information is the least important for the downstream task — that compression distortion falls on redundant, low-value content rather than critical facts.

Three distinct types of information loss can occur during compression:

- **Omission:** Important information is entirely absent from the compressed representation. This is the most obvious and detectable failure mode.
- **Distortion:** Information is present but altered — numerical values changed, relationships reversed, conditional statements made unconditional, or temporal relationships disrupted.
- **Fabrication (Hallucination):** The compression process introduces information not present in the original context. This can occur during abstractive summarization when the summarization model "fills in" details that seem plausible but are incorrect.

### 1.2 Task-Dependent Criticality

The tolerance for information loss is highly task-dependent:

- **Legal and Medical Applications:** Near-zero tolerance for factual errors. A single distorted number, inverted negation, or omitted condition can have catastrophic consequences.
- **Customer Support:** Moderate tolerance. Key customer details and issue descriptions must be preserved, but conversational filler can be freely compressed.
- **Creative Writing and Brainstorming:** High tolerance. Thematic preservation matters more than factual precision.
- **Code Generation:** Mixed tolerance. Variable names, logic structures, and API contracts must be preserved exactly, while comments and documentation can be compressed.

### 1.3 The Faithfulness Problem in Summarization

Research in automatic summarization has extensively documented "unfaithfulness" — where summaries contain claims not supported by the source document [2]. Studies estimate that 30-70% of abstractive summaries generated by LLMs contain at least one unfaithful claim, ranging from benign paraphrasing errors to substantive factual fabrications. This unfaithfulness problem directly applies to any context compression method that involves abstractive transformation of the original text.

## 2. State-of-the-Art Review and Leading Methodologies

### 2.1 Factual Consistency Evaluation Metrics

Several automated metrics have been developed to assess whether compressed/summarized text remains faithful to its source:

**QA-Based Evaluation (QAFactEval, QuestEval):**
1. Generate factual questions from the source document (e.g., "What was the delivery date?" "How many units were ordered?").
2. Answer these questions using only the compressed text.
3. Compare the answers from source-derived and compression-derived QA.
4. The agreement rate is the factual consistency score.

This approach is effective at detecting omission and distortion but expensive to compute and limited by the question generation model's coverage.

**NLI-Based Evaluation (SummaC, AlignScore):**
1. Decompose the compressed text into individual claims (atomic propositions).
2. For each claim, use a Natural Language Inference (NLI) model to determine if it is entailed, contradicted, or neutral with respect to the source.
3. Claims that are contradicted represent distortions; claims that are neutral may represent hallucinations.

NLI-based evaluation is faster than QA-based methods and provides per-claim granularity, but accuracy depends on the NLI model's capability.

**BERTScore and Semantic Similarity:**
Compute semantic similarity between source and compressed text using contextual embeddings. While useful as a coarse quality signal, these metrics cannot distinguish between legitimate paraphrasing and subtle factual distortion — two texts can have high semantic similarity while disagreeing on critical factual details.

**LLM-as-Judge (G-Eval, GPT-4 Evaluation):**
Use a powerful LLM to evaluate the factual consistency of compressed text against the source. This is the most flexible approach and correlates well with human judgments, but is expensive and introduces model-specific biases.

### 2.2 Extractive vs. Abstractive Compression: Fidelity Properties

The type of compression fundamentally determines the nature of fidelity risks:

**Extractive Methods (Token Pruning, Sentence Selection, RECOMP-Extractive):**
- **Fidelity Profile:** High semantic integrity for retained content (original words preserved), but risk of contextual distortion — removing surrounding tokens can change the meaning of retained tokens. A sentence like "The system is NOT ready for production" pruned to "The system is ready for production" illustrates this catastrophic failure mode.
- **Failure Modes:** Negation loss, conditional clause removal, reference chain breakage (pronouns left dangling after antecedent removal).

**Abstractive Methods (RECOMP-Abstractive, Recursive Summarization, LLM-Generated Summaries):**
- **Fidelity Profile:** Can rephrase and restructure for clarity, but introduces hallucination risk. The generative model may produce plausible but incorrect details, especially for numerical data, proper names, and relational claims.
- **Failure Modes:** Numerical hallucination (dates, quantities, percentages), entity confusion (attributing statements to the wrong entity), causal inversion (reversing cause and effect), and scope errors (generalizing specific claims or specifying general ones).

**Continuous Embedding Compression (AutoCompressor, ICAE):**
- **Fidelity Profile:** Information preservation is implicit and unverifiable at the token level. Summary vectors may retain the "gist" while losing exact details. There is no way to inspect which facts are preserved without querying the downstream model.
- **Failure Modes:** Silent information loss (facts simply absent from the compressed representation with no way to detect without testing), precision degradation (retaining the topic but losing exact values), and temporal confusion (merging information from different time periods).

### 2.3 Techniques for Improving Fact Preservation

**Constrained Summarization:** Training summarization models with explicit faithfulness constraints — penalizing hallucinated entities, requiring numerical consistency with the source, and enforcing logical entailment between source and summary.

**Post-Compression Verification:** After compression, applying an automated factual consistency checker (NLI or QA-based) to flag potential fidelity violations before the compressed text is used for downstream tasks. Flagged compressions can be regenerated with lower compression ratios or reviewed manually.

**Importance-Weighted Compression:** Assigning explicit importance scores to different content types and applying differential compression — numerical data, named entities, negations, and conditional clauses receive minimal or zero compression, while descriptive and connective text is compressed aggressively. LLMLingua's budget controller implements a version of this approach.

**Dense-and-Sparse Decomposition (KVQuant-inspired):** For numerical and entity-critical content, maintaining a parallel "sparse" representation that preserves exact critical values at full precision, while the "dense" compressed representation handles general semantic content. At retrieval time, both representations are merged.

**Fact Extraction and Verification Pipeline:**
1. Before compression: Extract all factual claims as structured triples (subject, predicate, object).
2. After compression: Re-extract claims from the compressed representation.
3. Compare: Identify missing, distorted, or hallucinated claims.
4. Remediate: Append missing critical facts as a "fact sheet" supplement to the compressed text.

### 2.4 Semantic Integrity in Multi-Hop Reasoning

A particularly challenging scenario for fact preservation occurs in multi-hop reasoning, where the answer requires combining facts from multiple locations in the context:

**Challenge:** If a context contains "Company X acquired Company Y in 2023" in one passage and "Company Y's flagship product is Product Z" in another, the compressed representation must preserve both facts AND their linkability (Company Y appears in both). Token-level pruning might remove one passage, and abstractive summarization might merge the facts incorrectly.

**Solution:** Graph-based fact representation that explicitly models entity links across passages, ensuring that bridge entities (like Company Y) are never compressed away and their relationships to other entities are preserved in the compressed representation.

## 3. Practical Implementations, Challenges, and Case Studies

### 3.1 Evaluation Framework for Production Systems

A comprehensive fact preservation evaluation framework should assess:

| Dimension | Metric | Method | Acceptable Threshold (Production) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Completeness** | Fact recall rate | QA-based evaluation | >95% for critical facts |
| **Accuracy** | Factual consistency | NLI-based per-claim verification | >99% (no contradictions) |
| **Coherence** | Narrative coherence score | LLM-as-judge evaluation | >90% human-equivalent |
| **Entity Integrity** | Named entity preservation | NER comparison source vs. compressed | >98% for key entities |
| **Numerical Integrity** | Number preservation | Regex extraction and comparison | 100% for critical numbers |
| **Negation Integrity** | Negation preservation | Rule-based negation detection | 100% (zero distortion) |
| **Conditional Integrity** | Conditional clause retention | Syntactic analysis | >95% |

### 3.2 Case Study: Legal Document Compression

A legal services company implements prompt compression for contract review:

**Original:** A 15,000-token contract compressed to 3,000 tokens (5x compression) for LLM analysis.

**Failure Mode Detected:** The clause "The licensee shall NOT sublicense without prior written consent" was compressed to "The licensee shall sublicense with prior written consent" — a catastrophic negation loss that inverted the legal obligation.

**Mitigation Applied:**
1. Negation tokens (NOT, never, shall not, cannot) assigned maximum importance scores and exempted from compression.
2. Post-compression NLI verification added as a mandatory pipeline step.
3. Legal-domain fine-tuned compressor trained to recognize and preserve legal conditions.
4. Result: Zero negation loss errors over 10,000 subsequent contracts.

### 3.3 Case Study: Multi-Turn Customer Support

A customer support agent using recursive summarization across a 50-turn conversation:

**Failure Mode Detected:** After 30 turns of recursive summarization, the customer's original complaint ("product arrived damaged") was summarized through multiple iterations as "product quality concern" → "general feedback" → lost entirely. The agent lost track of the core issue.

**Mitigation Applied:**
1. Entity-tagged summarization that permanently pins key issue descriptors.
2. Dual-track memory: text summary for narrative context + structured issue tracker for core facts.
3. Periodic re-anchoring from archived full transcript every 10 summarization cycles.
4. Result: Core issue preserved across 100+ turn conversations.

| Compression Method | Omission Risk | Distortion Risk | Hallucination Risk | Verifiability | Mitigation Difficulty |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Token Pruning** | High (token-level gaps) | Moderate (context loss) | None (no generation) | High (text inspection) | Low (importance scoring) |
| **Sentence Extraction** | Moderate (sentence-level gaps) | Low (preserved context) | None | High | Low |
| **Abstractive Summarization** | Low (rephrases, doesn't omit) | Moderate (paraphrasing errors) | High (hallucination) | Moderate (NLI/QA needed) | High |
| **Recursive Summarization** | Very High (cascading loss) | High (cascading drift) | High (compound hallucination) | Low (history needed) | Very High |
| **Embedding Compression** | Unknown (opaque) | Unknown (opaque) | Unknown (opaque) | Very Low (probing required) | Very High |
| **Knowledge Graph + Summary** | Low (structured facts preserved) | Low (triple verification) | Low (constrained extraction) | High (graph inspection) | Moderate |

## 5. Identified Challenges, Proposed Solutions, and Future Research Avenues

### 5.1 The Verification Cost Problem

**Challenge:** High-quality factual verification (QA-based or LLM-as-judge) can be as expensive as the original inference, negating the cost savings of compression.

**Solution:** Lightweight, specialized verification models trained specifically for compression fidelity checking (analogous to LLMLingua-2's small BERT-based compressor). These models could run at 10-50x the speed of general-purpose LLMs while providing sufficient verification accuracy. Alternatively, verification can be applied probabilistically — checking a random sample of compressions rather than every one, with full verification triggered only for high-stakes contexts.

### 5.2 Domain-Specific Fidelity Requirements

**Challenge:** Different domains have radically different fidelity requirements, and a universal compression system cannot know which facts are "critical" without domain knowledge. A number that is trivial in casual conversation may be crucial in financial analysis.

**Solution:** Domain-specific fidelity profiles that define, for each application domain, which content types require absolute preservation (numbers, entities, conditions) and which are compressible. These profiles can be expressed as configuration for compression systems, enabling the same underlying compressor to operate at different fidelity levels for different applications.

### 5.3 Compositional Fidelity

**Challenge:** When multiple compression steps are composed (e.g., token pruning + abstractive summarization + recursive summarization), each step may individually preserve fidelity but the composition may not. Fidelity guarantees are not compositional — two 99% accurate steps yield only 98.01% accuracy in composition, and real production pipelines may have 5-10 compression stages.

**Solution:** End-to-end fidelity testing that evaluates the entire compression pipeline as a unit rather than individual stages. Development of compositional fidelity bounds that propagate worst-case accuracy estimates through the pipeline, enabling architects to predict the overall fidelity from component-level specifications.

## 6. Emerging Trends, Future Directions, and Broader Impact

### 6.1 Compression-Aware Training

Future LLMs may be trained to explicitly handle compressed inputs, developing internal error-correction capabilities that can reconstruct missing information from partial cues. By training on examples where facts are deliberately omitted or distorted, models could learn to identify and flag gaps in their context, requesting decompression or clarification when needed.

### 6.2 Certified Compression

Borrowing from formal verification, future compression systems may provide certificates — mathematical guarantees that specific properties of the original text are preserved in the compressed representation. For example, a certificate might guarantee that all named entities in the source appear in the compression, or that all negations are preserved with correct scope. While full formal verification is intractable for natural language, targeted property verification for structured content (entities, numbers, logical operators) is feasible.

### 6.3 Human-in-the-Loop Compression

For high-stakes applications, compression systems may present a "fact diff" to human reviewers — showing which facts were preserved, which were modified, and which were omitted, in a format analogous to code diffs. This enables efficient human verification of compression quality without requiring reviewers to read both the full and compressed texts.

### 6.4 Self-Correcting Compression Pipelines

Future systems will implement closed-loop compression where the downstream model's uncertainty or error rate feeds back into the compression decisions. If the model produces low-confidence outputs, the system automatically decompresses (retrieves fuller context) and retries, creating an adaptive compression system that uses minimal tokens when possible and full context when necessary.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Factual Consistency** | Agreement between claims in compressed text and the source document |
| **Faithfulness** | Property of a summary that makes only claims supported by the source |
| **Hallucination** | Generated content not supported by or contradicting the source material |
| **Omission** | Important information absent from the compressed representation |
| **Distortion** | Information present but altered in meaning or accuracy |
| **NLI** | Natural Language Inference — determining if one text entails another |
| **QAFactEval** | QA-based factual evaluation comparing answers from source and summary |
| **SummaC** | Summary Consistency evaluation using NLI at the sentence level |
| **AlignScore** | Metric combining NLI, QA, and information alignment for fidelity assessment |
| **Rate-Distortion** | Information-theoretic trade-off between compression rate and quality loss |
| **Entity Integrity** | Preservation of named entities and their relationships through compression |
| **Negation Preservation** | Correct retention of negations, conditions, and logical operators |
| **Compositional Fidelity** | Fidelity of the overall pipeline when multiple compression steps are composed |

## Conclusion

Fact preservation and semantic integrity represent the most critical yet often under-addressed dimension of context compression. The field has matured from treating compression purely as a token-reduction exercise to recognizing that the fidelity of compressed information determines the ceiling of downstream task performance.

The key takeaways for practitioners:

1. **Extractive compression is safest** for fact preservation but limited in compression ratio. When factual accuracy is paramount (legal, medical, financial), extractive methods should be preferred.

2. **Abstractive compression requires verification.** Any system using LLM-generated summaries for context compression must implement automated factual consistency checking (NLI or QA-based) as a mandatory pipeline step.

3. **Recursive summarization requires anchoring.** Without periodic re-generation from archived full text, recursive summarization will inevitably drift. Design memory systems with this constraint in mind.

4. **Critical content types need explicit protection.** Negations, numbers, named entities, and conditional clauses must be identified and protected during compression through importance weighting or exemption.

5. **Continuous embedding compression requires downstream probing.** Since summary vectors are opaque, fidelity can only be assessed by testing the downstream model's ability to recall specific facts — build fact-recall test suites for all compressed knowledge.

The strategic direction points toward certified compression systems that provide formal guarantees about which properties of the source are preserved, enabling principled deployment in high-stakes applications. Until such guarantees are available, the recommendation is to treat compression as a lossy operation requiring verification, not an optimization that can be applied blindly.
