# Goals to Avoid: Third-Party App Ecosystems (Shopify, Atlassian, HubSpot)

## 1. Executive Thesis
In the high-stakes environment of third-party ecosystems, the most dangerous goals for a founder are "False Positives"—problems that are intellectually obvious but commercially weak. These "Tarpit Ideas" often center on **Remedial Tasks**, **Native Feature Clones**, and **Vanity Aesthetics**. Building for these goals results in high initial interest but catastrophic churn, low price sensitivity, and a high risk of being "Sherlocked" by the parent platform. A founder must explicitly avoid building for goals that lack **Operational Recurrence** or **Data Gravity**.

## 2. What the Evidence Shows
*   **The "Sherlocking" Trap:** Platforms like Shopify and Atlassian have a history of absorbing successful third-party features into their core product (e.g., Shopify adding native "Local Pickup" or "Subscriptions"). Evidence shows that apps built solely on these gaps are "false positives" that can disappear overnight (Source [1][2]).
*   **One-Off Churn Patterns:** Analysis of "Crisis Fix" tools (e.g., bulk data deleters or migration assistants) reveals a pattern of high install spikes followed by 90%+ churn within 30 days. These apps solve a real problem but fail to provide recurring value (Source [1]).
*   **Commoditization of Basic Sync:** HubSpot's acquisition of PieSync and its rollout of native "Data Sync" has commoditized the goal of simple contact synchronization. Apps that only offer basic sync without "Enrichment" or "Intelligence" are now commercially unviable (Source [4][6]).
*   **Vanity Metric Satiation:** In the Shopify ecosystem, "Trust Badges" and "Countdown Timers" have become so saturated and associated with "spammy" marketing that professional merchants (the high-LTV segment) actively avoid them (Source [1][3]).

## 3. Which Consumer Goals Should Be Explicitly Avoided as Core Product Drivers
### Low-Urgency / "Nice-to-Have" Goals (Reasoned Inference)
*   **Basic Visualization / Dashboards:** Users often *say* they want "better visibility," but rarely pay for a standalone dashboard if the platform's native reporting is "good enough." This is a classic "False Positive."
*   **Minor UI Customization:** "Dark Mode" macros or custom button styles. While users appreciate them, the willingness to pay is near zero, and the platform will eventually add them natively.

### Highly Price-Sensitive / Easily Substituted Goals (Directly Evidenced)
*   **Generic Automation Macros:** If a task can be automated with a simple, free script or a generic tool like Zapier, a specialized ecosystem app will struggle to justify its cost.
*   **Standard Compliance Templates:** Unless the app provides ongoing *monitoring*, providing a one-time "Compliance Checklist" is a low-value goal with no recurring revenue potential.

### Support-Heavy / Low-Retention Goals (Directly Evidenced)
*   **Custom Niche Integrations for SMBs:** Building deep, custom integrations for small merchants with unique legacy systems. The support burden and technical debt far outweigh the LTV of the customer.
*   **Remedial Data Cleanup:** "Fixing the mess." Once the data is clean, the app's job is done, and the user uninstalls. This is a one-time goal that cannot support a SaaS model.

## 4. Dangerous False Positives
*   **"The Gap the Platform Missed":** Founders often assume a missing feature is an opportunity. However, if the feature is a logical next step on the platform's roadmap (e.g., "Advanced Discount Logic" in Shopify), building it is a race against an unbeatable competitor.
*   **"High Review Count = High Opportunity":** Seeing a category with many reviews (like "Email Pop-ups") and thinking it's a safe bet. These categories are often "Red Oceans" where acquisition costs exceed LTV for new entrants.
*   **"The Utility Tool with High Installs":** A bulk-editing tool might have 10,000 installs, but if the average customer stay is 14 days, the business is a "leaky bucket" that will never scale profitably.

## 5. Strategic Implications for a Founder
*   **Build for the "Post-Crisis" State:** Don't just help a user *fix* a problem; help them *manage* the new reality. Transition from "Data Migration" to "Continuous Data Optimization."
*   **Follow the "Roadmap of Inevitability":** Avoid building features that are destined to be native. Instead, build for "Niche Workflow Complexity" that the parent platform will never address because it is too specific for their mass market.
*   **Prioritize "Sticky" Data over "Slick" UI:** A tool that stores historical performance data or audit logs is harder to delete than a tool that just changes how a page looks.

## 6. Risks, Counterarguments, and Uncertainty
*   **The "Niche Survival" Argument:** Some founders successfully build "Lifestyle Businesses" around low-value goals by keeping costs extremely low and leveraging high organic marketplace traffic.
*   **Pivot Potential:** A "one-off" tool can sometimes be a successful "lead magnet" for a more robust, recurring product.
*   **Uncertainty of AI Speed:** AI may make it easier for platforms to "Sherlock" complex third-party features even faster, increasing the danger of building in the "platform's path."
*   **Segment Variation:** A "vanity goal" for a high-end brand might be a "brand protection goal" that carries high willingness to pay, unlike for a low-end dropshipper.
*   **Recency of Platform Roadmaps:** Public roadmaps are not always reliable; platforms may delay native features for years, creating a "temporary" opportunity that lasts long enough to be profitable.

## 7. Final Recommendations
*   **Exclude "Remedial" Goals:** If the customer goal is "Make this bad thing go away once," do not build a SaaS around it.
*   **Avoid "Native-Adjacent" Features:** If you can imagine the platform's internal product team presenting your feature at their next annual conference, don't build it.
*   **Focus on "Workflow Depth":** Build for goals that are integrated into the user's daily or weekly business operations and that grow in value as they accumulate data.

### Internal Adversarial Review
1.  **Weakness:** The thesis might be too dismissive of "Utility Tools." Many successful companies (like WinZip or various Shopify image optimizers) started as simple utilities and scaled through volume.
2.  **Incompleteness:** The analysis doesn't fully account for "Platform Incentives." Sometimes platforms *want* developers to build certain features to test the market before they build it themselves.
3.  **Bias:** I may be biased toward "Complex SaaS" models, while simple "Micro-SaaS" tools can be highly profitable for solo founders with low overhead.
4.  **Optimism:** I assume a founder can accurately "predict" the platform's roadmap, but platforms are often unpredictable and may leave huge gaps for decades.
5.  **Evidence Limitation:** The 90% churn rate for "Crisis Fix" tools is a commonly cited industry figure (Source [1][5]) but varies wildly depending on the specific niche and app quality.

**Revision Note:** Section 6 was updated to acknowledge the potential of "Niche Survival" and the "Lead Magnet" strategy for one-off tools.
