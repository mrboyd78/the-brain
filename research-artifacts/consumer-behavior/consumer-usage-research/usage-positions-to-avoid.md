# Deep Research: Which Usage Positions Should a Founder Explicitly Avoid Occupying?

## 1. Executive Thesis
In third-party app ecosystems (Shopify, Atlassian, HubSpot), a "Usage Position" is the specific cadence and intensity of interaction a founder designs for their product. Some positions are fundamentally **fragile or dangerous** because they create "Usage Friction" that leads to rapid churn. A founder should explicitly avoid positions that are "Too Frequent without Proportional Value" (Fatiguing), "Too Shallow for Recurrence" (Disposable), or "Too Intensive for the Segment" (Overwhelming). Occupying these positions leads to high "Reactance-Based Churn" and makes the business vulnerable to platform competition or "Shelfware" audits.

## 2. What the Evidence Shows
Public marketplace dynamics and regulatory studies (2025-2026) highlight several "Toxic Usage Positions":
- **The "Fatiguing Frequency" Position**: Apps that demand daily interaction (notifications, manual updates) for a task that is naturally weekly or monthly. This leads to "App Fatigue" and silent uninstalls.
- **The "Disposable Utility" Position**: Apps that solve a one-time problem (e.g., a single data migration) but have no ongoing value density. This results in high "Churn-by-Design."
- **The "Data Island" Position**: Using proprietary data formats or lacking a clear export path. This is increasingly rejected by enterprise IT managers as "Security Debt."
- **The "Over-Engagement Theater" Position**: Forcing users into the UI to "see" value that should have been automated. This creates resentment among busy professionals (e.g., Shopify merchants).

## 3. Usage Positions to Explicitly Avoid

### 1. Too Frequent Without Enough Value Density
- **The Position**: Forcing daily logins for a "Status Check" that rarely changes.
- **The Risk**: The user quickly realizes the "Cost of Attention" outweighs the "Benefit of Knowledge." They will eventually disable notifications and forget the app exists.

### 2. Too Shallow to Justify Recurrence
- **The Position**: A simple UI tweak or a "Single-Feature" tool that doesn't integrate into a broader workflow.
- **The Risk**: "Commodity Vulnerability." The host platform (Shopify/Atlassian) will eventually add the feature natively, wiping out the app’s entire value proposition.

### 3. Too Intensive for the Target Segment
- **The Position**: Requiring complex "Scripting" or "Regex" for a non-technical SMB user.
- **The Risk**: "Implementation Failure." The user installs the app with high hopes but never reaches the "Aha! Moment" because the "Intensity Floor" is too high.

### 4. Too Event-Dependent to Support Stable Value
- **The Position**: An app that only provides value during "Black Swan" events (e.g., a "Disaster Recovery" tool that doesn't do proactive monitoring).
- **The Risk**: "Out of Sight, Out of Mind." When the crisis isn't happening, the app is viewed as a "Waste of Budget" and is cut during the next audit.

### 5. Too Passive to Create Loyalty (The "Invisible" Trap)
- **The Position**: A background utility that provides zero feedback or reporting.
- **The Risk**: "Silent Churn." The user forgets they are paying for it and uninstalls it during a "Clean-up" phase because they don't see the impact.

## 4. Dangerous False Positives
Founders often mistake these for "Strong Engagement" when they are actually "Risk Traps":
- **High "Curiosity Clicks" on New Features**: Often just users exploring, not adopting. If these clicks don't lead to "Workflow Integration," they are vanity metrics.
- **Long "Session Length" due to Complexity**: As previously noted, this can be a sign of "UX Failure" rather than deep engagement.
- **"High Growth" during Platform Hype**: An install spike during a platform conference (e.g., Shopify Unite) doesn't correlate with long-term usage quality.

## 5. Strategic Implications for a Founder
- **Discipline in Design**: Rejecting "Engagement-Building" features that don't add "Value Density." It’s better to be used once a month and loved than used every day and resented.
- **Architectural Flexibility**: Build for "Low-Friction Entry" but "High-Depth Growth." Allow users to start shallow and move to intensive use at their own pace.
- **Pricing Strategy**: Use "Usage-Based" or "Outcome-Based" pricing for background/low-frequency apps to avoid the "Disposable Budget" cut.

## 6. Risks, Counterarguments, and Uncertainty
- **The "Short-Term Engagement" Penalty**: Avoiding "Theater" can lead to lower initial DAU numbers, which may be harder to explain to "Growth-Obsessed" investors.
- **Platform Homogenization**: As platforms standardize "Usage Badges" (e.g., "Daily Choice"), it becomes harder to be a "High-Value Occasional" app without being penalized by the algorithm.
- **The "AI Automation" Variable**: As AI handles more "Usage," the concept of a "Usage Position" will shift from "Human Interaction" to "Agentic Autonomy."

## 7. Final Recommendations
1. **Audit your "Attention Cost"**: If you are demanding more than 5 minutes of a user's day, you must deliver at least $100 of value (or equivalent) in return.
2. **Standardize your "Data Portability"**: Avoid the "Data Island" position at all costs. An "Easy Exit" is a high-confidence signal for enterprise buyers.
3. **Minimize "UI Frequency" for Admin tasks**: Be the tool that "Just Works" without being seen.
4. **Use "Proactive Reassurance" for Passive Apps**: Send a monthly "Impact Digest" to stay top-of-mind without being fatiguing.
5. **Monitor for "Resentment Keywords"**: Look for "annoying," "too many," "hard to use," or "why do I have to..." in your negative reviews.
