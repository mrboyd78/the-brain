# How Can Public Evidence Be Used to Infer a SaaS Postmortem?

## 1. Executive Thesis
Founders do not need leaked internal pitch decks, confidential financial audits, or private insider interviews to understand exactly why a competitor died. The digital rigor mortis of a failed SaaS startup is permanently etched into the public internet. A company's collapse is never a sudden event; it is a slow, agonizing chronological sequence of public desperation. By forensically auditing historical pricing page changes via the Wayback Machine, tracking the degradation of their API changelogs, observing the sudden shift in their executive LinkedIn hiring activity, and running sentiment analysis on the final 6 months of their App Store reviews, a founder can reconstruct a highly accurate, mathematically sound postmortem of exactly which unit-economic lever snapped and killed the company.

## 2. What the Evidence Shows
Analyzing the digital footprints of publicly failed SaaS companies reveals explicit causal chains that precede shutdown announcements by 12 to 18 months:
*   **The "Desperation Re-Brand" (Positioning Collapse):** A highly specialized tool (e.g., "The Best Cold Emailer for Realtors") suddenly redesigns their entire website to read "The AI Productivity Operating System for Everyone." This public pivot is desperate proof that their original Total Addressable Market (TAM) was far too small, and they are attempting to justify their venture valuation by blindly chasing horizontal scale. They usually die 8 months later.
*   **The "Enterprise Mirage" (The Pricing Hail Mary):** A self-serve, PLG startup permanently removes their $29/mo pricing tier from the website, replacing it exclusively with "Contact Sales." This public maneuver proves their churn rate on the low end was fatal; they are attempting to survive by forcefully dragging up-market. If they lack the compliance features (SOC2) required for Enterprise, this pivot fails instantly.
*   **The Ghost-Town Changelog:** The most definitive proof of imminent death is not what the company publishes, but what they stop publishing. When a company that previously shipped small, consistent weekly updates suddenly goes silent for 4 months, and then ships entirely unrelated cosmetic redesigns, it means the core engineering team has quit or been fired, and the remaining skeleton crew is "keeping the lights on."

## 3. Best Public Signals for Inferring a SaaS Postmortem
1.  **The Wayback Pricing Audit (The Mathematical Truth):** Track the pricing page over 24 months. If the entry price continually drops while the "Pro" tier continually rises, they are desperately trying to acquire leads while simultaneously attempting to extract cash from a tiny subset of locked-in power users.
2.  **Review-Pattern Velocity Shift:** A sudden, sustained drop from 4.8 stars to 2.1 stars focused almost entirely on the phrase "Support hasn't responded in weeks." This proves the company has laid off its Customer Success team to conserve runway.
3.  **The Integration Graveyard:** The company stops marketing native integrations and instead tells users to "Just use our Zapier link." This indicates they no longer have the engineering bandwidth to maintain brittle, direct API connections with host platforms.

## 4. Weak or Misleading Signals
*   **Lack of Press Coverage/PR:** Quiet companies are often the most profitable. A lack of TechCrunch articles means absolutely nothing regarding an incumbent's financial health or structural defensibility.
*   **Website Design Decay:** A website that looks like it was built in 2014 is not proof of failure; it is often proof of extreme, unassailable monopoly power. If a company hasn't updated its UI in 10 years but still holds the #1 spot in the App Store, their moat is terrifyingly deep.
*   **"We're joining forces!" Acquisition Announcements:** A press release stating a failed startup is "thrilled to join [Massive Corp]" is almost always an acqui-hire fire sale, not a strategic triumph. Read the terms closely; if they immediately shut down the core product post-acquisition, it was a failure, not an exit.

## 5. A Repeatable Public-Postmortem Inference Framework
1.  **The Chronological Reverse-Audit:** Start at the official "Shutdown/Acquisition" notice and work backward exactly 18 months.
2.  **Map the Pivots:** Identify the exact month the pricing model changed, the exact month the marketing H1 headline changed, and the exact month key executives removed the company from their active LinkedIn profiles.
3.  **Identify the Catalyst:** Correlate those pivot dates with external market events. Did a massive competitor launch a free clone 30 days before their pricing page panicked? Did the Host Platform change their API terms of service? The catalyst reveals the exact fragility that broke their business model.

## 6. Risks, Counterarguments, and Uncertainty
**Adversarial Review:**
1.  *The Competitor Misdirection:* Some incredibly smart companies intentionally leave "dead" pricing pages or neglected public channels specifically to make competitors think they are struggling in a certain segment, while actively dominating a totally different, unpublicized Enterprise segment via direct sales.
2.  *The 'Zombie SaaS' Reality:* A startup can exhibit every single signal of imminent death (terrible reviews, dead changelog, high prices) and STILL survive simply because B2B procurement is incredibly lazy and refuses to cancel their auto-renewing credit cards. The postmortem is theoretically correct, but the patient refuses to die.
3.  *Confirmation Bias:* If a founder *wants* to believe a competitor failed because of "bad design," they will selectively highlight UI complaints in the reviews and ignore the fact the competitor actually died because of an obscure technical database migration error.

## 7. Final Recommendations
A strategic founder must become a digital forensic investigator. Every failed competitor is a multi-million-dollar Harvard Business School case study handed to you for free, provided you know where to look. Stop reading founder interviews where they blame "market conditions" for their bankruptcy. Read their final 100 App Store reviews. Audit their final 3 permutations of their pricing tiers. The public exhaust of a dying software company provides an exact, high-fidelity map of the landmines hidden within a specific market category. Let your competitors spend venture capital discovering the failure modes; you only need to spend the time required to read their wreckage.
