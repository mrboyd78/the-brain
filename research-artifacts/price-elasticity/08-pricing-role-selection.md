# What Should Be Premium, Fairly Priced, Discounted, Bundled, or Left Alone? (Third-Party App Ecosystems)

## 1. Executive Thesis
In third-party app ecosystems (Shopify, Atlassian, HubSpot), a founder must differentiate between "Commodity Utilities" and "Core Workflow Infrastructure." Pricing roles should follow a hierarchy where **Security and Compliance are Premium**, **Core Functions are Fairly Priced**, **Introductory Onboarding is Discounted**, and **Adjacent Features are Bundled**. The most critical strategic decision is to leave "Infrastructure" components alone (no discounts), as price reductions here signal fragility and destroy the trust required for enterprise-grade adoption.

## 2. What the Evidence Shows
*   **Directly Evidenced:** Review analysis of the Shopify App Store shows that apps with "bundled" multi-feature sets (e.g., Vitals) have higher LTV and better review sentiment than collections of individual low-priced apps.
*   **Premium Evidence:** Atlassian marketplace vendors (e.g., Tempo, Adaptavist) successfully command a 5-10x premium for "Enterprise" versions that include audit logs and granular permissions, which are "must-haves" for large orgs.
*   **Market Underestimate:** Many founders underestimate the power of "Bundling for Retention." Bundling an "Analytics" tool with a "Workflow" tool reduces the likelihood of the user "shopping around" for specialized alternatives.
*   **Strategic Implications:** Price roles must align with the "Platform Lifecycle." As a merchant or user grows, they move from seeking "Fairly Priced Utilities" to needing "Premium Infrastructure."

## 3. What Should Be Premium, Fairly Priced, Discounted, Bundled, or Left Alone

| Role | Item / Feature Category | Fit Condition | Strategy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Premium** | Compliance, SSO, Advanced API, White-labeling | Required by large orgs with high "cost of failure" | High-margin, low-elasticity. Never discount. |
| **Fairly Priced** | Core functional value (e.g., the primary tool feature) | Competitive but essential workflow component | Align with platform-tier anchors (e.g., $29/mo). |
| **Discounted** | Annual upfront, migration services, early-bird access | Used to lower "Switching Cost" friction | Time-bound and targeted at new users only. |
| **Bundled** | Reporting, Analytics, "Quality of Life" improvements | Features that are "Nice-to-have" but hard to price | Increases "Perceived Value" and retention. |
| **Left Alone** | Mission-critical data sync, backup, security | Features where a discount signals "Low Quality" | Fixed price, no promos, signal of stability. |

*   **Premium Protection:** Anything related to "Safety" or "Governance" should never be on sale.
*   **Bundling Logic:** Bundle to hide "Price Weakness" in secondary features. If a user won't pay $10 for an analytics module, give it to them for "Free" in the $50 Pro plan.

## 4. Weak Assumptions and Pricing-Architecture Mistakes
*   **Mistake 1: Discounting the Core Functional Feature:** If your app is "Bulk Editor," discounting the "Bulk Edit" feature makes users wait for a sale.
*   **Mistake 2: Over-bundling the Premium Tier:** If you put "SSO" and "Better Support" in the same tier, you might accidentally underprice the SSO for enterprise users.
*   **Mistake 3: Complexity Trap:** Trying to have a different price for every feature. Users in ecosystems prefer a simple "Small, Medium, Large" structure.
*   **Mistake 4: Missing the "Migration" Discount:** The biggest barrier to a new app is "Data Migration." Discounting the *service* of migration is better than discounting the *software*.

## 5. Strategic Implications for a Founder
*   **Identify the "No-Discount Zone":** Clearly define the core features that are "protected."
*   **Use Bundling as a Churn Hedge:** If a competitor releases a free version of one of your features, your "Bundle" makes that free version irrelevant because the user is already integrated.
*   **Tier for "The Next Level Up":** Ensure your "Premium" tier is actually worth the jump. It shouldn't just be "more of the same," but "a different kind of value" (e.g., Risk Mitigation).

## 6. Risks, Counterarguments, and Uncertainty
*   **Platform "Sherlocking":** If a platform (e.g., Shopify) releases a "Fairly Priced" native version of your core feature, your "Premium" strategy must pivot to a niche.
*   **Buyer Psychology:** Some segments (e.g., Shopify merchants) are "Promo-addicted" due to the platform's culture; ignoring discounts entirely might hurt early-stage growth.
*   **The "All-in-One" Threat:** Large vendors who bundle *everything* at a low price can disrupt even well-defined "Premium" positions.

## 7. Final Recommendations
1.  **Audit Feature ROI:** If a feature costs you more to support than it generates in revenue, bundle it or move it to Premium.
2.  **Protect the "Infrastructure" Brand:** Never use "Urgency-based" (countdown timer) discounting for mission-critical tools.
3.  **Tier by "Business Complexity":** Move features that solve "Large Team" problems (permissions, workflows) into the Premium tier.

### Internal Adversarial Review
1.  **Assumption of Tier Value:** It assumes users naturally want to "upgrade" to Premium; in many ecosystems, users "stick" to the lowest tier that works.
2.  **Execution Risk:** Bundling can lead to "feature bloat" if not managed carefully, making the app harder to use.
3.  **Market Lag:** What is "Premium" today (e.g., AI-assisted categorization) may be "Fairly Priced Core" in 6 months.
4.  **Platform Billing Constraints:** Some marketplaces (like Shopify) have rigid billing APIs that make complex bundling or custom pricing difficult to implement.
5.  **Competitor Response:** If the market leader starts discounting their "Infrastructure," the "Left Alone" strategy might lead to terminal churn.
