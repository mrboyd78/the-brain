# Deliverable 5: Recommended Structural Elements for High-Quality Deliberation

> **Survey**: Empirical Literature on Group Deliberation Structure and Decision Quality  
> **Topic**: Evidence-Backed Structural Recommendations with Effect-Size Grounding  
> **Date Compiled**: April 2026

---

## 5.1 Overview

This deliverable synthesizes findings from the four preceding reports into a set of actionable structural recommendations for high-quality deliberation. Each recommendation is grounded in the strongest available effect-size evidence from the empirical literature. The goal is not a normative checklist but a *ranked*, *evidence-calibrated* protocol that a deliberation designer — in jury, committee, clinical panel, or organizational decision-making contexts — can use to choose interventions by expected effect magnitude.

Recommendations are organized into **five phases** of deliberation design:

1. **Pre-deliberation composition and setup**
2. **Structural information protocols**
3. **Process architecture during deliberation**
4. **Role assignments and structured dissent**
5. **Decision aggregation and closure**

---

## 5.2 Phase 1: Pre-Deliberation Composition and Setup

### 5.2.1 Recommendation: Select for Cognitive Diversity, Not Only Demographic Diversity

**Evidence basis:** Stasser & Titus (1985); van Knippenberg & Schippers (2007); Page (2007); Bahrami et al. (2010)

**Mechanism:** The Diversity Prediction Theorem guarantees that predictive variance reduces collective error, but only cognitive diversity (heterogeneous mental models and information sets) directly maps onto predictive variance. Demographic diversity is a *proxy* for cognitive diversity that works best when group membership determines information access (e.g., racial composition in racially charged cases per Sommers, 2006).

**Structural action:** 
- In expert panels: select members across functional domains, not just seniority within one domain
- In juries: voir dire should not exclusively target demographic balance; consider including questions that surface worldview and reasoning style diversity
- In committees: rotate membership to prevent stable coalitions that reduce information novelty

**Expected effect size if implemented:** *r* ≈ .17–.35 improvement in team decision quality vs. homogeneous composition (van Knippenberg & Schippers, 2007), conditional on information elaboration structures being in place.

### 5.2.2 Recommendation: Size the Group at 7–12 for Judgment Tasks; 4–5 for Intellective Tasks

**Evidence basis:** Saks & Marti (1997) meta-analysis; Laughlin, Bonner, & Miner (2002)

**Evidence summary:**

| Task Type | Optimal Size | Effect vs. Alternatives |
|---|---|---|
| Criminal verdict (judgment) | 12 > 6 | *d* ≈ 0.30–0.45 on accuracy; OR ≈ 1.8–2.5 on hung-verdict rate |
| Intellective problem-solving | 4–5 | *d* ≈ 0.60 vs. 2–3 person groups |
| Complex forecasting | 5–9 | Empirical optimum in Good Judgment Project |

**Structural action:**
- Do not reduce jury size below 12 in high-stakes criminal proceedings (Saks & Marti, 1997 provides strong evidence against functional equivalence)
- For advisory committees: 7–9 members balances thoroughness with participation equality
- For forecasting panels: 5–8 individuals with structured independent estimation before discussion

### 5.2.3 Recommendation: Provide Information in a Structured Narrative Format Before Deliberation

**Evidence basis:** Pennington & Hastie (1988, 1992); Ellsworth (1989)

Story Model research demonstrates that jurors (and by extension, deliberators in analogous high-stakes settings) process evidence more accurately when it is presented in causal-narrative sequence rather than witness-by-witness or category-by-category. Pre-deliberation information briefings should:

- Present a chronological account of events
- Explicitly link evidence to decision criteria
- Provide both supporting and contradicting evidence for each possible conclusion

**Effect:** Ellsworth (1989) found ~15 percentage-point improvement in verdict accuracy in mock juries receiving narrative vs. atomistic evidence presentation — a large practical effect.

## 5.3 Phase 2: Structural Information Protocols

### 5.3.1 Recommendation: Collect Independent Individual Judgment Before Discussion ("Aggregate First")

**Evidence basis:** Sunstein & Hastie (2015); Hastie & Kameda (2005); Good Judgment Project; Anderson & Holt (1997)

**Mechanism:** This is the single most robust intervention against conformity cascades (OR ≈ 2.6 for cascade behavior in sequential disclosure settings, Anderson & Holt, 1997) and anchoring bias from first-speaker effects (~15–25% positional bias, Hastie & Kameda, 2005).

**Structural action:**
- Before any group discussion, have all members submit a written assessment of the key question(s) — anonymously if possible
- Aggregate and share the distribution of these pre-deliberation positions with the group simultaneously
- Designate this initial distribution as evidence, not as a target consensus

**Expected effect:** Reducing first-speaker anchoring by ~60–70% of its magnitude (Sunstein & Hastie, 2015); substantially reducing cascade risk.

**Implementation note:** In jury contexts, this could be implemented as a preliminary secret ballot before any verbal deliberation, with the foreperson presenting the distribution without attribution. This is already practiced informally in some jurisdictions.

### 5.3.2 Recommendation: Assign Information Roles to Elicit Unique Information

**Evidence basis:** Stasser & Titus (1985); Wittenbaum & Park (2001); van Knippenberg & Schippers (2007)

**Mechanism:** The hidden-profile bias (shared information discussed 3–4× more than unique information) is reduced by ~40% when group members have explicit, known domains of expertise.

**Structural action:**
- Formally assign each member responsibility for one category of evidence or one perspective
- Require each member to summarize their assigned domain's key points *before* general discussion begins
- Use round-robin reporting in the first phase of deliberation

**Expected effect:** 40% reduction in hidden-profile bias (Wittenbaum & Park, 2001 meta); improved suboptimal-decision rate from ~67% to approximately ~35–40% in hidden-profile conditions.

### 5.3.3 Recommendation: Delay the First Formal Poll Until After Evidence Discussion

**Evidence basis:** Hastie, Penrod, & Pennington (1983); Sunstein & Hastie (2015)

**Mechanism:** Evidence-driven deliberation (discussing evidence before polling) produces better verdict accuracy than verdict-driven deliberation (polling first), with deliberation-time effect *d* ≈ 0.55 and evidence-coverage effect *d* ≈ 0.45.

**Structural action:**
- Explicitly instruct the group: "We will discuss all evidence before conducting any vote"
- Establish a minimum evidence-discussion period (e.g., 30–60 minutes for high-stakes decisions before any vote)
- Foreperson should facilitate evidence discussion by going through defined evidence categories

**Expected effect:** Transition from verdict-driven to evidence-driven deliberation reduces hung-jury rate by ~10 percentage points and improves judge-agreement on verdict quality in mock juries (Hastie et al., 1983).

## 5.4 Phase 3: Process Architecture During Deliberation

### 5.4.1 Recommendation: Require Unanimous Decision Rules for High-Stakes Judgments

**Evidence basis:** Nemeth (1977); Saks & Marti (1997); Kalven & Zeisel (1966)

**Mechanism:** Unanimous decision rules increase minority influence (Nemeth, 1977: *d* ≈ 0.45–0.55 on deliberation thoroughness), increase time on evidence, and reduce premature convergence. The higher hung-jury rate under unanimity (OR ≈ 1.8–2.5 for 12-person unanimous vs. majority-rule juries) represents a feature — correctly unresolved cases — not a bug, in high-stakes contexts where false certainty is more costly than deadlock.

**Structural action:**
- In criminal verdicts: maintain unanimity requirements
- In advisory panels: do not proceed to recommendation unless consensus is achieved or a formal dissent record is created
- In organizational committees: use a "no objection" requirement rather than simple majority for high-stakes decisions

**Expected effect:** Unanimity requirement reduces probability of suppressed minority information by increasing incentive for minorities to articulate their position (Nemeth, 1977). Effect is strongest on deliberation thoroughness and minority voice.

### 5.4.2 Recommendation: Structure Turn-Taking; Neutralize Status Hierarchies

**Evidence basis:** Asch (1955); Hollenbeck et al. (1995); Bahrami et al. (2010)

**Mechanism:** Status hierarchies create implicit weighting schemes that may not align with actual competence on the specific decision at hand. Asch's conformity research shows that even in objective perceptual tasks, social pressure from high-status seeming confederates produced 37% compliance. Bahrami et al. (2010) found that expertise diversity only benefits the group when *metacognitive calibration is accurate* — members must know who is most reliable for which question.

**Structural action:**
- Use structured speaking rounds with explicit equal-participation rules for the first phase
- Solicit lowest-status members' views before highest-status members declare positions
- Consider anonymous pre-discussion polling (removes status cues)
- Specifically ask: "Before we hear from [leader/expert], let's hear from others first"

**Expected effect:** Reducing the conformity effect from 37% (Asch, unstructured) to approximately 5–10% (with even a single structural dissent enforcer). Effect on decision quality: *d* ≈ 0.40–0.60 improvement in independent evidence weighting.

### 5.4.3 Recommendation: Actively Elicit Unique Information Using Direct Questioning

**Evidence basis:** Stasser, G., Vaughan, S. I., & Stewart, D. D. (2000). "Pooling unshared information: The benefits of knowing how access to information is distributed among group members." *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, 82(1), 102–116.

Stasser et al. (2000) found that when members' information access is made explicit (who knows what), the hidden-profile bias is substantially reduced because other members *specifically ask* those with unique information to contribute it.

**Structural action:**
- At the start of deliberation, map which evidence each member has seen or analyzed
- Periodically ask: "Does anyone have information that hasn't been discussed yet?"
- Designate a "devil's advocate" or "information officer" role whose explicit function is to surface unshared information

## 5.5 Phase 4: Role Assignments and Structured Dissent

### 5.5.1 Recommendation: Institutionalize a Devil's Advocate Role

**Evidence basis:** Schwenk (1990) meta-analysis: *d* = 0.56 on decision quality; Cosier (1978): *d* ≈ 0.55

**Structural action:**
- Assign the DA role *before* deliberation, not after consensus is reached
- The DA should be rotated across meetings to prevent it becoming a "troublemaker" identity
- The DA's challenge should be time-boxed (e.g., 10–15 minutes of structured critique)
- Group members should be instructed to engage with DA arguments, not dismiss them

**Expected effect:** *d* ≈ 0.50–0.65 improvement on decision quality metrics (Schwenk, 1990 meta-analysis, 14 studies). This is the strongest single structural intervention with both lab and field support.

**Trade-off to manage:** DA and DI reduce process satisfaction (*d* ≈ −0.35–0.45 on satisfaction measures). This can be mitigated by: (a) using anonymous DA via computer-mediated systems (reduces satisfaction penalty to *d* ≈ −0.14, Schweiger & Valacich, 1994), or (b) framing the DA role as a service to the group's mission.

### 5.5.2 Recommendation: For High-Uncertainty Decisions, Use Dialectical Inquiry

**Evidence basis:** Schweiger et al. (1986): DI assumption quality *d* ≈ 0.75; Schwenk (1990) meta: *d* = 0.51

**When to use DI vs. DA:**
- **DA** is appropriate when there is a clear initial recommendation that needs vetting
- **DI** is appropriate when the decision space is genuinely uncertain, assumptions are fragile, and multiple viable approaches exist

**Structural action:**
1. Group develops Plan A with explicit underlying assumptions listed
2. A designated sub-team develops Plan B from opposing assumptions
3. Both plans are presented; 30-minute structured debate
4. Group develops synthesis using insights from both plans

**Expected effect:** DI produces consistently higher *assumption quality* than DA (*d* ≈ 0.75 vs. *d* ≈ 0.65, Schweiger et al., 1986), meaning the group better understands *when their recommendation will fail.* This is particularly important for strategic decisions under uncertainty.

### 5.5.3 Recommendation: For External Validation, Commission a Red Team

**Evidence basis:** Tetlock & Gardner (2015) Good Judgment Project; Pillay et al. (2016) multidisciplinary oncology boards

**Structural action:**
- Appoint an independent team that was *not* involved in the main deliberation to review the output
- Brief the red team with the main group's conclusions and assumptions (not just the conclusion)
- Require red team to identify: (a) the three most fragile assumptions, (b) the most likely failure scenario, (c) alternatives not considered
- Red team report is presented to the main group before final decision

**Expected effect:** Good Judgment Project teams using structured skeptical challenge: ~20–25% Brier score improvement (*d* ≈ 0.60–0.70). Multidisciplinary tumor boards: HR = 0.80–0.85 for patient survival.

## 5.6 Phase 5: Decision Aggregation and Closure

### 5.6.1 Recommendation: Use Structured Aggregation of Individual Estimates Before Final Discussion

**Evidence basis:** Delphi method literature; Bahrami et al. (2010); Page (2007) DPT

The Delphi method — structured iterative anonymous polling with feedback — consistently outperforms unstructured face-to-face group deliberation for estimation and forecasting tasks. Key mechanism: it prevents cascade, elicits genuine individual assessment, then allows individuals to update in response to revealed information rather than social pressure.

**Effect magnitude:** Delphi vs. face-to-face committee on estimation tasks: ~15–25% reduction in collective error in studies reviewed by **Rowe & Wright (1999)** (*International Journal of Forecasting*, 15, 353–375).

**Structural action for hybrid deliberation:**
1. Ask each member to state their final reasoning and conclusion in writing before the group vote
2. Share these written statements simultaneously
3. Conduct final discussion based on written statements
4. Call for final vote only after written statements have been reviewed

### 5.6.2 Recommendation: Document and Preserve Dissents Formally

**Evidence basis:** Nemeth & Goncalo (2011); minority influence literature (Moscovici, 1969–1985)

A dissent that is formally recorded as part of the decision record serves three functions:
1. Reduces self-censorship (minority members know their view will be preserved)
2. Creates an analytic audit trail for reviewing errors post hoc
3. Provides a basis for reconsideration if new evidence emerges

**Structural action:**
- Any member who objects to the final consensus has the right to file a written dissent that becomes part of the formal record
- Dissents should include: the member's alternative assessment, the evidence they relied on, and the assumptions they believe were incorrectly adopted by the majority

**Expected effect:** The presence of dissent-recording norms significantly reduces self-censorship (Turner & Pratkanis, 1998: *d* ≈ 0.55–0.75 on reduction of critical evaluation suppression). No direct effect size is available for the dissent-recording mechanism specifically, but its theoretical grounding in minority influence research is strong.

### 5.6.3 Recommendation: Calibrate the Threshold for "Sufficient Consensus"

**Evidence basis:** Kalven & Zeisel (1966); Nemeth (1977)

The Kalven–Zeisel finding that supermajorities (≥9 of 12) predict final verdict with 90–95% probability suggests that *supermajority consensus is a reliable signal of genuine agreement.* However, this predictability cuts both ways: it can also represent cascade to a suboptimal position.

**Structural action:**
- In high-stakes decisions, require not just a vote count but also an explicit statement of the **core assumptions** those voting for the majority position share
- If the majority is not able to articulate shared, independently arrived-at assumptions, treat the apparent consensus as fragile and return to evidence discussion
- In ambiguous cases (e.g., 7–9 of 12), explicitly do NOT treat the initial ballot as binding; reserve time for additional evidence deliberation before the final ballot

## 5.7 Cross-Cutting Recommendation: Combine Structure Sequentially

No single intervention produces the full quality advantage. The evidence supports a **layered architecture** that applies multiple complementary structures:

```
[Pre-Deliberation]
1. Cognitive-diversity composition
2. Narrative pre-briefing of evidence
3. Independent written judgment (anonymous)

[Opening Phase]
4. Round-robin information disclosure (assigned domains)
5. Evidence-discussion period with no polling

[Core Deliberation]
6. Status-neutralizing turn-taking
7. Devil's Advocate structured challenge
8. Explicit elicitation of unique information

[Synthesis Phase]
9. Dialectical Inquiry or Red Team for high-uncertainty elements
10. Second independent individual estimate (post-discussion)

[Closure]
11. Formal dissent documentation
12. Unanimity or supermajority threshold with assumption articulation
```

## 5.9 Where Evidence Is Strongest vs. Weakest

### Strongest Evidence Base
- First-ballot prediction of verdict (phi ~.75): established, replicated, archival
- Group size effects (Saks & Marti, 1997): formal meta-analysis, multiple study types
- Devil's Advocate quality effect (Schwenk, 1990): formal meta-analysis
- Conformity and cascade effects (Asch; Anderson & Holt): replicated experimental

### Moderate Evidence, Important Caveats
- Evidence-driven vs. verdict-driven style: primarily mock-jury lab data; field replication limited
- Dialectical Inquiry: strong in MBA lab settings; limited field replication in real organizations
- Diversity effects: highly moderated by process variables; demographic diversity effects are inconsistent

### Evidence Gaps
- Optimal Delphi-deliberation hybrid protocols: no head-to-head RCT in legal or clinical settings
- Long-run longitudinal effects of structural interventions in real deliberating bodies
- Digital/asynchronous deliberation vs. synchronous face-to-face: growing literature, few meta-analyses
- Cognitive diversity operationalization: no validated psychometric for deliberation-relevant cognitive style
