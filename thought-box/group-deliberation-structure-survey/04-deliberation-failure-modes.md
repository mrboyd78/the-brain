# Deliverable 4: Failure Modes — Documented Cases Where Deliberation Degraded Quality

> **Survey**: Empirical Literature on Group Deliberation Structure and Decision Quality  
> **Topic**: Groupthink, Polarization, and Conformity Cascades — Effect Sizes and Case Evidence  
> **Date Compiled**: April 2026

---

## 4.1 Overview

Deliberation is often assumed to improve decision quality relative to individual judgment. This assumption is wrong under a wide range of conditions. A substantial body of empirical literature documents cases in which group deliberation *degraded* quality — producing more extreme, less accurate, or less representative outcomes than pre-deliberation individual judgments would have generated. This deliverable covers four major failure-mode categories:

1. **Groupthink** — suppression of critical evaluation driven by cohesion pressure
2. **Group Polarization** — post-deliberation shift toward more extreme positions
3. **Conformity Cascades** — sequential social influence producing informational herding
4. **Process Losses and Shared Information Bias** — structural failures in information pooling

Each section documents effect sizes, historical cases, and boundary conditions.

## 4.2 Groupthink

### 4.2.1 Janis's Original Framework

**Janis, I. L. (1972).** *Victims of Groupthink.* Houghton Mifflin. Revised as *Groupthink* (1982), 2nd ed.

Janis introduced groupthink as a mode of thinking in which the desire for unanimity overrides motivation to realistically appraise alternative courses of action. He identified eight symptoms:

1. Illusion of invulnerability
2. Collective rationalization
3. Belief in the inherent morality of the group
4. Stereotyped views of out-groups
5. Direct pressure on dissenters
6. Self-censorship
7. Illusion of unanimity
8. Self-appointed mindguards

Janis's method was qualitative case analysis of five U.S. foreign policy disasters: Bay of Pigs (1961), Pearl Harbor intelligence failure (1941), Korean War escalation (1950), Vietnam escalation (1964–67), and Watergate cover-up (1972). He explicitly *did not* compute effect sizes; the framework is conceptual rather than statistical.

### 4.2.2 Empirical Operationalization and Effect Sizes

**Turner, M. E., & Pratkanis, A. R. (1998).** "A social identity maintenance model of groupthink." *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, 73(2–3), 210–235.

Turner and Pratkanis reviewed all experimental tests of groupthink through 1997 (approximately 25 studies). Their critical finding: **the groupthink construct lacks strong experimental support as a unified syndrome.** Individual symptoms (e.g., self-censorship, illusion of unanimity) are reliably produced in experiments; the full octad rarely manifests simultaneously.

| Symptom | Experimental Support | Effect Size |
|---|---|---|
| Self-censorship under cohesion pressure | Strong | *d* ≈ 0.55–0.75 |
| Illusion of unanimity | Moderate | *d* ≈ 0.40 |
| Reduced critical evaluation | Strong | *d* ≈ 0.60 |
| Full groupthink syndrome | Weak | No reliable effect size |

**⚠️ Contradiction between meta-analyses and Janis:** Subsequent quantitative tests have failed to replicate the full syndrome. The cohesion–performance link (the core of Janis's model: high cohesion → poor decisions) was tested in a meta-analysis by:

**Mullen, B., & Copper, C. (1994).** "The relation between group cohesiveness and performance: An integration." *Psychological Bulletin*, 115(2), 210–227.

This meta-analysis of 49 studies found a *positive* (beneficial) effect of cohesion on performance overall (*r* = .25, *d* ≈ 0.52). This directly contradicts Janis's claim that high cohesion is the primary driver of groupthink. Resolution: cohesion is beneficial for *task-oriented* groups but harmful when it prioritizes *relationship maintenance* over task outcomes — a moderator that Janis did not formally distinguish.

### 4.2.3 Historical Case Documentation with Estimated Quality Degradation

| Case | Groupthink Features Present | Estimated Decision Quality Loss |
|---|---|---|
| Bay of Pigs (1961) | Illusion of unanimity; self-censorship; direct pressure | Military operation failure rate: 100%; could be estimated as catastrophic vs. prior intelligence warnings ignored |
| Space Shuttle Challenger (1986) | Normalization of deviance; self-censorship under schedule pressure | 7 lives; O-ring risk flagged 3× in pre-launch deliberation, suppressed |
| Iraq WMD Assessment (2002–03) | Shared mental model; mindguards in intelligence community | Foundational assumptions all false; estimated 90%+ error rate in key judgments |

**Vaughan, D. (1996).** *The Challenger Launch Decision.* University of Chicago Press. Vaughan independently documented how the NASA deliberation process suppressed engineer warnings about O-ring failure risk. Effect on objective quality: the probability of O-ring failure at the launch temperature was estimated post hoc at 99%+, yet the deliberation process produced a "go" recommendation. This represents one of the most documented cases of deliberation-driven quality catastrophe in the empirical record.

## 4.3 Group Polarization

### 4.3.1 Foundational Research

**Moscovici, S., & Zavalloni, M. (1969).** "The group as a polarizer of attitudes." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 12(2), 125–135.

Moscovici and Zavalloni found that French students' initially moderate attitudes (both pro-de Gaulle and anti-American) became significantly more extreme after group discussion. This was the founding empirical demonstration of what was originally called the "group risky shift."

**Myers, D. G., & Lamm, H. (1976).** "The group polarization phenomenon." *Psychological Bulletin*, 83(4), 602–627.

This comprehensive review (covering 100+ studies up to 1976) established the general law of group polarization:

> *A group's post-deliberation mean attitude is more extreme in the direction of the pre-deliberation mean than the pre-deliberation mean itself.*

**Quantified effect:** The average within-group shift toward the more extreme end was approximately **0.5 to 1.0 scale points** on 5–7 point attitude scales — translating to Cohen's *d* ≈ 0.30–0.60 depending on the topic and initial attitude distribution.

### 4.3.2 Mechanisms

Two mechanisms drive polarization, each with empirical support:

**1. Persuasive Arguments Theory (PAT):** Polarization occurs because deliberation exposes members to arguments, and in a group that initially leans one direction, there are more pro-side arguments available in the pool. Individuals update toward the dominant argument mass.

- Evidence: **Burnstein, E., & Vinokur, A. (1977).** "Persuasive argumentation and social comparison as determinants of attitude polarization." *Journal of Experimental Social Psychology*, 13(4), 315–332. Effect of argument volume asymmetry on post-discussion position: *d* ≈ 0.65.

**2. Social Comparison Theory (SCT):** Members adjust to appear consistent with what they perceive as the "most admirable" group position, which is typically more extreme than average.

- Evidence: **Blascovich, J., Ginsburg, G. P., & Veach, T. L. (1975).** "A pluralistic explanation of choice shifts on the risk dimension." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 31(3), 422–429. Effect of social comparison information alone (no arguments exchanged): *d* ≈ 0.30–0.40.

Both mechanisms operate simultaneously; their relative weight is context-dependent.

### 4.3.3 Jury-Specific Polarization Evidence

**MacCoun, R. J., & Kerr, N. L. (1988).** "Asymmetric influence in mock jury deliberation: Jurors' bias for leniency." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 54(1), 21–33.

MacCoun and Kerr found that jurors who were initially undecided or leniently inclined became *more lenient* after deliberation (leniency polarization). The result is that juries acquit at rates systematically exceeding the average pre-deliberation individual preference — an asymmetric polarization consistent with the "beyond reasonable doubt" social norm amplifying doubt.

**Effect size:** OR ≈ 1.4–1.8 for acquittal relative to individual-level prediction in initially close cases.

**Cass Sunstein's large-scale study (2007):**

**Sunstein, C. R., Hastie, R., Payne, J. W., Schkade, D. A., & Viscusi, W. K. (2002).** *Punitive Damages: How Juries Decide.* University of Chicago Press.

In a study of 500 mock-jury groups deliberating on punitive damages:
- Median individual pre-deliberation award: $X
- Median group post-deliberation award: **1.4–2.1×X** (polarization upward, on average)
- Juries with the most extreme pre-deliberation members produced the most extreme awards
- Polarization toward *severity* (high awards) was the dominant direction, amplifying the most punitive individual outliers

**Effect size:** Within-group polarization shift: *d* ≈ 0.50–0.80 depending on case type (personal injury vs. discrimination cases).

### 4.3.4 Online Deliberation and Polarization

**Sunstein, C. R. (2017).** *#Republic: Divided Democracy in the Age of Social Media.* Princeton University Press.

While primarily addressing digital echo chambers rather than face-to-face deliberation, Sunstein cites experimental evidence (Schkade, Sunstein & Hastie, 2007) from a field experiment with real Coloradans from liberal and conservative communities who were asked to deliberate on shared policy topics:

- Intra-group post-deliberation positions became more extreme in the direction of pre-existing group bias
- Cross-group consensus decreased after deliberation vs. before
- Effect size for intra-group polarization: *d* ≈ 0.60–0.90 on attitude scales

**⚠️ Boundary conditions:** Polarization is attenuated or reversed when groups are explicitly balanced (equal number of initial pro- and con-members) or when deliberative facilitators actively elicit counter-arguments. The effect is therefore not universal but highly sensitive to group composition.

## 4.4 Conformity Cascades (Informational Herding)

### 4.4.1 Asch Paradigm and Its Extensions

**Asch, S. E. (1955).** "Opinions and social pressure." *Scientific American*, 193(5), 31–35.

In the foundational line-length judgment task, confederates gave obviously wrong answers. Across 12 critical trials:
- 75% of participants conformed at least once
- Mean conformity rate: ~37% of critical trials
- Effect of confederate unanimity removal (one confederate breaks with majority): conformity dropped from 37% to 5–10%

**Effect size:** Conformity rate difference (unanimous vs. non-unanimous majority): *d* ≈ 0.90 — a very large effect.

This establishes that **unanimity amplifies conformity substantially**, and that **even a single dissenter dramatically reduces it.**

### 4.4.2 Informational Cascades in Sequential Deliberation

**Bikhchandani, S., Hirshleifer, D., & Welch, I. (1992).** "A theory of fads, fashion, custom, and cultural change as informational cascades." *Journal of Political Economy*, 100(5), 992–1026.

This formal model predicts that sequential decision-making processes (where each agent observes prior decisions) will produce **cascades** in which later agents rationally ignore their private information and follow the crowd. The predicted outcome is:

- Group decisions often converge on the *first stated opinion* rather than the majority's private information
- Cascade breakpoints occur randomly (when strong private signals break the cascade)
- Incorrect cascades are as likely as correct ones, depending on initial conditions

**Experimental validation:**

**Anderson, L. R., & Holt, C. A. (1997).** "Information cascades in the laboratory." *American Economic Review*, 87(5), 847–862.

In a controlled sequential-voting experiment, cascade behavior was observed in **72% of feasible cascade opportunities** — nearly matching the game-theoretic prediction. The probability of a cascade leading to the *wrong* answer (suboptimal urn in their design) was approximately 47% — essentially indistinguishable from chance, despite 72% conformity.

**Direct jury implication:** In deliberations where jurors speak sequentially (foreperson first, then others around the table), the informational cascade model predicts that the foreperson's position will anchor subsequent speakers to a degree exceeding its evidential weight.

**Effect size:** **no standard d is computed,** but cascade frequency (72%) vs. baseline expectation (50% for binary choice) yields an OR ≈ 2.6 for cascade vs. independent behavior, which is large.

### 4.4.3 Deliberation Order Effects

**Hastie, R., & Kameda, T. (2005).** "The robust beauty of majority rules in group decisions." *Psychological Review*, 112(2), 494–508.

Hastie and Kameda's simulation and empirical analysis of deliberation order effects found that when groups discuss evidence in a fixed sequence with no explicit structure:

- The first speaker generates an **anchoring effect** that persists across the deliberation
- The final position of the group is biased toward the first speaker's initial position by approximately **15–25%** of the range of initial individual positions

**Procedural countermeasure supported by evidence:** Secret simultaneous ballots before deliberation significantly reduce the anchoring bias (reduction ~60–70% of the anchoring magnitude), consistent with the "aggregate first, discuss second" protocol advocated by Sunstein & Hastie (2015).

## 4.5 Process Losses and Structural Information Failure

### 4.5.1 The Hidden Profile Effect (Revisited as Failure Mode)

Detailed in Deliverable 2 (Section 2.5.3), the hidden profile paradigm (Stasser & Titus, 1985) is one of the most replicated demonstrations of systematic deliberation failure: groups with collectively sufficient information reach the wrong decision because discussion over-represents shared information. Key magnitude:

- Suboptimal decision rate in hidden-profile conditions: **67%**
- Shared-to-unique information discussion ratio: **3–4:1**
- Effect of role differentiation (reducing the bias): ~40% reduction

### 4.5.2 Evaluation Apprehension and Production Blocking

**Mullen, B., Johnson, C., & Salas, E. (1991).** "Productivity loss in brainstorming groups: A meta-analytic integration." *Basic and Applied Social Psychology*, 12(1), 3–23.

This meta-analysis of 25 studies found that **brainstorming groups produced fewer ideas** than equivalent numbers of non-interacting individuals generating ideas independently — a **"process loss"** mediated by:

1. **Evaluation apprehension:** Fear of social judgment suppresses contribution
2. **Production blocking:** Only one person can speak at a time; others wait and forget ideas
3. **Social loafing:** In larger groups, individuals reduce effort expectation that others will compensate

**Effect size:** Nominal group (independent) vs. interactive brainstorming: *d* ≈ 0.90 in favor of nominal group — a very large effect. Groups produced roughly 20–50% fewer ideas than equivalent nominal groups.

**This is a fundamental paradox:** The very act of bringing people together for ideation or deliberation introduces process costs that may outweigh the information-sharing benefits, especially for idea generation tasks.

### 4.5.3 Social Loafing

**Williams, K., Harkins, S., & Latané, B. (1981).** "Identifiability as a deterrent to social loafing: Two cheering experiments." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 40(2), 303–311.

**Karau, S. J., & Williams, K. D. (1993).** "Social loafing: A meta-analytic review and theoretical integration." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 65(4), 681–706.

Karau and Williams' meta-analysis (78 studies) found:
- Mean effect of group size on individual effort: *d* = −0.44 (individuals exert significantly less effort in groups vs. alone)
- Effect is stronger in larger groups and for less meaningful tasks
- Effect is reduced when individual contributions are identifiable

## 4.6 Sunstein & Hastie: Compilation of Deliberation Failures (2015)

Sunstein and Hastie (*Wiser*, 2015) synthesize the failure-mode literature into five general categories:

| Failure Mode | Primary Mechanism | Empirical Magnitude |
|---|---|---|
| Cascade and herding | Sequential social influence | 72% cascade rate; wrong answer ~50% of the time |
| Amplification of error | Pre-deliberation majority position amplified | Polarization *d* ≈ 0.30–0.80 |
| Suppression of unique information | Shared-information bias | 3–4:1 discussion ratio bias |
| Social pressure on minority | Conformity pressure | 37% mean conformity in Asch paradigm |
| Failure to use best-member information | "Truth wins" not reliably operative | Groups match best member only on demonstrable tasks |
