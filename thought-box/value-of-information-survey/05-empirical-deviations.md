# Deliverable 5: Empirical Deviations — Behavioral Realities of VoI

> **Survey**: Formal Theory of Value of Information  
> **Topic**: Documented patterns where human decision-makers deviate from VoI-optimal effort  
> **Date Compiled**: April 2026

---

## 5.1 The Deviation Taxonomy

While VoI provides a normative benchmark for "rational" information seeking, empirical research in psychology and behavioral economics has identified systematic deviations. These are generally categorized into **Over-investment** (seeking too much information), **Under-investment** (deciding too quickly), and **Mis-allocation** (wrong priority).

---

## 5.2 Over-Investment: Analysis Paralysis and the "Value of Certainty"

### 5.2.1 Information Bias (Baron et al., 1988)
Individuals often seek information that *cannot* change their decision. In clinical experiments, physicians frequently order tests that, regardless of the result, will not alter the recommended treatment.
- **Root Cause**: A confusion between "information that reduces uncertainty" and "information that changes action."
- **Psychological Driver**: Anticipated Regret. Decision-makers seek "defensive" information to justify their choice ex-post, even if it adds zero ex-ante utility.

### 5.2.2 The Paradox of Choice (Schwartz, 2004)
Traditional VoI suggests that more options or more data can only increase expected utility (or leave it unchanged). However, empirical studies show that beyond a certain complexity threshold, additional information leads to:
- **Cognitive Overload**: Degradation of processing quality.
- **Decision Deferred**: Total paralysis resulting in the "Status Quo" choice (effectively an under-investment in the alternative action).

### 5.2.3 Illusion of Knowledge (Hall et al., 2007)
As people gather more information, their **confidence** increases at a much faster rate than their **accuracy**. 
- **Effect**: Decision-makers may pay for information that makes them feel more certain without actually reducing the true probability of error. This is a "utility-negative" investment where $C > \text{EVSI}$.

## 5.3 Under-Investment: Premature Closure and Heuristics

### 5.3.1 Satisficing (Simon, 1956)
Bounded rationality posits that humans do not maximize, but "satisfice"—stopping at the first option that meets a minimum threshold.
- **Deviation**: This leads to under-investment in search for higher-stakes decisions where the "optimal" threshold is far above the "satisfactory" one.

### 5.3.2 Need for Closure (Kruglanski, 2004)
In high-stress or time-pressured environments, decision-makers experience an aversive psychological state toward uncertainty.
- **Effect**: "Seizing and Freezing." They seize the first available (often biased) information and freeze their opinion, failing to process subsequent high-value information.

### 5.3.3 Availability and Salience
Search effort is often dictated by the ease with which information can be retrieved, rather than its diagnostic value. Decision-makers "under-invest" in hard-to-reach but high-EVSI data sources.

---

## 5.4 Mis-Allocation: Stakes-Insensitivity

Rationality requires that effort scales with stakes. Humans often exhibit the opposite or a flattened response:

### 5.4.1 The "Law of Triviality" (Parkinson's Law of Beige)
Decision-makers often spend disproportionate time on low-stakes, easily understood decisions (e.g., the color of the office carpet) while under-deliberating on high-stakes, complex ones (e.g., the strategic pivot).
- **Reason**: The marginal cost of cognitive effort is lower for simple problems, leading to a "feeling of progress" that is decoupled from actual value.

### 5.4.2 Denominator Neglect and Probability Blindness
Individuals often struggle to distinguish between a 1% risk and a 0.01% risk, treating them both as "low."
- **VoI Impact**: This leads to massive mis-allocation of research effort, where people over-invest in analyzing rare, salient risks and under-invest in common, high-impact ones.

---

## 5.5 Systematic Failure of EVPI Sensitivity

Normative VoI (see Deliverable 2) is maximized when the prior is near the indifference threshold. Empirical observations (Tversky & Edwards, 1966) show:
- **Confirmation Bias**: People over-invest in seeking information when they are *already certain* (seeking validation), and under-invest when they are at the indifference point (avoiding the conflict of choice).
- **This is a direct inversion of the VoI curve**, leading to effort being spent exactly where it has the lowest marginal utility.
