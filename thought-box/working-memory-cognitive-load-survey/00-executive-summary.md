# Working Memory & Cognitive Load: Empirical Limits on Multi-Proposition Reasoning
## Executive Summary

**Survey Date:** April 2026  
**Topic:** Empirical limits of human working memory and cognitive load when reasoning about multiple interacting propositions simultaneously  
**Scope Period:** 1956–2026

---

## The Central Empirical Answer in Brief

The popular claim that "humans can hold 7±2 items in working memory" (Miller, 1956) is an historical artifact that has been substantially revised by subsequent research. The current empirical consensus establishes a tighter and operationally more meaningful set of limits:

| Claim Type | Estimate | Strongest Supporting Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Raw item capacity (chunks, no strategy)** | 3–4 items | Cowan (2001); Luck & Vogel (1997); change detection paradigms |
| **Interacting-proposition ceiling (relational complexity)** | 3–4 variables (quaternary) | Halford et al. (1998, 2005); Birney et al. (2006) |
| **Functional ceiling before quality degradation** | 2–3 mutually-interacting claims | Applied domain convergence (medicine, intelligence, law) |
| **Expert ceiling with domain schemata** | Up to 4 interacting elements | Expertise/chunking studies; novice–expert comparisons |

**The core distinction this survey establishes:** counting *independent* facts is a fundamentally different cognitive operation from reasoning about *interacting* propositions. Miller's "7±2" was about independent items; the relational-complexity literature shows that the interacting-claim ceiling is substantially lower — approximately 2–4 depending on schema availability and task demands.

## Where Popular Psychology Diverges from Empirical Consensus

The following popular-psychology claims are directly contradicted or substantially revised by the empirical literature:

| Popular Claim | Actual Empirical Finding | Key Sources |
|---|---|---|
| "Humans can hold 7±2 items in working memory" | Capacity is 3–4 *chunks* when rehearsal/grouping is prevented; 7 items was an artifact of chunking strategies | Cowan 2001; Broadbent 1975 reanalyses |
| "7 is the limit for designing menus and lists" | Design-relevant limit for simultaneously *interacting* items is 2–4; the 7 figure applies to sequentially-scanned independent items | Sweller & Paas 2010; Miller's own 1989 retraction |
| "Experts can hold much more" | Experts chunk information differently, compressing N interacting variables to 1 schema unit; they don't exceed the 3–4 slot limit, they use slots more efficiently | Chase & Simon 1973; Ericsson & Kintsch 1995 |
| "Practice expands working memory" | Practice expands long-term memory schemas, not the raw slot count of WM; the bottleneck itself does not grow | Baddeley 2000; Conway et al. 2005 |
| "Intelligence = large WM" | WM capacity and fluid intelligence correlate (r ≈ 0.5–0.7) but are not equivalent; high-WM individuals still hit relational-complexity ceilings at 4 variables | Halford et al. 2005; Kane & Engle 2002 |

---

## Key Convergence Across Three Research Traditions

The empirical convergence from three independent research traditions is striking:

1. **Attention/capacity theory** (Cowan 2001): Focus of attention = 3–4 chunks
2. **Relational complexity theory** (Halford 1998, 2005): Parallel processing limit = 4 variables (quaternary ceiling)
3. **Cognitive load theory** (Sweller 1988+): High element interactivity overwhelms WM; tasks requiring simultaneous integration of 5+ novel elements produce failure

All three converge on the range **3–4** as the functional ceiling for simultaneously *interacting* cognitive elements under conditions of full engagement by a trained reasoner.

---

## Practical Implication

Any reasoning task that requires simultaneously tracking and integrating **more than 4 mutually-interacting propositions without external support** is operating at or beyond the empirical limit of human cognitive architecture. Tasks with 5+ interacting propositions, when given to unaided subjects, produce performance at or near chance on the interaction effects themselves — even when subjects correctly recall the individual propositions.
