# Deliverable 5: Transfer and Durability of Debiasing Gains

> **Survey**: Cognitive Biases and Debiasing Interventions  
> **Focus**: Cross-domain transfer, temporal persistence, and conditions for durability  
> **Date Compiled**: April 2026

---

## 5.1 The Transfer Problem: Why It Matters

A debiasing intervention that reduces anchoring on a salary negotiation task is only useful if it also (a) reduces anchoring on a medical dosage decision the following week, (b) is still effective 6 months later, and (c) does not require the practitioner to consciously identify each new situation as an "anchoring risk." Without transfer and durability, debiasing gains are laboratory curiosities, not practical tools.

The transfer and durability literature presents a largely discouraging picture for cognitive debiasing but a more optimistic one for structural and procedural interventions. This deliverable documents both bodies of evidence systematically.

---

## 5.2 Definitions

**Near transfer**: Applying a debiasing skill to a task very similar to the trained task (same bias, same domain, slightly different surface features).

**Far transfer**: Applying a debiasing skill to a substantially different task (same bias, different domain; or different bias type; or different decision context).

**Temporal durability**: How long the debiasing effect persists after training without reinforcement or booster sessions.

**Ecological transfer**: Whether laboratory-demonstrated debiasing produces improvements in real-world naturalistic decisions.

---

## 5.3 Cognitive Debiasing: Transfer Evidence

### 5.3.1 Near Transfer — Generally Successful Under Specific Conditions

**Morewedge et al. (2015)**: The interactive debiasing game showed transfer to novel tasks not in the training set — this is near transfer (same bias types, different problem scenarios). The 2-month follow-up maintained effects, suggesting successful near transfer within bias categories trained.

**Larrick, Morgan & Nisbett (1990)** (*Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, 45, 331–347): Training in statistical reasoning rules (law of large numbers) showed significant near transfer to structurally similar problems involving regression to the mean. *d* ≈ 0.55 for near-transfer tasks.

**Critical condition for near transfer**: The trainee must recognize that a new situation is structurally similar to the trained case. When surface features differ substantially, near-transfer failure increases.

### 5.3.2 Far Transfer — Consistently Poor

**Larrick, Morgan & Nisbett (1990)**: The same statistical training that produced near transfer showed near-zero transfer to problems with different surface content (e.g., medical risk problems when training was on sports examples). Far-transfer effect: *d* ≈ 0.10 (ns in most conditions).

**Fischhoff (1982)**: Reviewing 30 years of debiasing research: "The overall picture is: debiasing effects are typically small, often limited to the biases directly targeted, and rarely transfer to other biases or novel decision domains."

**Nisbett, Krantz, Jepson & Kunda (1983)** (*Psychological Review*, 90(4), 339–363): Training on statistical reasoning rules showed transfer only when: (a) the rule was taught abstractly (not just by example), (b) subjects were shown multiple examples across different domains during training, (c) the far-transfer task made the statistical structure salient. Without all three conditions, far transfer was negligible.

**Meta-analytic evidence**: Lilienfeld, Ammirati & Landfield (2009, *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 4, 390–398): Reviewed the debiasing literature comprehensively. Transfer effects: "The evidence for generalization of debiasing effects across bias types is consistently weak. Most studies have shown that successful debiasing of one bias type does not generalize to other bias types."

### 5.3.3 Domain Transfer: Professional to Personal Decisions and Vice Versa

Training on bias in professional contexts (medical diagnosis, financial analysis) does not reliably transfer to personal decisions (health risk choices, personal investment decisions), despite both domains involving the same cognitive mechanisms.

**Evidence**: Shanteau (1992, *Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes*, 53(2), 252–266): Expert decision-making competence in professional domains (weather forecasting, livestock judging) showed minimal correlation with general decision-making quality in personal domains. Domain expertise does not produce domain-general debiasing.

**Reason**: Domain expertise creates rich schematic representations that facilitate rapid pattern recognition (reducing certain biases in domain) but does not modify the underlying System 1 architecture. The same expert who is well-calibrated in their specialty can be severely biased in adjacent domains.

## 5.4 Cognitive Debiasing: Temporal Durability Evidence

### 5.4.1 The Short-Term / Long-Term Gap

The pattern across studies is consistent: debiasing effects, when present, decay substantially over time without reinforcement.

**Morewedge et al. (2015)**: 2-month follow-up showed partial retention (interactive game: 24% vs. 32% immediately; video: 19% vs. 19% — interestingly more stable for video). No 12-month data.

**Sedlmeier & Gigerenzer (2001)** (*Psychological Review*, 108, 730–750): Bayesian reasoning training via frequency formats showed durability at 5 weeks: *d* ≈ 0.90 (comparable to immediate effect *d* ≈ 0.95). This is unusual — most other debiasing training shows steeper decay at 5 weeks.

**Larrick et al. (1990)**: Statistical rule training showed modest retention at 2 months but was not tested beyond that.

**Cognitive flexibility training** (Blackwell et al., 2007, *Child Development*, 78(1), 246–263): Growth mindset interventions (a debiasing of fixed-ability thinking) showed 1-year durability in early adolescent samples. This is among the longest longitudinal tests of cognitive retraining effects.

**What the durability literature shows**: 

| Study | Intervention | Immediate *d* | 2-Month *d* | 12-Month *d* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Morewedge et al. (2015) | Interactive game | ~0.90 | ~0.70 | Unknown |
| Morewedge et al. (2015) | Passive video | ~0.55 | ~0.56 | Unknown |
| Sedlmeier & Gigerenzer (2001) | Frequency training | ~0.95 | ~0.90 (5 wk) | Unknown |
| Larrick et al. (1990) | Statistical rules | ~0.55 | ~0.30 | Unknown |
| Blackwell et al. (2007) | Growth mindset | ~0.45 | Not tested | ~0.35 |
| Typical awareness training | Lecture/slides | ~0.05 | ~0.00 | ~0.00 |

**Critical gap**: No published study has followed up debiasing training effects beyond 12 months in professional settings with objective outcome measures. The standard training-persistence literature in psychology (for skill learning) suggests most non-reinforced gains decay to baseline within 1–2 years for complex reasoning tasks.

## 5.5 Structural Debiasing: Transfer and Durability

### 5.5.1 Transfer Properties of Structural Interventions

Structural interventions do not "transfer" in the cognitive sense — they are applied to specific decision contexts by design. However, some structural changes have broad-scope applicability:

**Information format redesign** (frequency format): Once a medical system adopts frequency-format risk communication standards, the debiasing applies to every relevant decision point without individual effort. Transfer is achieved institutionally rather than cognitively.

**Reference class forecasting**: Once trained AND institutionally mandated (as in Denmark/UK), applies to every project forecast in the applicable class. Transfer is procedural.

**Defaults**: Apply to every individual encountering the choice system. Zero individual cognitive effort required; complete "transfer" to every instance.

### 5.5.2 Durability Properties of Structural Interventions

Structural interventions are durable precisely because they do not rely on individual memory or motivation:

- Opt-out organ donation default: Consent rates in France have remained 87–92% for 15+ years since adoption (Agence de la biomédecine, 2020)
- WHO Surgical Safety Checklist: 73% of participating hospitals maintained protocol compliance at 5-year follow-up (Gillies et al., 2014, *BMJ Quality and Safety*)
- 401(k) auto-enrollment: Participation rates maintained as long as the policy remains in force; have not reverted in 20-year post-Madrian & Shea follow-up (Choi, Laibson & Madrian, 2009)

**Decay condition**: Structural interventions decay only when the institution changes (policy reversal, personnel changes in the procedure owner, software updates that remove the format change). These are institutional rather than psychological decay mechanisms.

## 5.6 The Role of Reinforcement and Feedback in Durability

The strongest durability evidence comes from programs with continuous feedback loops:

**Good Judgment Project** (Tetlock et al.): Forecasters who continued to participate and receive Brier score feedback maintained calibration improvements over 3+ years of active participation. Performance *improved* over time (not decayed) when feedback was continuous. This is the closest analog to a "debiasing with reinforcement" paradigm.

**Flight simulators and aviation**: Pilots who undergo regular recurrent training (every 6–12 months in simulators with specific failure scenario feedback) maintain significantly higher performance on subsequent real-event emergencies. This is the institutional model for sustaining expert performance against complacency and bias drift. No direct cognitive bias study has replicated this, but the principle is analogous.

**Key principle**: **Feedback that directly connects the debiasing practice to objective performance outcomes** is the most powerful durability mechanism. Without feedback, debiasing gains fade. With continuous calibrated feedback (GJP model), gains compound.

## 5.7 Individual Differences in Transfer and Durability

Not all decision-makers benefit equally from debiasing training. Several moderating factors have been identified:

### 5.7.1 Actively Open-Minded Thinking (AOT)

**Baron (1988)** (*Thinking and Deciding*) formalized actively open-minded thinking as a trait: seeking evidence against one's beliefs, considering multiple possibilities, revising beliefs in response to evidence.

**Haran, Ritov & Mellers (2013)** (*Thinking & Reasoning*, 19(1), 26–43): AOT predicted forecasting accuracy and was the strongest individual-difference predictor of benefiting from GJP-style training. High-AOT individuals showed 25% better calibration improvement response to training vs. low-AOT.

**Implication**: Debiasing training is most effective for those who are already somewhat open-minded. Training those with low AOT requires first building metacognitive motivation — a much harder task.

### 5.7.2 Cognitive Reflection Test (CRT)

**Frederick (2005)** (*Journal of Economic Perspectives*, 19(4), 25–42): CRT measures the tendency to engage deliberate thinking rather than accepting the first intuitive response.

**Liberali, Reyna, Furlan, Stein & Pardo (2012)** (*Journal of Behavioral Decision Making*, 25(5), 521–533): High-CRT individuals showed significantly better transfer of statistical reasoning training to new domains. Low-CRT individuals showed near-zero far transfer. *d* ≈ 0.60 for high vs. low CRT on transfer tasks.

**Critical implication**: For far-domain transfer, cognitive ability moderates the effect substantially. Population-level debiasing programs will have heterogeneous effects based on CRT distribution of the target population.

### 5.7.3 Domain Expertise

High domain expertise is a double-edged moderator:
- **Within-domain**: Reduces some biases (availability is more calibrated; overconfidence on routine tasks decreases with experience — Shanteau, 1992)
- **Cross-domain**: Domain expertise can *increase* certain biases (overconfidence in unfamiliar domains; representativeness based on expert schema; status quo bias driven by expertise)

**Professionals with 20+ years in a domain** are often *harder* to debias within that domain because their pre-existing representations are strongly entrenched and resistant to feedback that challenges them (Ericsson, 2004, *Psychological Review*).

## 5.8 Ecological Transfer: From Laboratory to Field

The largest unresolved question in the debiasing literature is ecological transfer: whether training that reduces bias in a controlled task (student judging hypothetical cases) produces measurable reductions in real-world decision error in field conditions.

**The evidence is largely absent**. Most debiasing studies measure:
- Performance on the *same or structurally similar task* immediately after training
- Stated confidence levels on quiz-style problems

Almost none measure:
- Real clinical decisions made by trained vs. untrained physicians
- Real investment returns comparing trained vs. untrained portfolio managers
- Real intelligence assessment accuracy before/after institutional ACH adoption

**Key exception — RCF field evidence**: Flyvbjerg's reference class forecasting adoption by Danish and UK governments provides the closest thing to field ecological transfer evidence: the institution adopted the procedure, and project-level cost accuracy improved measurably over years. This is the gold standard that other debiasing interventions have not yet been subjected to.

**Key exception — GJP tournament evidence**: The GJP measures real geopolitical prediction accuracy with objective ground truth. This makes it the strongest ecological validation for any cognitive debiasing program.

## 5.9 Recommendations for Practitioners

Based on the transfer and durability evidence:

### For Organizations Designing Debiasing Programs:

1. **Do not invest in one-time awareness training without feedback mechanisms** — gains will not persist or transfer.

2. **Design for structural persistence**: Implement format changes (frequency-framed risk communications), mandatory procedures (reference class forecasting for project approval), and structured protocols (premortem, red-team reviews) that apply automatically at each relevant decision point.

3. **Use feedback loops**: If cognitive training is used, pair it with ongoing objective feedback on decision quality. GJP-style calibration scoring, post-decision audits, and prediction-resolution tracking are viable mechanisms.

4. **Assess individual AOT and CRT**: Training investments produce larger returns for high-AOT/high-CRT individuals. Screen or pre-select if resources are limited.

5. **Provide boosters**: Even well-designed cognitive training shows decay. Plan for 6-month refreshers with new problem sets (not re-exposure to the same training material — the learning benefit of re-exposure is negligible).

6. **Measure real outcomes**: Define upfront what a "better decision" looks like in concrete, observable terms. Track forecast accuracy, project cost deviation, and diagnostic accuracy — not knowledge quiz scores or self-reported confidence in the debiasing technique.

### For Individuals:

1. Build and sustain a **calibration tracking** practice: Keep a record of predictions with explicit probability estimates; review resolutions regularly. This is the personal analog of the GJP feedback loop.

2. Learn **reference class identification** as a procedural habit: Before any plan or forecast, explicitly ask "what is the base rate for this type of outcome?"

3. Practice **prospective hindsight** (premortem) before any significant commitment.

4. Seek genuine **dissenting views** from people with different vantage points — not to be polite but as an institutional commitment to your own decision process.
