## 1.2 Per-Framework Band Detail

### 1.2.1 Legal Standards of Proof (U.S. Law)

The three primary civil and criminal standards, with their constitutional grounding:

| Standard | Constitutional Grounding | Verbal Definition | Elicited Probability Range | Primary Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Preponderance of the evidence** | 5th/14th Amendment due process (civil context) | "More likely than not"; "greater weight of the evidence" | >50% (conventional); juror studies: 54–74% (see §1.3.1) | Civil litigation, tort, contract |
| **Clear and convincing evidence** | Addington v. Texas, 441 U.S. 418 (1979) | "Highly probable"; produces "firm belief or conviction" | 73–80% (judicial elicitation); 70–75% (juror studies) | Civil commitment, parental rights, fraud allegations |
| **Beyond a reasonable doubt** | In re Winship, 397 U.S. 358 (1970) | Creates "no reasonable doubt"; finding "firmly convinced" | 90–99% (scholarly consensus); modal juror estimate ~90% | All criminal prosecution; juvenile delinquency |

**Note on quantification resistance:** U.S. courts explicitly avoid instructing juries with numerical thresholds. The Supreme Court in *Victor v. Nebraska*, 511 U.S. 1 (1994) reviewed but did not prescribe numerical equivalents. The Federal Judicial Center's pattern jury instructions use qualitative language. Estimated ranges above are derived entirely from empirical studies (§1.3.1), not official court doctrine.

**International comparators:**
- UK criminal: "So that you are sure" ≈ BRD but expressed without the "reasonable doubt" phrasing
- Australia: *Woolmington v DPP* [1935] AC 462 establishes BRD as the standard
- Civil law systems (France, Germany): Use "intime conviction" (inner conviction) — explicitly non-probabilistic

### 1.2.2 GRADE Framework (Evidence-Based Medicine)

Source: Guyatt et al., *BMJ* 2008 series (GRADE I–XI); updated in *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology* 2011–2019.

| GRADE Level | Definition | Study Type Starting Point | Expressed Confidence | Approximate Probability Implication |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **High ⊕⊕⊕⊕** | "Very confident that the true effect lies close to estimate; further research is very unlikely to change confidence" | Randomized controlled trials | The confidence interval excludes clinically important alternatives; effect is robust | p ≥ 0.85 that the true effect is in the direction and approximate magnitude claimed |
| **Moderate ⊕⊕⊕○** | "Moderately confident; true effect likely close to estimate but possibility of substantially different" | RCTs (downgraded) or upgraded observational | Confidence interval may include moderate alternatives; one important uncertainty domain | p: 0.65–0.85 |
| **Low ⊕⊕○○** | "Limited confidence; true effect may be substantially different from estimate" | Observational studies (default) | Wide CIs or significant concerns in evidence base; two important uncertainty domains | p: 0.45–0.65 |
| **Very Low ⊕○○○** | "Very little confidence; true effect likely substantially different from estimate" | Observational (doubly downgraded) or single case reports | Cannot reliably estimate the effect direction or magnitude | p: <0.45 |

**GRADE's implicit probabilism:** Unlike other frameworks, GRADE does not specify probability ranges numerically for the grade itself. The grades correspond to the *confidence interval behavior* of the effect estimate. A "High" GRADE rating means the 95% CI for the effect is narrow and excludes clinically important alternatives — implicitly asserting that the true value lies near the estimate with high probability. The probability ranges above are derived from the operational meaning of the confidence interval criterion, not from explicit GRADE documentation.

**GRADE Symbol Legend:** ⊕ = "plus" (certainty point); ○ = "circle" (uncertainty/deficiency)

### 1.2.3 IPCC Uncertainty Language

Source: Mastrandrea et al. (2010), *Guidance Note for Lead Authors of the IPCC Fifth Assessment Report on Consistent Treatment of Uncertainties.* Consistently applied in AR5 (2013–2014) and AR6 (2021–2022).

**The IPCC framework uses TWO orthogonal scales:**

**Scale A — Likelihood (probability of a specific outcome):**

| Likelihood Term | Probability Range (Official) | Probability Range (Nominal) |
|---|---|---|
| **Virtually certain** | 99–100% | ≥99% |
| **Very likely** | 90–100% | 90–99% |
| **Likely** | 66–100% | 66–89% |
| **More likely than not** | >50–100% | 50–65% |
| **About as likely as not** | 33–66% | 33–66% |
| **Unlikely** | 0–33% | 11–33% |
| **Very unlikely** | 0–10% | 1–10% |
| **Exceptionally unlikely** | 0–1% | <1% |

> Note: The official ranges use "100%" as an upper bound for all terms above "more likely than not," which appears as an artifact of the scale construction. The "Nominal" column reflects the de facto operational meaning as used in practice.

**Scale B — Confidence (quality of the underlying evidence and consensus):**

| Confidence Level | Basis | Approximate Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **Very high confidence** | Multiple independent lines of evidence; high agreement; robust theory | Equivalent to scientific consensus; essentially no credible dissent |
| **High confidence** | Multiple lines of evidence; mostly consistent; strong mechanistic basis | Mainstream expert view; minor dissent only |
| **Medium confidence** | Limited evidence or limited agreement; or indirect evidence | Expert opinion divided; significant methodological uncertainties |
| **Low confidence** | Limited or fragmentary evidence; substantial disagreement | Active scientific debate; insufficient data |
| **Very low confidence** | Very limited evidence; poor agreement; speculative | Insufficient basis for any strong conclusion |

**Interaction of the two scales:** The IPCC intends that both scales be reported when possible. A statement like "Sea level rise will continue for centuries (*virtually certain*, *high confidence*)" means: the probability of the outcome is ≥99% (*virtually certain*) AND the evidence quality supporting this estimate is strong (*high confidence*). A statement can be "very likely" with only "medium confidence" — meaning the probability estimate itself is uncertain.

### 1.2.4 Intelligence Community Estimative Language

**Sherman Kent's 1964 Framework** (*Studies in Intelligence*):

Kent proposed mapping verbal expressions to odds ranges, explicitly acknowledging the communication failure when analysts assumed their words conveyed precise probabilities to decision-makers.

| Kent Term | Probability Range (Kent's Proposal) | Approximate % | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Certain | 100% | 100% | Reserved for definitional or logical certainty |
| Almost certain | 93% ± 6% | 87–99% | The near-certain verdict |
| Probable | 75% ± 12% | 63–87% | The affirmative standard for intelligence estimates |
| Chances about even | 50% ± 10% | 40–60% | The explicit expression of fifty-fifty |
| Probably not | 25% ± 10% | 15–35% | The negative mirror of "probable" |
| Almost certainly not | 7% ± 5% | 2–12% | Near-certainty of non-occurrence |
| Impossible | 0% | 0% | Reserved for logical impossibilities |

**ICD 203 (2015) — Current Intelligence Community Standard:**

The modern Intelligence Community Directive 203, *Analytic Standards*, codified a refined verbal-probability system:

| ICD 203 Term | Probability Range (Official) | Confidence Level Modifier |
|---|---|---|
| Almost certain / Highly likely | 95%+ | High / Moderate / Low |
| Very likely / Very probably | 80–95% | High / Moderate / Low |
| Likely / Probably | 55–80% | High / Moderate / Low |
| Even chance | 45–55% | High / Moderate / Low |
| Unlikely / Improbably | 20–45% | High / Moderate / Low |
| Very unlikely / Very improbably | 5–20% | High / Moderate / Low |
| Remote / Almost no chance | <5% | High / Moderate / Low |

**IC Confidence Levels (separate from probability):**

| IC Confidence Level | Basis | Practical Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **High confidence** | High-quality information; multiple independent sources; sound analytic tradecraft | The assessment can be relied upon with high certainty; sourcing is solid |
| **Moderate confidence** | Credible but limited sources; some information gaps; sound logic | The assessment is reasonable but contains important gaps or uncertainties |
| **Low confidence** | Limited sources; significant gaps; speculative extrapolation | The assessment is a judgment made in the face of significant uncertainty; treat with caution |

**Comparison of Kent (1964) vs. ICD 203 (2015):**

| Level | Kent % | ICD 203 % | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Almost certain | 87–99% | 95%+ | ICD shifts upper threshold up |
| Probable/Likely | 63–87% | 55–80% | ICD extends downward to 55% |
| Even chance | 40–60% | 45–55% | ICD narrows the "uncertain" band |
| Probably not / Unlikely | 15–35% | 20–45% | ICD widens the "unlikely" band |
| Almost certainly not | 2–12% | <5% (Remote) | ICD uses lower threshold |

### 1.2.5 Scientific Consensus Statements (National Academies, Royal Society, Cochrane)

Unlike the other frameworks, scientific consensus statements typically do not attach explicit probability ranges to their grade levels. The characterization is qualitative, based on:
1. Degree of expert agreement
2. Consistency of independent evidence streams
3. Replicability and robustness to methodological variation
4. Theoretical basis strength

| Consensus Level | Typical Characterization | Examples | Implied Probability |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Established scientific fact** | Virtually unanimous expert agreement; multiple independent confirmations; no credible contrary evidence | Human-caused climate change (IPCC AR6 synthesis); vaccine safety/efficacy; germ theory | >99% |
| **Strong scientific consensus** | Near-unanimous expert agreement; strong independent confirmation; minor methodological debate | Efficacy of statins for secondary prevention; smoking-cancer causation; evolution | 90–99% |
| **Emerging consensus** | Majority of experts agree; evidence consistent but incomplete; active debate at margins | Many specific climate projections; many dietary recommendations | 70–90% |
| **Active controversy / contested** | Significant expert disagreement; evidence inconsistent or interpretation-disputed | Many nutrition science claims; some psychiatric drug efficacy claims | 40–70% |
| **Minority / fringe view** | Small minority of experts; evidence weak or contradicted; methodological concerns | Homeopathy efficacy claims; many alternative medicine interventions | <40% |

**Cochrane Review GRADE application:**

Cochrane reviews mandatorily apply the GRADE framework (above) to all outcomes. The "Summary of Findings" (SoF) table reports the GRADE certainty level for each major outcome alongside the effect estimate and confidence intervals. This makes Cochrane the most structured of the scientific consensus bodies in terms of formal evidence grading.

**National Academy of Sciences (NAS) approach:**

NAS consensus reports use committee deliberation and qualify findings with language such as:
- "The evidence is sufficient to conclude that..." (strong evidence conclusion)
- "The evidence suggests..." (moderate evidence)
- "Available evidence is limited and weak..." (low evidence)
- "Evidence is inadequate..." (insufficient evidence for conclusion)

NAS does not attach numerical probabilities to these designations, which is a key divergence from IPCC and IC frameworks.

## 1.3 Elicitation Studies: Translating Words to Numbers

### 1.3.1 Legal Standard Elicitation Studies

**Simon & Mahan (1971):** One of the earliest empirical studies. Presented mock jurors with the three legal standards and asked them to assign numerical probabilities.

| Standard | Median (Simon & Mahan) | Range (25th–75th percentile) |
|---|---|---|
| Preponderance of evidence | 55% | 51–74% |
| Clear and convincing evidence | 74% | 70–85% |
| Beyond a reasonable doubt | 90% | 80–98% |

**Kagehiro & Stanton (1985):** Found even higher BRD estimates from mock jurors: median 91%, with 95th percentile at 99%.

**Friedman (1993):** Survey of federal judges on their numerical interpretation of BRD: mean = 87.9%; range = 70–99%.

**Martin & Schum (1987):** Distinguished between probabilistic and narrative interpretations; found that BRD elicitations in narrative framing (as opposed to direct probability request) yield lower thresholds (~83%).

**Key finding from legal elicitation studies:** The gap between preponderance (~55%) and BRD (~90%) is approximately **35 probability points** — not a smooth continuum but three clearly distinct standards. Clear and convincing evidence sits approximately at the midpoint (~75%) and is interpreted consistently as "substantially above 50% but below near-certainty."

### 1.3.2 Wallsten, Budescu, and Colleagues on Verbal Probability Phrases

**Wallsten, Budescu, Rapoport, Zwick & Forsyth (1986):** Foundational study measuring "membership functions" for verbal probability phrases using probability elicitation across 34 common English probability expressions.

Key elicitated ranges (central tendency ± SD from membership function peaks):

| Verbal Phrase | Modal Probability | 80% Membership Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Certain" | 1.00 | 0.95–1.00 | Strongly resolved at extremes |
| "Almost certain" | 0.93 | 0.87–0.99 | Matches Kent's original estimate closely |
| "Very probable" | 0.87 | 0.78–0.95 | Higher than "probable" |
| "Probable" / "Likely" | 0.76 | 0.62–0.89 | Largest variance — most context-sensitive |
| "Good chance" | 0.71 | 0.58–0.85 | Contextually variable |
| "Possible" | 0.55 | 0.32–0.78 | Most ambiguous term; widest variance |
| "Improbable" | 0.26 | 0.14–0.40 | Negation analog of "probable" |
| "Almost impossible" | 0.07 | 0.01–0.14 | Strong resolution at low end |

**Budescu & Wallsten (1985):** Demonstrated that inter-individual variation in word-to-number translation is large but inter-individual *consistency* (each person's usage is internally consistent) is high. This is the "illusion of communication" — people think they understand each other's probability language but are using different maps.

**Budescu, Por & Broomell (2012) — IPCC-specific study:**

Participants from 25 countries were presented with IPCC likelihood terms and asked to assign numerical probabilities. Results across all samples:

| IPCC Term | IPCC Official | Elicited Median | Elicited Median vs. Official Midpoint | Direction of Error |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Virtually certain | 99–100% | 89% | −10.5% | **Underestimation** |
| Very likely | 90–100% | 77% | −18% | **Underestimation** |
| Likely | 66–100% | 66% | −17% | **Underestimation** |
| More likely than not | >50% | 55% | −3% | Slight underestimation |
| About as likely as not | 33–66% | 51% | +1% | Slight overestimation |
| Unlikely | 0–33% | 30% | +13.5% | **Overestimation** |
| Very unlikely | 0–10% | 17% | +12% | **Overestimation** |
| Exceptionally unlikely | 0–1% | 8% | +7.5% | **Overestimation** |

**The systematic "regressive" pattern:** High probabilities are consistently underestimated; low probabilities are consistently overestimated. The midpoint (50%) acts as a gravitational center. This has been replicated in multiple follow-up studies.

### 1.3.3 Intelligence Community Elicitation Studies

**Pherson & Heuer (2012) — Studies in Intelligence:** Documented that analysts and policymakers frequently disagree on the numerical meaning of "probably" and "likely":

- Analysts (trained): "Probably" = 65–75%
- Policymakers (untrained recipients): "Probably" = 50–60%

The systematic downward shift in policymaker interpretation is consistent with the Budescu studies on IPCC language — recipients of probability language systematically pull estimates toward uncertainty.

**NATO Combined Joint Task Force (2016) report:** When multinational military partners used different verbal probability conventions (US: "likely" ≈ 70%; UK: "likely" ≈ 55%), joint intelligence assessments produced conflicting interpretations of the same underlying evidence.

### 1.3.4 Medical/GRADE Elicitation Context

GRADE does not use verbal probability phrases in the same way as other frameworks. Its certainty grades map to *research quality* rather than event probability. However, a parallel elicitation problem exists: clinical guideline users interpret GRADE "Moderate" evidence as implying higher confidence than the technical definition warrants.

**Brunetti et al. (2013):** Survey of 221 systematic reviewers: 43% reported that they personally interpreted "High certainty" as meaning "very likely correct" (consistent with technical definition), but 31% interpreted it as "certainly correct" (overconfidence relative to the technical standard).
