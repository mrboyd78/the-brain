# Evidence-Grading and Confidence-Expression Standards: Cross-Disciplinary Survey
## Executive Overview

**Survey Date:** April 2026  
**Topic:** Comparative analysis of evidence-grading and confidence-expression standards across five major disciplinary frameworks  
**Central Question:** Where do these frameworks converge, where do they diverge, and can a unified grading scale be derived?

---

## The Five Frameworks

This survey examines five independent frameworks that independently developed confidence-expression standards, often without reference to each other:

| Framework | Primary Domain | Nature of Output | Probability Specified? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Legal Standards of Proof** | Law / criminal & civil adjudication | Threshold decision (guilt/liability) | Rarely explicit; elicitation studies quantify |
| **GRADE** | Evidence-based medicine | Evidence certainty grade | Implicit (confidence interval-based) |
| **IPCC Uncertainty Language** | Climate science / policy | Likelihood + Confidence assessment | Yes (likelihood scale); No (confidence scale) |
| **Intelligence Estimative Language** | National security analysis | Probability of event + source confidence | Yes (Kent/ICD 203 word-to-number mappings) |
| **Scientific Consensus Statements** | General science (NAS, Royal Society, Cochrane) | Consensus characterization | Rarely explicit |

---

## The Core Insight: Same Problem, Five Different Solutions

All five frameworks address the same underlying problem: **how to communicate degrees of epistemic confidence to a decision-maker who must act.** The diversity of solutions reflects:

1. **Different base rates of error costs:** Criminal law minimizes false positives (wrongful convictions); intelligence sometimes accepts false negatives to avoid premature action; medicine balances both.
2. **Different update rates:** Intelligence estimates update daily; legal standards are applied once per case; GRADE evidence grades may persist for years.
3. **Different audiences:** Judges (experts in legal reasoning), voters/policymakers (general public), clinicians (domain experts), analysts (trained specialists).
4. **Different institutional constraints:** Courts resist quantification; IPCC mandates it; GRADE uses an implicit numeric framework visible in confidence intervals.

## Quick-Reference Convergence Summary

The only probability range that all five frameworks agree represents "high confidence / likely outcome" is approximately **p = 0.66–0.90**, corresponding to:
- Legal: "Clear and convincing" to "approaching beyond reasonable doubt"
- GRADE: "High" certainty (for well-conducted RCTs)
- IPCC: "Likely" to "Very likely" on the likelihood scale
- Intelligence: "Probably" / "Likely" (Kent) or ICD 203 equivalent
- Science: Strong consensus territory

The primary structural divergence is the **"beyond reasonable doubt" threshold**: set at approximately p ≥ 0.90–0.95 by legal scholars and juror elicitation studies, with no clean equivalent in any other framework.

---

## The Unified Evidence Grading Scale (UEGS)

To resolve these divergences, this survey proposes a **6-band UEGS** that serves as a common denominator for all five frameworks:

1. **Certainty (99%+)**: Mapped to *Virtually Certain*.
2. **High Confidence (90-99%)**: Mapped to *Beyond Reasonable Doubt*.
3. **Substantial Evidence (75-90%)**: Mapped to *Clear & Convincing*.
4. **Preponderance (51-75%)**: Mapped to *Civil Proof / Likely (ICD)*.
5. **Emerging Support (33-51%)**: Mapped to *More Likely Than Not*.
6. **Uncertain / Remote (<33%)**: Mapped to *Low Confidence / Unlikely*.
