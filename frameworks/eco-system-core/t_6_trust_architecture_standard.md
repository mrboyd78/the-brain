# T6 — Trust Architecture Standard

## Document Status
**System:** Market-Intelligence Operating System  
**Layer:** Strategy Layer  
**Designation:** T6  
**Document Type:** Standard  
**Status:** Canonical Draft  
**Purpose Class:** Trust Mapping / Credibility Design / Legitimacy Analysis

---

## 1. Purpose

The **Trust Architecture** is the authoritative trust-intelligence artifact for the Market-Intelligence Operating System.

Its function is to map how credibility is earned, transferred, verified, withheld, damaged, and repaired inside an ecosystem.

This document exists because many opportunities do not fail on utility alone. They fail because the actor offering the utility has not earned the right to be believed, adopted, or endorsed. Trust is not a soft afterthought. In many ecosystems it is a structural condition of entry.

T6 exists to identify the specific trust mechanics that govern whether a new entrant, offer, message, tool, or partner is treated as legitimate.

---

## 2. Strategic Role in the Operating System

The Ecosystem Analysis identifies the market terrain. T1–T5 explain culture, leverage, decision pathways, language, and competitive shape. T6 begins the Strategy layer by focusing on the conditions under which any move in that terrain is treated as believable.

T6 serves nine strategic roles:

1. **Credibility mapping** — identifies what signals actually create trust.
2. **Legitimacy gating** — reveals what an entrant must demonstrate before the ecosystem takes it seriously.
3. **Trust transfer analysis** — shows how trust can be borrowed from other actors, institutions, platforms, or signals.
4. **Distrust detection** — identifies the patterns that trigger skepticism or rejection.
5. **Reputation pathway mapping** — clarifies how trust grows over time and how it compounds.
6. **Barrier exposure** — shows where trust requirements make otherwise strong opportunities harder to enter.
7. **Segment differentiation** — reveals how trust mechanics vary across participant groups.
8. **Entry support** — gives T10 a realistic basis for deciding whether a vector is credible enough to work.
9. **Validation grounding** — helps T15 determine what trust assumptions must be tested before commitment.

T6 is therefore the operating system’s credibility-and-legitimacy layer.

---

## 3. What This Document Is

This document is:

- a map of how trust is formed and withheld in the ecosystem
- a legitimacy and credibility analysis
- a guide to trust signals, trust pathways, and trust barriers
- a downstream input to entry-vector design, validation planning, communication design, and platform strategy

---

## 4. What This Document Is Not

The Trust Architecture is **not**:

- a branding guide
- a marketing testimonial checklist
- a generic statement that “trust matters”
- a reputation-management playbook
- a product feature roadmap
- a substitute for legal compliance or security review

It should not treat trust as purely emotional when in many ecosystems it is also technical, procedural, symbolic, and institutional.

---

## 5. When to Commission or Use It

T6 should be commissioned once the ecosystem and intelligence layers are complete enough to understand the actors, culture, leverage, participant decision pathways, language, and competitive field.

It becomes mandatory whenever any of the following are true:

- the opportunity requires credibility to overcome skepticism, switching cost, or perceived risk
- the ecosystem is trust-sensitive, reputation-sensitive, or status-sensitive
- T10 Entry Vector Analysis will be produced
- messaging, partnerships, or go-to-market design risk failing because the trust requirements are misunderstood
- the offer touches money, compliance, identity, operations, or any domain where perceived risk is high

T6 is especially important when the ecosystem has visible gatekeepers, reputational memory, or prior trauma from bad actors.

---

## 6. Mandatory vs Conditional Use

**Mandatory** for:

- ecosystems where risk, compliance, money, data, safety, or identity are materially involved
- ecosystems with visible gatekeepers, endorsement hierarchies, or high skepticism toward outsiders
- any effort that will later produce T10 Entry Vector Analysis
- any opportunity where an entrant must be trusted before value can even be observed

**Conditionally recommended** for:

- highly low-risk, low-commitment consumer contexts where trust exists but is less structurally central
- early scans where trust mechanics are not yet shaping the decision horizon

Where entry depends on being believed before being tried, T6 should be treated as required.

## 11. Core Analytical Questions

T6 should allow a reviewer to answer the following questions quickly:

1. What makes an actor credible in this ecosystem?
2. What signals create immediate suspicion?
3. How is trust transferred between actors, institutions, and platforms?
4. What kinds of proof are required before adoption becomes possible?
5. Where in the participant decision path does trust matter most?
6. Which trust signals are superficial and which are decisive?
7. How does trust vary by participant type, sub-tribe, or context?
8. What causes trust to compound over time?
9. What causes trust to collapse quickly?
10. What must a new entrant do to be taken seriously at all?

---

## 12. Required Structure of the Document

Every Trust Architecture must contain the following seven sections.

### 12.1 Section 1 — Trust Preconditions
**Governing question:** What baseline conditions must be met before an actor can be considered credible at all?

This section must identify:

- baseline legitimacy requirements
- minimum proof expectations
- role of credentials, experience, or social proof
- differences between “allowed to exist” trust and “actively preferred” trust

### 12.2 Section 2 — Trust Signals
**Governing question:** What signals create trust, and how strong are they?

This section must catalogue the key trust signals in the ecosystem.

For each signal, document:

- signal type
- who emits it
- who trusts it
- relative strength
- durability
- whether it is earned, borrowed, bought, or simulated

### 12.3 Section 3 — Trust Transfer Pathways
**Governing question:** How does trust move from one actor, institution, or surface to another?

This section must map:

- endorsements
- institutional transfer
- peer-to-peer trust transfer
- platform-mediated transfer
- certification or review-based transfer
- cases where trust fails to transfer despite proximity

### 12.4 Section 4 — Distrust Triggers
**Governing question:** What causes actors to become suspicious, skeptical, or dismissive?

This section must identify:

- language triggers
- visual or symbolic triggers
- behavior patterns associated with bad actors
- pricing or offer signals that create suspicion
- platform or channel contexts that lower baseline trust

### 12.5 Section 5 — Trust by Participant Type
**Governing question:** How do trust requirements vary across participant groups?

This section must map differences in:

- proof requirements
- endorsement sensitivity
- institutional trust
- peer trust
- skepticism threshold
- trust repair possibility

### 12.6 Section 6 — Trust Compounding and Collapse
**Governing question:** How does trust build over time, and how does it break?

This section must identify:

- what causes trust to compound
- what causes it to plateau
- what causes abrupt trust collapse
- whether repair is possible and under what conditions

### 12.7 Section 7 — Strategic Trust Implications
**Governing question:** What does the trust structure imply for strategic entry, validation, and communication?

This section must remain analytical, not tactical.

It should identify:

- which trust barriers are hardest to cross
- which trust pathways seem usable by entrants
- where trust requirements make otherwise attractive opportunities much harder
- where a vector may fail because it is not credible enough, even if it is useful

## 14. Trust Preconditions Standard

This section must identify what an actor must prove just to be taken seriously.

Trust preconditions may include:

- credentials or experience
- category fluency
- institutional affiliation
- visible customer proof
- platform approval or certification
- security, compliance, or operational credibility

This section must distinguish baseline legitimacy from full trust.

---

## 15. Trust Signals Standard

This section must catalogue the signals that function as trust shortcuts in the ecosystem.

Signals may include:

- peer endorsements
- institutional affiliations
- platform badges or certifications
- proof artifacts and case evidence
- transparent pricing or process
- high-status associations
- language fluency and cultural fit

The analysis must distinguish between signals that merely look impressive and signals that actually move participants toward trust.

---

## 16. Trust Transfer Pathways Standard

This section must explain how trust moves.

Transfer pathways may include:

- one trusted actor endorsing another
- institutional membership or compliance transfer
- platform-mediated legitimacy
- social proof inside communities or sub-tribes
- proof transfer from adjacent domains

Not all trust transfers cleanly. This section must note where transfer breaks, weakens, or fails entirely.

---

## 17. Distrust Triggers Standard

This section must identify what creates suspicion quickly.

Distrust triggers may include:

- outsider language or symbolic mismatch
- aggressive pricing or offer structure
- scam-adjacent patterns
- overclaiming or hype inflation
- lack of proof or visible history
- inappropriate channel use
- platform reputation damage

Distrust triggers should be grounded in actual ecosystem patterns rather than analyst intuition.

---

## 18. Trust by Participant Type Standard

Trust is rarely uniform across the ecosystem.

This section must identify how trust differs by participant type, including:

- what each group requires to believe
- who each group trusts most
- whether each group trusts institutions, peers, platforms, or visible outcomes more heavily
- how forgiving each group is after a trust breach

This section should preserve meaningful variation rather than collapsing the ecosystem into one trust model.

---

## 19. Trust Compounding and Collapse Standard

This section must map trust over time.

It should identify:

- what kinds of repeated behavior deepen trust
- whether trust compounds linearly or nonlinearly
- what kinds of failures cause rapid collapse
- whether repair is possible, and what it requires

Some ecosystems are forgiving. Others are one-strike systems. T6 must state which kind is present.

---

## 20. Strategic Trust Implications Standard

This section must remain analytical.

It should identify:

- which trust barriers are likely to block entry entirely
- which proof burdens are manageable versus prohibitive
- where trust transfer offers leverage
n- where an entrant will need to earn trust directly rather than borrow it
- where trust assumptions must be validated before scaling effort

It should not prescribe messaging or product design in tactical detail.

---

## 21. Research Methodology Standard

The Trust Architecture should follow a credibility-pattern methodology.

Recommended minimum protocol:

### 21.1 Trusted-Actor Review
Study who is treated as credible in the ecosystem and why.

### 21.2 Distrusted-Actor Review
Study who is distrusted, mocked, ignored, or treated as unsafe, and why.

### 21.3 Proof Demand Review
Review participant demands for evidence, proof, credentials, and testimonials.

### 21.4 Trust Transfer Observation
Map where endorsements, affiliations, badges, or institutional backing materially change perception.

### 21.5 Trust Failure Review
Examine visible cases of trust collapse, backlash, or reputation damage where evidence is available.

The methodology should prioritize observed trust behavior over generic branding theory.

---

## 22. Source and Evidence Expectations

The Trust Architecture must remain grounded in ecosystem evidence.

Source expectations:

- prioritize participant-native expressions of belief, proof demand, skepticism, and endorsement behavior
- distinguish actual trust transfer from prestige signals that only appear meaningful
- flag inferred trust pathways as interpretive where evidence is partial
- use repeated patterns before treating a signal as dominant when feasible
- remain alert to recent trust shocks that may change the landscape quickly

Trust should be treated as a structural phenomenon, not merely a rhetorical one.

---

## 23. Length Calibration

Recommended target length: **3,500–5,000 words**.  
Maximum: **6,500 words**.

If the document materially exceeds the maximum, it may be cataloguing many examples without extracting the trust system clearly.

---

## 24. Required Usage Rules

The following rules are mandatory.

### Rule 1 — No generic trust statements
T6 must identify ecosystem-specific trust mechanics, not broad claims that apply anywhere.

### Rule 2 — No branding-only interpretation
Trust must be treated as structural and evidentiary, not merely emotional or aesthetic.

### Rule 3 — No participant flattening
Trust variation across participant groups must be preserved where it matters.

### Rule 4 — No transfer assumptions without testing
Trust transfer pathways must be evaluated explicitly rather than presumed.

### Rule 5 — No product contamination
T6 may not contain feature plans, campaign plans, or implementation proposals.

### Rule 6 — No distrust-blind analysis
Distrust triggers must be surfaced with the same rigor as trust signals.

### Rule 7 — No static trust assumptions
Trust should be treated as something that compounds, stalls, or collapses over time.

## 25. Recommended Operating Procedure

### Step 1 — Anchor in the Intelligence layer
Use T1–T5 to understand culture, leverage, participant decision logic, language, and competitive structure.

### Step 2 — Identify trust preconditions
Determine what an actor must prove before the ecosystem grants baseline legitimacy.

### Step 3 — Catalogue trust signals
Map the strongest signals and who they influence.

### Step 4 — Map transfer and failure pathways
Show how trust moves and where it breaks.

### Step 5 — Differentiate by participant type
Clarify how trust mechanics vary across groups.

### Step 6 — Map compounding and collapse
Understand how trust evolves over time.

### Step 7 — Produce strategic implications
Translate the trust structure into analytical conclusions that later strategy and validation work can use.

---

## 26. Quality Standard / Acceptance Criteria

The Trust Architecture should be considered acceptable only if all of the following are true:

1. Trust preconditions are clearly identified.
2. Trust signals are catalogued with relative strength and durability.
3. Trust transfer pathways are mapped explicitly.
4. Distrust triggers are surfaced and explained.
5. Participant-type variation is preserved where relevant.
6. Trust compounding and collapse dynamics are mapped.
7. Strategic implications remain analytical rather than tactical.
8. Evidence and inference are distinguished where needed.
9. The document gives later strategic work a realistic understanding of how legitimacy functions in the ecosystem.
10. The document prevents false assumptions that usefulness alone will overcome distrust.

---

## 27. Failure Modes and Misuse Warnings

Common failure modes include:

### 27.1 Generic trust language
Using empty claims such as “people need social proof” without identifying actual ecosystem trust mechanics.

### 27.2 Prestige confusion
Mistaking visible status or polish for actual trust transfer.

### 27.3 Uniformity bias
Assuming every participant group trusts the same sources and signals.

### 27.4 Overreliance on endorsements
Treating endorsements as universally transferable when many ecosystems require direct proof.

### 27.5 Distrust omission
Ignoring what makes communities suspicious or hostile.

### 27.6 Static credibility assumption
Failing to model how trust deepens, stalls, or collapses over time.

### 27.7 Product leakage
Allowing the document to become a stealth go-to-market plan.

## 29. Relationship to Agentic Research Systems

The Trust Architecture should be structured so that agents can assist with signal collection and pattern identification without mistaking visible prestige markers for validated trust mechanics.

This means:

- trust signals should be discrete and comparable
- transfer pathways should be recorded explicitly
- participant variation should be separated clearly
- distrust triggers should remain linked to evidence of actual suspicion or rejection
- strategic implications should remain reviewable rather than auto-derived from signal counts alone

An agent may help surface candidate trust signals and failure patterns.
The operating system must still judge what truly creates credibility in the ecosystem.

---

## 30. Versioning Notes

The Trust Architecture Standard should evolve when any of the following occur:

- repeated use shows missing trust-signal or distrust-trigger categories
- T15 validation reveals that proof burdens were misjudged
- T17 platform work shows stronger dependence on platform-mediated trust than the current structure captures
- T19 learning shows repeated entry failures due to underestimating skepticism or overestimating trust transfer
- fast-moving ecosystems require stronger integration with T16 refresh protocols because trust conditions shift rapidly after scandals or platform changes

Version updates should record what trust-intelligence weakness prompted the revision.

---

## 31. Final Standard Statement

In many ecosystems, being useful is not enough. An actor must also be believable.

The Trust Architecture exists to reveal how credibility is granted, borrowed, tested, and lost inside the ecosystem so that later strategy is grounded in the actual conditions of legitimacy.

Without T6, the operating system can identify opportunity.
With T6, it can understand what must be true for that opportunity to be trusted.
