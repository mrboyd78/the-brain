T 0 MARKET INTELLIGENCE KICKOFF AND COMMISSIONING
STANDARD
T0 — Market-Intelligence Kickoff & Commissioning
Standard
Document Status
**System:** Market-Intelligence Operating System
**Layer:** Pre-Analysis Commissioning Layer
**Designation:** T0
**Document Type:** Standard
**Status:** Canonical Draft
**Purpose Class:** Intake / Scoping / Workflow Initiation / Analysis Readiness
---
1. Purpose
The **Market-Intelligence Kickoff & Commissioning Standard** is the formal intake and initiation layer for the
Market-Intelligence Operating System.
Its function is to ensure that every ecosystem study begins with sufficient structure, scope definition, decision
context, boundary discipline, and evidence expectations before the analytical workflow starts.
This document exists because the operating system can only produce strong conclusions if the input problem is
framed correctly. Without a formal kickoff layer, ecosystem studies risk starting with:
• vague ecosystem boundaries
• unclear decision objectives
• missing time horizons
• hidden assumptions
• mismatched evidence expectations
• premature product thinking
• inconsistent output quality across studies
T0 exists to prevent those failures by standardizing how the system is commissioned.
---
2. Strategic Role in the Operating System
The Market-Intelligence Operating System already contains:
• the foundational ecosystem analysis doctrine
• the intelligence suite
• the strategy suite
• the T1–T19 standalone standards
What it still needs is a formal front door.

--- Page 6 ---
T0 serves ten strategic roles:
1. **Commissioning discipline** — ensures every analysis begins from a defined brief rather than a loose
prompt.
2. **Boundary control** — defines what ecosystem is actually being studied and what is excluded.
3. **Decision alignment** — forces clarity on what decision the analysis is meant to support.
4. **Input sufficiency** — ensures the system has enough context to generate meaningful conclusions.
5. **Workflow routing** — determines which documents in the operating system are required versus conditional
for a given study.
6. **Evidence readiness** — clarifies expected source classes, available evidence, and known information
gaps before analysis begins.
7. **Anti-drift protection** — prevents premature collapse into product ideation, features, or tactical execution.
8. **Comparability support** — makes multiple ecosystem studies easier to compare later under T18.
9. **Agent readiness** — creates a standardized template that humans or agents can fill consistently.
10. **Quality gating** — establishes whether the study is ready to begin or whether more scoping is needed
first.
T0 is therefore the formal commissioning layer for the full operating system.
---
3. What This Document Is
This document is:
• the official kickoff and intake standard for the operating system
• a commissioning brief template
• a readiness gate before Ecosystem Analysis begins
• a workflow-routing standard that determines which downstream documents should be produced
• the canonical starting point for each new ecosystem study
---
4. What This Document Is Not
The Kickoff & Commissioning Standard is **not**:
• the Ecosystem Analysis itself
• a product brief
• a feature ideation document
• a market-size memo
• a general brainstorm worksheet
• a substitute for T11 evidence governance or T12 uncertainty governance
It is the structured setup layer that enables all later documents to be produced with rigor.
---
5. Core Recommendation
Yes — the operating system should have a formal kickoff template standard.
Without T0, each ecosystem study must be improvised at the point of entry. That weakens consistency,
increases analytical drift, and makes it harder to know whether conclusions are strong because the system

--- Page 7 ---
worked or weak because the study began with poor framing.
With T0, every ecosystem analysis starts from:
• a defined ecosystem specification
• a clear decision question
• declared boundaries
• known evidence conditions
• a document-routing plan
• explicit readiness criteria
This should be treated as mandatory for all formal studies.
---
6. Position in the Full Workflow
T0 sits before all other documents.
Full operating sequence
1. **T0 — Kickoff & Commissioning Standard**
2. **Ecosystem Analysis Standard**
3. **T1–T5 Intelligence Layer**
4. **T6–T10 Strategy Layer**
5. **T11–T19 Companion Governance / Conversion / Portfolio / Learning Layer**
T0 does not replace any existing document. It governs when and how the rest of the system begins.
---
7. When to Use T0
T0 should be used whenever:
• a new ecosystem is being introduced into the system
• a new platform ecosystem, market ecosystem, or vertical is being studied
• a prior ecosystem study is being substantially reframed
• an agent or researcher needs a standardized intake packet before analysis begins
• multiple ecosystems will later be compared, prioritized, or routed into the same operating system
T0 should be completed before the Ecosystem Analysis is started.

A completed T0 should produce the following outputs:
• a clear ecosystem specification
• a clearly stated governing decision question
• a scope boundary definition
• a declared time horizon
• a known-input inventory
• a known-gap inventory

9. T0 Governing Questions
A good kickoff process should answer the following before analysis begins:
1. What ecosystem exactly are we studying?
2. What decision is this study meant to support?
3. What is inside scope and outside scope?
4. What geography, platform, segment, or category boundaries apply?
5. What time horizon matters?
6. What inputs do we already have?
7. What information is missing but important?
8. Which downstream documents are required for this case?
9. What would make the study insufficient before it even begins?
10. What must remain explicitly out of bounds to avoid premature ideation?

10. Required Structure of the Standard
Every T0 commissioning packet should contain the following sections.
10.1 Section 1 — Analysis Request Header
This section defines the basic request metadata.
Required fields:
• study title
• ecosystem name
• commissioning date
• requested by
• owner / analyst / agent
• status
• version
10.2 Section 2 — Governing Decision Question
This section states the exact decision the study is meant to support.
Examples:
• Is this ecosystem strategically worth entering?
• What is the best entry path into this ecosystem?
• Is this platform ecosystem viable for a small independent developer?
• Which of several ecosystems should receive focus first?
This must be specific. A vague request produces vague analysis.
10.3 Section 3 — Ecosystem Specification

--- Page 9 ---
This section defines what the ecosystem is.
Required fields:
• ecosystem type
• primary domain
• core market or platform name
• key participant groups
• primary interfaces or surfaces
• primary channels or marketplaces
• relevant business model context
• one-paragraph ecosystem description
This section should describe the ecosystem itself, not a proposed product idea.
10.4 Section 4 — Scope Boundaries
This section defines what is inside and outside the study.
Required fields:
• geographic scope
• customer / participant scope
• platform scope
• adjacent categories included
• adjacent categories excluded
• timeframe of interest
• explicit exclusions
This section is critical for preventing analysis sprawl.
10.5 Section 5 — Decision Horizon and Strategic Context
This section defines the temporal and strategic lens of the study.
Required fields:
• decision horizon
• urgency level
• why this study matters now
• strategic objective supported
• level of rigor required
The same ecosystem may be studied differently depending on whether the goal is quick-profit exploration,
long-term strategic positioning, or platform portfolio comparison.
10.6 Section 6 — Known Inputs and Existing Evidence
This section records what is already known before research begins.
Required fields:
• documents already available
• prior ecosystem knowledge
• existing audits, notes, or prior analyses
• connected platform or marketplace knowledge
• known players or actors already identified
• known relevant policies or constraints
This prevents the system from ignoring available context.

--- Page 10 ---
10.7 Section 7 — Known Unknowns and Open Questions
This section identifies what is already suspected to be unclear.
Required fields:
• top open questions
• areas of likely ambiguity
• terms needing clarification
• likely evidence gaps
• risk areas that may require special attention
This section does not replace T12. It is pre-analysis uncertainty framing.
10.8 Section 8 — Workflow Routing Matrix
This section determines which downstream documents should be produced.
Required outputs:
• Ecosystem Analysis Standard: mandatory or not
• T1–T5: mandatory / recommended / optional
• T6–T10: mandatory / recommended / optional
• T11–T19: mandatory / recommended / optional
Routing should be justified briefly.
10.9 Section 9 — Readiness Check
This section determines whether the analysis can begin.
Possible statuses:
• ready to begin
• ready with cautions
• not ready — more scoping required
• blocked — critical information missing
10.10 Section 10 — Anti-Drift Constraints
This section explicitly states what the study must not do prematurely.
Examples:
• do not propose products yet
• do not recommend features yet
• do not collapse into pricing or go-to-market tactics yet
• do not assume the ecosystem is attractive before analysis proves it
This keeps the operating system disciplined.

13. Readiness Criteria

--- Page 12 ---
A study is **ready to begin** only if all of the following are true:
1. The ecosystem is defined clearly enough to distinguish it from adjacent spaces.
2. The governing decision question is explicit.
3. Scope boundaries are set.
4. Time horizon is stated.
5. Strategic objective is stated.
6. Known inputs are logged.
7. Known unknowns are acknowledged.
8. Routing decisions for downstream documents are made.
9. Anti-drift constraints are defined.
If these conditions are not met, the study should not advance into the Ecosystem Analysis Standard.

14. Required Usage Rules
The following rules are mandatory.
Rule 1 — No analysis without a governing decision question
The system should not begin formal work on a vague ecosystem prompt.
Rule 2 — No product ideation in T0
T0 is for scoping the ecosystem study, not proposing products.
Rule 3 — No scope ambiguity
Inside-scope and outside-scope boundaries must be stated explicitly.
Rule 4 — No hidden assumptions about why the ecosystem matters
The strategic objective and urgency must be stated directly.
Rule 5 — No skipping routing
T0 must determine which downstream documents are required.
Rule 6 — No starting with zero evidence awareness
Known inputs and known gaps must be logged before beginning.
Rule 7 — No false readiness
If key scoping elements are missing, T0 must say the study is not ready.

15. Recommended Operating Procedure

--- Page 13 ---
Step 1 — Define the ecosystem
State exactly what market, platform, or ecosystem is being studied.
Step 2 — Define the decision
State exactly what decision the analysis must support.
Step 3 — Draw the boundaries
Clarify geography, participants, platforms, time horizon, and exclusions.
Step 4 — Log what is already known
Capture existing documents, known actors, existing platform knowledge, and prior findings.
Step 5 — Log what is not known
Identify the major open questions and likely gaps.
Step 6 — Route the workflow
Decide which of the Ecosystem Analysis, T1–T10, and T11–T19 documents will be required.
Step 7 — Run the readiness check
Determine whether the study can begin now or needs more scoping first.
Step 8 — Launch the analysis
Only after T0 is complete should the operating system proceed into Ecosystem Analysis.

17. Failure Modes and Misuse Warnings

--- Page 14 ---
Common failure modes include:
17.1 Vague ecosystem naming
Labeling the ecosystem too broadly or too narrowly to support useful analysis.
17.2 Decision ambiguity
Starting a study without knowing what decision the study is meant to inform.
17.3 Product contamination
Allowing the kickoff template to smuggle in preferred product directions before analysis.
17.4 Missing routing discipline
Failing to determine which downstream documents are required for the case.
17.5 Scope sprawl
Not defining what is excluded, leading to endless adjacent-market drift.
17.6 False readiness
Starting formal analysis even though key context is missing.
17.7 Agent inconsistency
Using freeform prompts instead of a standard intake packet, causing uneven quality across ecosystem studies.

T 10 ENTRY VECTOR ANALYSIS STANDARD
T10 — Entry Vector Analysis Standard
Document Status
**System:** Market-Intelligence Operating System
**Layer:** Strategy Layer
**Designation:** T10
**Document Type:** Standard
**Status:** Canonical Draft
**Purpose Class:** Strategic Entry Design / Vector Ranking / Market Insertion Analysis
---
1. Purpose
The **Entry Vector Analysis** is the authoritative strategic-entry artifact for the Market-Intelligence Operating
System.
Its function is to translate the full analytical stack into ranked, decision-grade pathways for entering an
ecosystem.
This document exists because the operating system is not meant to stop at description. After terrain,
intelligence, trust, timing, risk, and economic structure have been mapped, the organization still needs to
answer the central strategic question:
**Where should we enter, through what path, and why that path instead of another?**
T10 exists to answer that question without collapsing prematurely into product design, implementation planning,
or tactical execution. It converts the full stack into structured entry logic.
---
2. Strategic Role in the Operating System
T10 is the capstone of the original market-intelligence stack.
The Ecosystem Analysis defines the terrain. T1–T5 deepen ecosystem intelligence. T6–T9 add strategic
structure around trust, timing, fragility, and capital flow. T10 synthesizes all of that into ranked entry vectors.
T10 serves ten strategic roles:
1. **Synthesis** — converts the full stack into decision-grade entry logic.
2. **Pathway design** — identifies the specific surfaces, segments, channels, or wedges through which entry
may succeed.
3. **Vector ranking** — compares alternative pathways rather than assuming one obvious route exists.
4. **Constraint integration** — carries forward trust, timing, fragility, capital, and platform realities.
5. **False-opportunity filtration** — prevents elegant but non-viable entry ideas from advancing.
6. **Strategic sequencing** — clarifies whether a broad push, narrow wedge, partnership route, platform-native
route, or adjacent route makes the most sense.
7. **Handoff discipline** — creates the final upstream artifact before conversion into T13, T14, and T15.

--- Page 19 ---
8. **Portfolio readiness** — provides comparable opportunity pathways that can later be assessed under T18.
9. **Validation focus** — reveals which pathway-specific assumptions must be tested first.
10. **Decision accountability** — records why one entry path outranked others at this moment.
T10 is therefore the operating system’s primary strategic-entry synthesis layer.
---
3. What This Document Is
This document is:
• a ranked analysis of strategic entry pathways
• a synthesis document built from the full operating-system stack
• a pre-concept strategic decision artifact
• a bridge from ecosystem intelligence into actionable opportunity selection
---
4. What This Document Is Not
The Entry Vector Analysis is **not**:
• a product brief
• a PRD
• a launch plan
• a marketing campaign plan
• a feature roadmap
• a sales playbook
• a loose brainstorm of possible ideas
It should not prescribe implementation details, specific build scopes, or execution tasks. Its job is to identify and
rank entry paths, not to operationalize them.
---
5. When to Commission or Use It
T10 should be commissioned only after the analytical foundation is mature enough to support real strategic
choice.
It becomes mandatory whenever any of the following are true:
• the organization needs to decide where to enter an ecosystem
• multiple plausible pathways, segments, or interfaces exist
• concept work is about to begin and must be constrained by upstream strategy
• portfolio prioritization will require a clean statement of each opportunity’s best entry path
• the team risks drifting from analysis into ad hoc ideation without a disciplined bridge
T10 should not be produced too early. A weak T10 is usually a symptom of unfinished upstream work.
---
6. Mandatory vs Conditional Use

--- Page 20 ---
**Mandatory** for:
• any market-intelligence effort intended to lead to actual opportunity selection
• any ecosystem with more than one plausible entry path
• any effort that will proceed into T13, T14, T15, T17, or T18
• any strategic process where the mode of entry matters as much as the existence of demand
**Conditionally recommended** for:
• one-off descriptive research that will not inform real strategic choice
• very immature ecosystem scans where no credible pathway analysis can yet be supported
Where the goal is to move from understanding a market to entering it, T10 should be treated as required.

11. Core Analytical Questions
T10 should allow a reviewer to answer the following questions quickly:
1. What are the plausible ways to enter this ecosystem?
2. Which vectors are most credible, usable, and economically meaningful?
3. Which vectors fail because of trust, timing, fragility, or economic access?

1. Purpose
The **Evidence Ledger** is the authoritative evidence-governance artifact for the Market-Intelligence Operating
System.
Its function is to create a single, structured record of the sources, claims, confidence boundaries,
contradictions, and freshness conditions that support all downstream analytical work.
The ledger exists to ensure that the operating system does not merely produce compelling analysis, but
produces analysis that can be:
• traced
• challenged
• refreshed
• defended
• revised
• compared over time
The Evidence Ledger is the system’s control layer for factual grounding.
---
2. Strategic Role in the Operating System
The Market-Intelligence Operating System already produces analytical outputs that depend on disciplined
sourcing, confidence boundaries, and explicit assumptions. The Evidence Ledger operationalizes those
requirements by turning evidence management into a governed system rather than an informal research habit.
It serves five strategic roles:
1. **Traceability** — every meaningful claim should be traceable to an identified evidence source or explicitly
marked as inferential.
2. **Auditability** — reviewers must be able to verify what evidence was used, when it was gathered, and how
strong it is.
3. **Freshness control** — the system must know which conclusions are vulnerable to staleness.
4. **Contradiction handling** — conflicting evidence must be surfaced and governed, not silently collapsed.

--- Page 36 ---
5. **Cross-document continuity** — evidence used in EA, T1–T10, and later companion documents must be
linkable across the full lifecycle.
This document is therefore a **governance backbone** rather than a narrative analysis template.

This document is:
• a structured ledger for research evidence
• a control mechanism for source quality and claim support
• a freshness and contradiction registry
• a cross-reference layer between sources and analytical outputs
• a reusable artifact that persists beyond any single report

4. What This Document Is Not
The Evidence Ledger is **not**:
• an ecosystem analysis
• a market summary
• a bibliography without structure
• a notes dump
• a quote repository without claim linkage
• a substitute for judgment
• a place to hide unsupported assertions
It does not perform the analysis itself. It governs the evidence used to produce analysis.

14. Source Classification Rules
All sources must be classified in a way that allows the system to distinguish strong evidence from weak
evidence.

• official platform documentation
• regulatory filings
• company earnings materials
• first-party policies

• official marketplace requirements
Primary sources should be preferred wherever available.
14.2 Secondary Sources
Examples include:
• reputable analyst commentary
• industry publications
• trusted market research summaries
• credible press reporting used to interpret a primary event
Secondary sources may support context, synthesis, and external interpretation, but should not quietly replace

• uncontrolled commentary
• hearsay summaries

15. Authority Rating Framework
Each source must receive an authority rating appropriate to context.
Recommended scale:
• **A — Authoritative:** direct, official, highly relevant, and current
• **B — Strong:** credible and relevant, but not fully canonical
• **C — Useful but limited:** partially credible, contextual, or incomplete
• **D — Weak:** low-confidence, weakly attributable, or highly indirect
• **E — Unacceptable:** should not be used for support
Authority must be judged relative to the claim being supported.
A source can be A-level for one question and C-level for another.
---
16. Freshness Rating Framework
Because some evidence decays faster than others, each source should receive a freshness rating.
Recommended categories:
• **F1 — Highly time-sensitive**
• **F2 — Moderately time-sensitive**
• **F3 — Slowly changing**
• **F4 — Structurally stable**
Examples:
• policy updates, pricing, roadmap clues, and marketplace rules may be F1
• annual reports may be F2 or F3 depending on use
• foundational ecosystem structure may be F3 or F4
Freshness must be recorded independently from authority.
A source may be authoritative but stale.
---
17. Support Strength Rules
Claims should not all be treated equally. The ledger must distinguish support strength.

--- Page 43 ---
Recommended categories:
• **Directly supported** — clearly established by source evidence
• **Strongly supported** — not explicit in one source, but strongly substantiated by multiple sources
• **Conditionally supported** — plausible but dependent on assumptions or incomplete evidence
• **Weakly supported** — thin evidence; use caution
• **Unsupported** — no acceptable support found
Unsupported claims should not appear in strategic outputs unless explicitly framed as hypotheses.
---
18. Inference Level Rules
The ledger must mark whether a claim is evidence-derived or analyst-constructed.
Recommended categories:
• **I0 — Descriptive fact**
• **I1 — Low inference**
• **I2 — Moderate inference**
• **I3 — High inference / synthesis**
• **I4 — Strategic hypothesis**
This is necessary because many of the system’s most valuable outputs are not simple facts; they are
judgments. The ledger should not suppress judgment. It should govern it.
---
19. Confidence Handling
Confidence should reflect the relationship between evidence strength, contradiction level, and inference load.
Recommended fields:
• high confidence
• moderate confidence
• provisional confidence
• low confidence
Confidence should not be assigned based on writing quality or analyst certainty. It must reflect evidentiary
condition.

20. Contradiction Handling Standard
Contradictions must be recorded whenever:
• two sources disagree materially
• one source appears to supersede another
• evidence differs by geography, segment, or time frame
• the interpretation of a source is contested
• platform or policy language is ambiguous enough to support multiple readings
Contradictions should never be quietly ignored.
Each contradiction must be assigned one of the following statuses:

--- Page 44 ---
• resolved
• unresolved but low impact
• unresolved and decision-relevant
• pending additional evidence
Any unresolved contradiction that affects T10, T13, T14, T15, T17, or T18 should be escalated visibly.

21. Evidence Gap Standard
A formal gap should be logged whenever:
• an important question lacks acceptable evidence
• a key conclusion depends on a thin base of support
• evidence exists but is outdated relative to decision stakes
• access constraints prevented collection of needed evidence
• the ecosystem is shifting too quickly for older evidence to remain reliable
Gaps are not failures. Ungoverned gaps are failures.

22. Required Usage Rules
The following rules are mandatory.
Rule 1 — No silent sourcing
No strategic claim should appear in formal outputs without either:
• linked evidence, or
• explicit labeling as inference, assumption, or hypothesis
Rule 2 — No hidden contradictions
Contradictory evidence must be logged.
Rule 3 — No stale evidence masquerading as current evidence
Time-sensitive evidence must be freshness-scored.
Rule 4 — No single-source fragility for major conclusions
Major rankings, vectors, or platform judgments should not depend on one weak source where broader evidence
is available.
Rule 5 — No undifferentiated source lists
A simple bibliography is insufficient. Source function and support role must be explicit.
Rule 6 — No evidence laundering through summary language
An unsupported inference cannot be made to look stronger by being stated elegantly.

1. Purpose
The **Assumption & Unknowns Register** is the authoritative uncertainty-governance artifact for the
Market-Intelligence Operating System.
Its function is to identify, classify, govern, and track the assumptions, unresolved questions, missing knowledge,
interpretive leaps, and confidence boundaries that remain after evidence collection and analysis.
The register exists to ensure that the system does not confuse:
• evidence with inference
• inference with conviction
• conviction with certainty
• missing knowledge with resolved knowledge
Its role is not to eliminate uncertainty. Its role is to make uncertainty visible, bounded, actionable, and
governable.

2. Strategic Role in the Operating System
The Market-Intelligence Operating System is designed to produce disciplined judgments about ecosystems,
participants, strategy, timing, risk, and entry vectors. Those judgments inevitably contain assumptions.
Some assumptions arise because:
• the market is partially opaque
• evidence is incomplete or contradictory
• future behavior cannot be directly observed yet
• strategic synthesis requires interpretation beyond descriptive fact
• platform or ecosystem conditions are changing faster than the evidence base can stabilize
The Assumption & Unknowns Register exists to prevent those realities from being buried inside elegant writing.
It serves six strategic roles:

--- Page 51 ---
1. **Boundary setting** — clarifies what the system knows versus what it believes.
2. **Risk exposure** — identifies which assumptions carry real downstream decision risk.
3. **Validation readiness** — creates a direct bridge into experiment design and concept testing.
4. **Decision hygiene** — prevents overconfident executive compression.
5. **Refresh discipline** — highlights which conclusions are most vulnerable to new information.
6. **Learning-loop integrity** — allows future outcomes to be compared against explicit prior assumptions.
This document is therefore a core governance instrument, not a narrative template.

3. What This Document Is
This document is:
• a structured register of assumptions embedded in analysis
• a governed inventory of unknowns and unresolved questions
• a severity map for uncertainty exposure
• a bridge between research and validation
• a mechanism for preventing hidden fragility in strategic outputs

The Assumption & Unknowns Register is **not**:
• a place to excuse weak research
• a substitute for evidence collection
• a brainstorming dump of every possible question
• a narrative risk memo

• a vague disclaimer layer
It does not justify avoidable ignorance. It governs unavoidable uncertainty.

14. Assumption Type Framework
Each assumption must be categorized so the system understands what kind of fragility it is dealing with.
Recommended categories:
• **Behavioral assumption** — assumes participant behavior, motivation, adoption, or response
• **Structural assumption** — assumes something about ecosystem shape or force persistence
• **Strategic assumption** — assumes an entry vector, wedge, or positioning logic will hold
• **Trust assumption** — assumes credibility, legitimacy, or adoption thresholds can be crossed
• **Timing assumption** — assumes market timing, readiness, or rhythm conditions
• **Access assumption** — assumes platform access, approval, distribution, or operational feasibility
• **Monetization assumption** — assumes pricing power, willingness to pay, margin logic, or revenue path
• **Execution assumption** — assumes the organization can actually deliver on the requirement
• **Comparative assumption** — assumes one ecosystem, segment, or vector is stronger than another
• **Validation assumption** — assumes a test method is fit to reveal the truth needed
A single assumption may touch multiple domains, but one primary type should be assigned.

15. Unknowns Framework
Unknowns are not identical to assumptions.
An **assumption** is a proposition the analysis is currently leaning on.
An **unknown** is a gap or unresolved domain where the system does not yet know enough to make a stable
claim.
Recommended unknown categories:
• missing data
• inaccessible evidence
• unresolved contradiction
• future-state ambiguity
• segment opacity
• operational blind spot
• platform policy uncertainty
• pricing uncertainty
• trust or adoption opacity
• competitive response uncertainty
The register must keep these distinct.
Unknowns become dangerous when quietly converted into assumptions without governance.

16. Why Assumptions Exist
Every assumption should record why it exists. Recommended reasons include:

--- Page 57 ---
• evidence incomplete
• evidence contradictory
• evidence stale
• evidence too costly to gather now
• future behavior cannot be observed directly
• inference required for strategic synthesis
• access limitation prevented confirmation
• platform or regulatory ambiguity remains unresolved
This matters because not all assumptions are equally blameworthy.
Some are unavoidable. Some are signs of weak process.

17. Decision Relevance Framework
Each assumption or unknown should be ranked by how directly it affects decisions.
Recommended categories:
• **Critical** — could materially change go / no-go, vector ranking, concept selection, or ecosystem choice
• **High** — unlikely to reverse the whole decision alone, but could materially distort execution or prioritization
• **Moderate** — meaningful, but manageable within adaptive execution
• **Low** — useful context, limited consequence if wrong
No executive decision memo should treat all assumptions as equally important.

Recommended impact areas:
• ecosystem selection
• segment selection

• vector ranking
• concept translation
• validation design
• pricing / monetization
• trust acquisition
• platform access
• operational feasibility
• timing / launch window
• portfolio prioritization

Severity should reflect the combined effect of:

--- Page 58 ---
• decision relevance
• blast radius
• time sensitivity

• **S1 — Critical / blocking**
• **S2 — High / decision-relevant**
• **S3 — Moderate / manageable**
• **S4 — Low / monitor**
Severity is not the same as confidence.

• local
• multi-section
• system-wide
• decision-reversing

• high confidence assumption
• moderate confidence assumption
• provisional assumption
• weak assumption

It reflects how strong the current basis is for leaning on it.

• what the vector implies must exist or be delivered
• what form the intervention might take
• why that form is coherent with the ecosystem’s structure
• what forms were rejected and why
• why the candidate concept is a faithful expression of the vector rather than a random product idea
This is one of the most important sections of T14.
It is the safeguard against strategic drift.
---
18. Concept Candidate Standard
Each concept candidate should be expressed in bounded form.
At minimum, each candidate should define:
• the primary user or actor
• the job or friction being addressed
• the mechanism of value delivery
• the interface or delivery surface
• the core differentiating logic
• the concept form (tool, extension, workflow layer, analytics layer, service wrapper, marketplace app, etc.)

--- Page 89 ---
• what is deliberately excluded at this stage
Candidates should be specific enough to validate and compare, but not yet expanded into full product plans.

19. Value Hypothesis Standard
Each candidate must include one or more explicit value hypotheses.
Recommended fields:
• value delivered to whom
• why the value matters now
• why existing alternatives underperform
• what outcome would cause the target participant to care
• what behavior or adoption signal would indicate the hypothesis may be true
A concept without a clear value hypothesis is not ready for T15.
---
20. Constraint Set Standard
T14 must preserve the constraints identified upstream.
Recommended constraint domains:
• platform access constraints
• policy or review constraints
• trust acquisition constraints
• timing constraints
• monetization constraints
• operational complexity constraints
• required integration constraints
• adoption-friction constraints
• evidence or assumption constraints
Constraints should shape the concept from the beginning rather than being discovered too late.
---
21. Embedded Assumptions and Risks Standard
The brief must carry forward only the assumptions and risks that materially shape the concept.
Recommended categories:
• demand assumption
• behavior-change assumption
• access assumption
• monetization assumption
• trust assumption
• implementation / delivery assumption
• validation-design risk
These should be drawn from T12, not invented casually inside T14.

22. Initial Monetization and Access Logic Standard
T14 should include an early but disciplined view of how the concept could capture value.
This should address:
• who pays or what economic event creates value
• whether the concept fits the platform’s monetization pathways
• whether pricing would likely attach to workflow, compliance, convenience, revenue lift, cost reduction, or some
other mechanism
• what access path exists to reach the target user or buyer
This section should remain provisional.
T14 is not the final pricing document.

23. Recommended Validation Direction Standard
The brief must end with a clear recommendation for what should be tested next.
Recommended outputs:
• select one concept candidate for focused validation
• select two candidates for comparative validation
• select one core assumption for direct testing before concept comparison
• defer concept testing until one blocking issue is resolved
T14 should not terminate in conceptual ambiguity.
It must point toward structured next action.

24. Required Usage Rules
The following rules are mandatory.
Rule 1 — No concept without vector lineage
Every concept candidate must be explicitly traceable to a selected vector.
Rule 2 — No freeform ideation drift
T14 is not an invitation to invent unrelated ideas.
Rule 3 — No constraint stripping
Platform, trust, access, timing, and monetization realities must remain visible.
Rule 4 — No feature sprawl

--- Page 91 ---
The brief must stay at concept level, not backlog level.
Rule 5 — No validation handoff without explicit hypotheses
A concept must contain stated value and assumption logic before moving into T15.
Rule 6 — No pretending all variants are equal
The brief should indicate which candidate or pathway appears strongest and why.
Rule 7 — No conversion without upstream maturity
T14 should not be used to replace unfinished strategic analysis.

25. Recommended Operating Procedure
Step 1 — Confirm the selected vector
Verify that T10 / T13 has clearly identified the path being advanced.
Step 2 — Restate the strategic logic
Capture the core force, friction, and value-capture rationale behind the vector.
Step 3 — Define the conversion objective
Clarify whether the goal is one candidate, multiple variants, or comparative concept shaping.
Step 4 — Generate bounded concept candidates
Develop concept options that remain faithful to the vector and constraint set.
Step 5 — Extract value hypotheses and embedded assumptions
Define what each candidate is really claiming about the market.
Step 6 — Carry forward critical constraints
Ensure trust, timing, access, platform, and monetization conditions remain visible.
Step 7 — Recommend a validation pathway
Identify what should move next into T15.
Step 8 — Archive the conversion logic
Preserve the document as the formal record of how strategy became concept.

26. Quality Standard / Acceptance Criteria
The Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief should be considered acceptable only if all of the following are true:
1. The selected vector is clearly identified and explained.
2. Concept candidates are traceable to upstream strategy.
3. The force or friction being targeted is explicit.
4. Concept candidates are bounded and distinct.
5. Value hypotheses are clearly stated.
6. Critical constraints remain visible.
7. Material assumptions and risks are carried forward from T12.
8. The brief provides a clear recommendation for next-step validation.
9. A reviewer can see why the concept exists, not just what it is.

27. Failure Modes and Misuse Warnings
Common failure modes include:
27.1 Ideation masquerading as conversion
If the brief drifts into generic product brainstorming, lineage is lost.
27.2 Feature inflation
Adding too much detail too soon makes the concept harder to test and easier to distort.
27.3 Strategy amnesia
If the vector rationale is no longer visible, T14 has failed its core function.
27.4 Constraint blindness
Ignoring platform, trust, timing, or monetization limits at the concept stage creates downstream waste.
27.5 Weak value hypotheses
If the concept cannot clearly state why anyone would care, it is not ready for validation.
27.6 False optionality
Producing many variants with no meaningful distinction or ranking creates noise rather than structured choice.
27.7 Premature certainty
T14 should shape candidates, not declare product-market fit before testing.

T 15 VALIDATION EXPERIMENT DESIGN STANDARD
T15 — Validation & Experiment Design Standard
Document Status
**System:** Market-Intelligence Operating System
**Layer:** Companion Validation Layer
**Designation:** T15
**Document Type:** Standard
**Status:** Canonical Draft
**Purpose Class:** Assumption Testing / Pre-Build Validation / Experimental Decision Control
---
1. Purpose
The **Validation & Experiment Design Standard** is the authoritative pre-build testing artifact for the
Market-Intelligence Operating System.
Its function is to convert the most decision-relevant assumptions embedded in a concept pathway into explicit
validation programs, test structures, and decision thresholds before full product specification or build
commitment occurs.
This document exists to solve a common failure pattern: a concept that is strategically coherent on paper often
proceeds directly into building, packaging, or selling before the highest-risk assumptions have been tested. T15
is designed to interrupt that pattern.
Its role is to ensure that the operating system does not move from:
• insight to implementation
• concept to commitment
• strategy to spend
without first determining what reality must reveal.
---
2. Strategic Role in the Operating System
The Market-Intelligence Operating System deliberately stops upstream work at strategy and concept
translation. That protects against premature ideation, but it does not by itself protect against premature
execution.
T15 serves nine strategic roles:
1. **Validation gating** — creates the formal gate between concept formation and build commitment.
2. **Assumption prioritization** — focuses attention on the assumptions most worth testing first.
3. **Experimental discipline** — forces validation into structured form rather than vague “market feedback.”
4. **Capital protection** — reduces avoidable spend on concepts that have not earned implementation.
5. **Decision calibration** — defines what evidence is sufficient to continue, revise, defer, or stop.
6. **Learning efficiency** — ensures tests are designed to generate useful information, not noise.

--- Page 98 ---
7. **Uncertainty reduction** — converts T12 from a passive registry into an active decision tool.
8. **Traceability** — preserves the chain from vector to concept to test to outcome.
9. **Portfolio utility** — allows multiple opportunities to be compared not only by attractiveness, but by
validation burden.
T15 is therefore the operating system’s experimental decision-control layer.
---
3. What This Document Is
This document is:
• a structured validation plan
• an experiment design standard
• a pre-build decision gate
• a bridge from concept logic into real-world testing
• a system for defining pass / fail / revise thresholds
---
4. What This Document Is Not
The Validation & Experiment Design Standard is **not**:
• a general product roadmap
• a growth plan
• a launch plan
• a backlog of tasks without learning logic
• a vague instruction to “talk to customers”
• an analytics dashboard specification detached from decision thresholds
• an excuse to perform experiments without a decision they are meant to inform
T15 should not be used to create activity without epistemic purpose.
---
5. When to Commission or Use It
T15 should be commissioned after a concept pathway has been defined in T14 and before significant
implementation, platform development, design elaboration, or go-to-market preparation proceeds.
It becomes mandatory whenever any of the following are true:
• T14 has produced one or more concept candidates
• one or more decision-critical assumptions in T12 remain unresolved
• the cost of building before learning would be material
• a founder or team is tempted to move from concept to execution on conviction alone
• the opportunity requires proof of demand, willingness to pay, platform access, trust, or behavior change before
commitment
T15 should not be delayed until after build begins.
Late validation is often a disguised form of non-validation.

6. Mandatory vs Conditional Use
**Mandatory** for:
• any concept candidate selected from T14 for serious consideration
• any build path with meaningful cost, time, or reputational exposure
• any ecosystem where platform approval, trust, or monetization uncertainty is material
• any portfolio allocation decision where validation burden affects prioritization
• any situation where T12 includes S1 or S2 assumptions relevant to the concept
**Conditionally recommended** for:
• very small low-cost probes where no meaningful build or capital commitment is at stake
• educational or exploratory experiments that are not yet tied to a formal opportunity decision
Where a concept may become real, T15 should be treated as required.
