## 1. Purpose

The **Lever Architecture** is the authoritative influence-and-activation artifact for the Market-Intelligence Operating System.

Its function is to identify the mechanisms through which movement actually occurs inside an ecosystem: what creates traction, what accelerates adoption, what amplifies trust, what shifts behavior, what converts attention into action, and what bottlenecks or deadens momentum.

This document exists because ecosystems are not governed only by formal structure. They are governed by leverage. Some actors, signals, channels, incentives, rituals, moments, and interfaces have disproportionate power to change outcomes. T2 exists to reveal that architecture.

Its role is to answer not merely **who exists in the ecosystem**, but **what actually moves the ecosystem**.

---

## 2. Strategic Role in the Operating System

The Ecosystem Analysis identifies the terrain. T1 reveals the cultural meaning structure. T2 explains the operating mechanics of movement inside that terrain.

T2 serves nine strategic roles:

1. **Influence detection** — identifies who or what exerts outsized downstream effects.
2. **Adoption logic mapping** — explains how interest becomes trial, trial becomes habit, and habit becomes spread.
3. **Bottleneck identification** — reveals where momentum stalls.
4. **Signal hierarchy clarification** — distinguishes what gets ignored from what gets acted upon.
5. **Channel relevance mapping** — identifies where leverage actually lives versus where visibility merely appears to live.
6. **Trigger recognition** — surfaces moments or conditions that make behavior more likely to shift.
7. **System asymmetry exposure** — reveals where small interventions may have large effects.
8. **Strategic force selection** — helps later strategy choose entry points that work with the ecosystem’s motion rather than against it.
9. **Validation grounding** — gives T15 and later work a more realistic basis for deciding what to test and where.

T2 is therefore the operating system’s leverage-intelligence layer.

---

## 3. What This Document Is

This document is:

- a map of ecosystem leverage mechanics
- an influence architecture analysis
- a signal, trigger, and bottleneck document
- a downstream input to strategy, trust, timing, and entry work
- a mechanism-level complement to participant and cultural analysis

---

## 4. What This Document Is Not

The Lever Architecture is **not**:

- a generic funnel diagram
- a marketing channel plan
- an org chart of ecosystem actors
- a feature roadmap
- a list of influencers without force analysis
- a growth-playbook substitute

It should not confuse visibility with leverage, or activity with movement.

---

## 5. When to Commission or Use It

T2 should be commissioned once the Ecosystem Analysis has defined the participant landscape and once there is a need to understand how adoption, behavior, trust, and momentum actually propagate.

It becomes mandatory whenever any of the following are true:

- later strategic work depends on choosing an entry point or wedge
- influence, coordination, or trust transfer seem material to success
- there is uncertainty about what actually causes movement in the ecosystem
- T10 Entry Vector Analysis will be produced
- messaging, channel, partnership, or timing decisions risk being made on false assumptions about leverage

T2 is most useful when the ecosystem appears dynamic, networked, socially mediated, or bottlenecked by specific triggers or gatekeepers.

---

## 6. Mandatory vs Conditional Use

**Mandatory** for:

- ecosystems where adoption, trust, and action are socially or platform mediated
- ecosystems with visible gatekeepers, amplifiers, influencers, or chokepoints
- any work that will later produce T10 Entry Vector Analysis
- any opportunity where channel choice, partner choice, or timing could materially alter outcomes

**Conditionally recommended** for:

- highly simple or linear transactional ecosystems
- small markets where leverage is weakly distributed or trivially understood
- early scans before force selection becomes decision-relevant

Where disproportionate movement exists, T2 should be treated as required.

T2 produces the following outputs:

- the mechanisms through which those levers exert force
- bottlenecks and choke points
- trigger conditions that increase the probability of movement

## 11. Core Analytical Questions

T2 should allow a reviewer to answer the following questions quickly:

1. What actually moves this ecosystem?
2. Which actors, signals, or conditions have disproportionate influence?
3. What channels amplify action rather than merely create exposure?
4. What triggers cause participants to pay attention, adopt, or switch behavior?
5. Where does movement stall?
6. Which bottlenecks or gatekeepers control flow?
7. What kinds of interventions are likely to have leverage versus low impact?
8. Where is leverage centralized, and where is it distributed?
9. How do culture, status, and incentives alter leverage?
10. What would make an entrant’s efforts compound rather than dissipate?

## 12. Required Structure of the Document

Every Lever Architecture must contain the following eight sections.

### 12.1 Section 1 — Dominant Levers
**Governing question:** What are the main mechanisms that create meaningful movement in this ecosystem?

This section must identify 5–10 dominant levers.

For each lever, document:

- what it is
- what it influences
- how strong it appears to be
- whether it is direct, indirect, symbolic, technical, social, or economic

### 12.2 Section 2 — Influence Pathways
**Governing question:** Through what pathways do levers exert force?

This section must map:

- who influences whom
- through what medium or interface
- with what delay, decay, or distortion
- where influence converts into action and where it only produces awareness

### 12.3 Section 3 — Trigger Conditions
**Governing question:** Under what conditions are participants most likely to move?

This section must identify the moments, events, states, or pressures that cause decision thresholds to shift.

Examples may include:

- seasonal changes
- platform changes
- social proof accumulation
- fear, urgency, prestige, cost shocks, or status opportunities

### 12.4 Section 4 — Bottlenecks and Choke Points
**Governing question:** Where does movement stall or get filtered?

This section must identify:

- adoption bottlenecks
- trust bottlenecks
- access bottlenecks
- technical bottlenecks
- approval bottlenecks
- attention bottlenecks

### 12.5 Section 5 — Amplifiers and Dampeners
**Governing question:** What magnifies movement, and what suppresses it?

This section must distinguish between factors that:

- increase spread, trust, or action
- reduce spread, trust, or action
- create asymmetry between actors or messages

### 12.6 Section 6 — Signal Hierarchy
**Governing question:** Which signals matter most inside the ecosystem, and which are treated as weak or ignorable?

This section must identify:

- high-trust signals
- low-trust signals
- signals that matter only in certain sub-tribes or contexts
- how signal meaning changes with source or channel

### 12.7 Section 7 — Leverage Concentration Map
**Governing question:** Where is leverage concentrated, and where is it diffuse?

This section must assess:

- whether the ecosystem is gatekeeper-driven or decentralized
- where a new entrant would need to plug in to matter
- whether leverage is owned, borrowed, rented, or emergent

### 12.8 Section 8 — Intervention Implications
**Governing question:** What kinds of moves appear most likely to create outsized effects?

This section must remain analytical, not prescriptive product planning.

It should identify:

- which leverage zones look promising
- which intervention types appear likely to compound
- which kinds of moves appear likely to fail because they ignore ecosystem mechanics

## 14. Dominant Levers Standard

This section must identify the few forces that actually move the ecosystem rather than listing every plausible influence.

Recommended lever categories include:

- social influence
- status transfer
- technical interoperability
- cost pressure
- urgency or deadline pressure
- policy or platform change
- trust endorsement
- habit formation
- workflow dependency
- visibility amplification

The section should prioritize explanatory power over completeness.

---

## 15. Influence Pathways Standard

This section must map movement pathways rather than static importance.

For each major pathway, the analysis should clarify:

- where force originates
- how it travels
- where it weakens
- where it becomes consequential action
- which actors can translate influence across boundaries

The goal is to identify the ecosystem’s motion mechanics, not just its loudest voices.

---

## 16. Trigger Conditions Standard

Triggers are the threshold shifters of the ecosystem.

This section should identify not only recurring moments, but also the emotional, financial, reputational, or technical states that make participants more likely to act.

Where possible, distinguish:

- chronic conditions that keep participants primed
- acute events that provoke action
- symbolic moments that heighten responsiveness

---

## 17. Bottlenecks and Choke Points Standard

Bottlenecks are leverage in reverse.

This section should identify where otherwise promising movement fails because:

- approval is required
- trust is insufficient
- switching cost is too high
- technical integration is too difficult
- information does not convert into action
- the wrong actor controls the next step

This section is critical because many weak strategies fail not from bad ideas but from ignored chokepoints.

---

## 18. Amplifiers and Dampeners Standard

This section must identify what makes a signal or intervention more potent or less potent than expected.

Amplifiers may include:

- status endorsement
- public visibility
- urgency compression
- social proof
- compatibility with existing rituals or habits

Dampeners may include:

- skepticism
- fragmented demand
- trust deficits
- complexity friction
- weak channel-message fit

---

## 19. Signal Hierarchy Standard

Not all signals are equal.

This section must distinguish between:

- signals that create belief
- signals that merely create awareness
- signals that matter only when emitted by certain actors
- signals that are discounted because they are overused, self-interested, or culturally foreign

Where possible, relate signal meaning back to T1 cultural hierarchy and taboo structure.

---

## 20. Leverage Concentration Map Standard

This section must identify whether leverage is:

- centralized in a few actors or interfaces
- decentralized but patterned
- highly fragmented
- platform-controlled
- community-owned
- transient and event-driven

This matters because entry tactics differ radically depending on whether leverage is concentrated or diffuse.

---

## 21. Intervention Implications Standard

This section must stay analytical.

It should identify:

- where leverage appears most usable
- which intervention classes appear promising
- which parts of the ecosystem are likely too inert or too defended
- where a small move might create disproportionate downstream effect

It should not propose product features, campaigns, or implementation plans directly.

---

## 22. Research Methodology Standard

The Lever Architecture should follow a mechanism-detection methodology.

Recommended minimum protocol:

### 22.1 Movement Case Review
Examine examples of adoption, spread, coordination, or trust transfer inside the ecosystem.

### 22.2 Failure Case Review
Examine examples where promising ideas, tools, or messages failed to move the ecosystem and identify why.

### 22.3 Actor-to-Actor Influence Mapping
Review how high-trust actors affect adjacent participants and where those effects break down.

### 22.4 Trigger Observation
Look for the conditions that repeatedly precede visible changes in behavior or attention.

The methodology should prioritize observed movement over abstract causal speculation.

---

## 23. Source and Evidence Expectations

The Lever Architecture must remain evidence-based even when some movement logic is partly inferential.

Source expectations:

- rely first on observed ecosystem behavior, repeat patterns, and primary ecosystem material where feasible
- use multiple examples before elevating a mechanism into a “dominant lever” when possible
- flag leverage claims as inferential where direct observation is thin
- distinguish durable levers from temporary anomalies

The document should be comfortable with structured inference, but not with unsupported causal storytelling.

---

## 24. Length Calibration

Recommended target length: **3,500–5,500 words**.  
Maximum: **7,000 words**.

If the document materially exceeds the maximum, the analysis may be cataloguing too many low-power mechanisms instead of isolating the dominant leverage architecture.

---

## 25. Required Usage Rules

The following rules are mandatory.

### Rule 1 — No visibility-for-leverage confusion
High visibility does not automatically equal high ecosystem leverage.

### Rule 2 — No generic funnel substitution
T2 must reflect ecosystem-specific movement mechanics, not imported marketing abstractions.

### Rule 3 — No bottleneck omission
Choke points and dampeners must be treated as integral to leverage analysis.

### Rule 4 — No static actor lists
Actors must be analyzed in terms of what force they exert and through what pathway.

### Rule 5 — No product contamination
T2 may not contain feature plans or build proposals.

### Rule 6 — No signal flattening
Different signals, sources, and channels must be distinguished by trust and consequence.

### Rule 7 — No overcounting minor mechanisms
The document should isolate dominant levers, not create an exhaustive list of every possible influence.

---

## 26. Recommended Operating Procedure

### Step 1 — Anchor in the Ecosystem Analysis
Use the participant map and preliminary leverage signals as the foundation.

### Step 2 — Identify movement cases
Study how ideas, tools, or behaviors have actually spread or stalled in the ecosystem.

### Step 3 — Isolate dominant levers
Determine which forces consistently explain movement.

### Step 4 — Map pathways, triggers, and bottlenecks
Clarify how leverage travels, when it activates, and where it dies.

### Step 5 — Differentiate amplifiers, dampeners, and signal hierarchies
Explain why some moves compound and others disappear.

### Step 6 — Assess leverage concentration
Determine whether entry requires centralized access, distributed trust, or hybrid motion.

### Step 7 — Produce intervention implications
Translate the mechanism map into analytical conclusions that later strategy can use.

---

## 27. Quality Standard / Acceptance Criteria

The Lever Architecture should be considered acceptable only if all of the following are true:

1. The dominant levers are clearly identified.
2. Influence pathways are mapped rather than implied.
3. Trigger conditions are specific and credible.
4. Bottlenecks and choke points are explicit.
5. Amplifiers and dampeners are distinguished clearly.
6. Signal hierarchy is mapped with attention to trust and source context.
7. Leverage concentration is assessed clearly.
8. Intervention implications remain analytical rather than drifting into product planning.
9. Evidence and inference are distinguished where needed.
10. The document gives later strategic work a realistic picture of how movement happens inside the ecosystem.

---

## 28. Failure Modes and Misuse Warnings

Common failure modes include:

### 28.1 Channel fetishism
Mistaking a visible channel for a true lever without showing how action actually propagates.

### 28.2 Actor glorification
Assuming famous actors matter most when structural or ritual levers may matter more.

### 28.3 Trigger vagueness
Using broad terms like “social proof” without specifying what kind, from whom, and under what conditions.

### 28.4 Bottleneck blindness
Ignoring the point where leverage fails to convert into adoption or trust.

### 28.5 Overmechanization
Treating the ecosystem like a machine while ignoring cultural or symbolic mediation already surfaced in T1.

### 28.6 Intervention drift
Allowing the document to become a tactics memo rather than a mechanism analysis.

### 28.7 Evidence thinness
Making strong causal claims from single anecdotes or weakly repeated patterns.

## 30. Relationship to Agentic Research Systems

The Lever Architecture should be structured so that agents can help surface recurring movement patterns without mistaking extraction for explanation.

This means:

- fields should separate levers, pathways, triggers, bottlenecks, and signals clearly
- evidence provenance should remain explicit
- inferred mechanisms should be distinguishable from directly observed ones
- the final leverage interpretation should remain reviewable rather than fully automated

An agent may help identify repeating patterns and candidate mechanisms.
The operating system must still judge what truly has leverage.

---

## 31. Versioning Notes

The Lever Architecture Standard should evolve when any of the following occur:

- repeated work reveals missing categories of leverage or bottleneck not captured well by the current structure
- validation outcomes under T15 show that apparent levers were weaker or more conditional than expected
- platform ecosystem work under T17 requires stronger treatment of platform-controlled amplifiers and dampeners
- T19 learning shows that the system overvalued visible signals and undervalued structural chokepoints
- fast-moving ecosystems require stronger integration with T7 temporal rhythms and T16 refresh protocols

Version updates should record what leverage-intelligence weakness prompted the revision.

---

## 32. Final Standard Statement

Every ecosystem has mechanisms that matter more than they first appear. Some create compounding movement. Others quietly block it.

The Lever Architecture exists to identify those mechanisms so that later strategic work can act where force actually exists instead of where motion merely seems visible.

Without T2, the operating system can describe the ecosystem.
With T2, it can see what actually moves it.
