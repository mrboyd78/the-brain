# T1 — Culture Map Standard

---

## 1. Purpose

The **Culture Map** is the authoritative cultural-intelligence artifact for the Market-Intelligence Operating System.

Its function is to identify and map the tribal identity, belief structure, rituals, status architecture, symbolic vocabulary, and internal factional dynamics of the ecosystem under analysis.

This document exists because markets are not merely transactional environments. They are social worlds with narratives, antagonisms, sacred beliefs, internal hierarchies, and belonging signals. Participants do not only buy because a need exists. They also respond to whether a message, offer, actor, or brand feels native to the culture they inhabit.

T1 exists to make those cultural mechanics explicit so that downstream strategy is not built on demographic abstraction alone.

---

## 2. Strategic Role in the Operating System

The Ecosystem Analysis identifies the terrain: participants, structural forces, commercial mechanics, and interface tensions. T1 deepens that understanding by asking how the people inside that terrain interpret themselves and one another.

T1 serves eight strategic roles:

1. **Identity decoding** — reveals how the ecosystem defines belonging.
2. **Belief mapping** — identifies the convictions that govern participation.
3. **Status interpretation** — explains how attention, legitimacy, and influence are distributed socially.
4. **Narrative alignment** — gives downstream strategy a culturally native frame.
5. **Taboo avoidance** — surfaces sacred and profane boundaries that shape trust or backlash.
6. **Faction detection** — distinguishes the broader ecosystem from its sub-tribes.
7. **Trajectory awareness** — shows how the culture is evolving over time.
8. **Communication grounding** — provides the foundation for T4 Language & Semiotics and any later messaging, positioning, or trust work.

T1 is therefore the operating system’s primary cultural-intelligence layer.

---

## 3. What This Document Is

This document is:

- a structured map of ecosystem culture
- a tribal identity analysis
- a belief and ritual architecture document
- a social-hierarchy and faction map
- a downstream input to language, trust, positioning, and messaging work

---

## 4. What This Document Is Not

The Culture Map is **not**:

- a generic audience persona
- a psychographic summary detached from ecosystem evidence
- a branding brainstorm
- a product brief
- a social-media trend memo
- a moral judgment of the culture under study

It should not flatten culture into stereotypes or abstract marketing demographics.

---

## 5. When to Commission or Use It

T1 should be commissioned when the Ecosystem Analysis has identified a viable ecosystem and there is a need to understand how participants inside that ecosystem construct meaning, status, legitimacy, and belonging.

It becomes mandatory whenever any of the following are true:

- language, messaging, or brand posture decisions are imminent
- T4 Language & Semiotics will be produced
- trust or credibility design work will depend on cultural fit
- the ecosystem appears identity-driven, factionalized, or culturally self-conscious
- outsider status or tone-deaf positioning would materially weaken entry

T1 may be produced early in the Intelligence layer because it depends only on the Ecosystem Analysis and later enriches multiple downstream templates.

---

## 6. Mandatory vs Conditional Use

**Mandatory** for:

- ecosystems with strong identity, ideology, or community behavior
- any effort where messaging, trust, positioning, or community-native design matters materially
- any case where T4 will be generated
- any strategy that depends on sounding culturally native rather than merely correct

**Conditionally recommended** for:

- highly transactional ecosystems with weak visible culture
- infrastructure markets where culture is thin but still non-zero
- early scans where deeper cultural fit questions are not yet decision-relevant

Where cultural misalignment could block adoption, T1 should be treated as required.

## 11. Core Analytical Questions

T1 should allow a reviewer to answer the following questions quickly:

1. What shared narrative explains why this ecosystem exists?
2. What does the community believe to be true that outsiders often miss?
3. What symbols, figures, artifacts, or references carry disproportionate weight?
4. What rituals reinforce belonging and identity?
5. What is treated as sacred, and what is treated as profane?
6. How is status earned, distributed, and lost?
7. What factions or sub-tribes exist inside the broader culture?
8. Where do those sub-tribes align or fight?
9. How is the culture changing over time?
10. What would make an entrant feel native rather than foreign?

---

## 12. Required Structure of the Document

Every Culture Map must contain the following eight sections.

### 12.1 Section 1 — The Creation Story
**Governing question:** What shared narrative explains why this ecosystem exists and why participation in it is meaningful?

This section must identify:

- the grievance narrative
- the catalyst moment
- the movement logic
- the implicit future promise if the culture “wins”

The creation story must be stated in the community’s own framing rather than imposed by the analyst.

### 12.2 Section 2 — The Creed
**Governing question:** What does this community believe to be true that outsiders may not?

This section must list 5–10 core beliefs stated as declarative sentences in the community’s own voice.

For each belief, note:

- how widely held it is
- how intensely held it is
- what contradicting it signals socially

### 12.3 Section 3 — The Icons
**Governing question:** What people, symbols, artefacts, or references carry disproportionate cultural weight?

This section must catalogue both positive and negative icons.

For each icon, document:

- what it symbolizes
- how it is used in communication
- whether its meaning is stable or evolving

### 12.4 Section 4 — The Rituals
**Governing question:** What recurring behaviours reinforce group identity and membership?

This section must catalogue daily, weekly, seasonal, and milestone rituals.

For each ritual, document:

- what triggers it
- what participants do
- what the social reward is
- how the culture treats non-participation
- whether the ritual is organic or manufactured

### 12.5 Section 5 — The Sacred and the Profane
**Governing question:** What topics, behaviours, or positions are untouchable — either revered or forbidden?

This section must identify:

- sacred topics, practices, figures, or values
- profane positions, associations, or behaviors
- any shifting boundaries where sacred items are becoming contested or profane items are being rehabilitated

### 12.6 Section 6 — The Social Hierarchy
**Governing question:** How is status distributed, earned, and maintained within the ecosystem?

This section must map:

- hierarchy tiers
- status currencies
- mobility mechanics
- status threats and common causes of social decline

### 12.7 Section 7 — The Sub-Tribes
**Governing question:** What distinct factions exist within the broader culture, and where do they diverge?

This section must identify 3–7 sub-tribes where such distinctions meaningfully exist.

For each sub-tribe, document:

- what distinguishes it
- where it diverges from the broader culture
- where it congregates
- how membership is signaled
- how it relates to other factions

### 12.8 Section 8 — Cultural Trajectory
**Governing question:** How is this culture evolving, and where is it heading?

This section must assess:

- current lifecycle position
- major cultural shifts under way
- external forces shaping evolution
- whether the culture is embracing, resisting, or ignoring change

## 14. Creation Story Standard

This section must treat the ecosystem as a meaning structure, not just a market.

The analysis should identify:

- what was “wrong before” in the community’s telling
- what event or realization made the community self-aware
- what social or cultural future participants believe they are moving toward

The creation story should be derived from community expression rather than imposed from industry commentary.

---

## 15. Creed Standard

The creed is the membership layer of the culture.

Beliefs included here should not be trivial preferences. They should be convictions that:

- shape in-group belonging
- distinguish insiders from outsiders
- influence how messages, products, or institutions are judged

Each belief should be stated in culturally native phrasing where possible.

---

## 16. Icons Standard

Icons function as compressed meaning inside the ecosystem.

They may include:

- founders or prominent creators
- villains or cautionary figures
- symbolic tools or artifacts
- memes, slogans, or recurring references
- events that operate as shorthand for shared values or warnings

The analysis should distinguish literal importance from symbolic importance.

---

## 17. Rituals Standard

Rituals should be treated as social reinforcement mechanisms rather than mere behaviors.

This section should identify:

- what rituals communicate publicly
- what rewards participants get from performing them
- what non-participation implies
- whether rituals emerged organically or were platform-shaped

Where possible, rituals should be categorized by cadence: daily, weekly, seasonal, and milestone.

---

## 18. Sacred and Profane Standard

This section is a boundary map of the culture.

Sacred items are those the culture defends reflexively.
Profane items are those that trigger rejection, distrust, ridicule, or moral distancing.

This section matters because many entry failures are not failures of utility but failures of boundary recognition.

---

## 19. Social Hierarchy Standard

The culture’s status architecture must be made explicit.

This includes:

- who receives attention and legitimacy
- what currencies matter socially
- how someone rises or falls inside the ecosystem
- whether gatekeepers or credentialing structures exist

Status should be treated as a real strategic variable, not a soft cultural aside.

---

## 20. Sub-Tribes Standard

This section must not assume the ecosystem is culturally uniform.

Where meaningful internal factions exist, the document should identify:

- where their beliefs diverge
- what antagonisms or alliances exist
- whether bridge figures connect them
- which factions are most strategically central, rising, or volatile

Over-fragmentation should be avoided. Factions should only be separated where the distinction materially affects communication, trust, or entry logic.

---

## 21. Cultural Trajectory Standard

The culture must be positioned as dynamic rather than fixed.

Recommended lifecycle categories:

- emergent
- growth
- mainstream adoption
- institutionalisation
- fragmentation

The section should explain what is changing, why it is changing, and how the culture feels about that change.

---

## 22. Research Methodology Standard

The Culture Map should follow a disciplined immersion-based methodology.

Recommended minimum protocol:

### 22.1 Immersion Scan
Spend sustained time consuming native ecosystem content across the primary platforms where the culture expresses itself.

### 22.2 Creator Content Audit
Review content across status tiers, not only top voices.

### 22.3 Community Mining
Read high-engagement community discussions, especially those with emotional intensity, conflict, or strong consensus signals.

### 22.4 Counter-Culture Scan
Review critics, defectors, or ex-members to understand pressure points, hypocrisy claims, and blind spots.

The methodology should prioritize direct cultural self-expression over outsider description.

---

## 23. Source and Evidence Expectations

The Culture Map must remain evidence-based even though it is culturally interpretive.

Source expectations:

- at least 60% of observations should come from primary community content where feasible
- community-native phrasing should be preserved where analytically useful
- outsider summaries should not dominate the analysis of insider culture
- any unsupported interpretation should be flagged as an inference rather than stated as fact

Primary community expression is the preferred source class for this template.

---

## 24. Length Calibration

Recommended target length: **3,500–5,500 words**.  
Maximum: **7,000 words**.

If the document materially exceeds the maximum, the sub-tribe analysis may be over-fragmented and should be consolidated where appropriate.

---

## 25. Required Usage Rules

The following rules are mandatory.

### Rule 1 — No product contamination
T1 may not contain product names, feature lists, or build plans.

### Rule 2 — No outsider-only framing
The creation story, creed, and symbolic map must be grounded in community expression, not only external commentary.

### Rule 3 — No stereotype compression
Cultural patterns must be ecosystem-specific and evidence-based.

### Rule 4 — No flattening of hierarchy
Status structure must be analyzed explicitly where it exists.

### Rule 5 — No taboo blindness
Sacred and profane boundaries must be surfaced where they materially shape entry risk or trust.

### Rule 6 — No static-culture assumption
The culture’s trajectory must be assessed, not ignored.

### Rule 7 — No faction inflation without meaning
Sub-tribes should only be separated where the distinction matters strategically.

---

## 26. Recommended Operating Procedure

### Step 1 — Anchor in the Ecosystem Analysis
Use the participant map and social-lever logic as the starting frame.

### Step 2 — Conduct immersion
Consume native content, comments, and discussions until recurring symbolic patterns become visible.

### Step 3 — Extract the cultural core
Identify the creation story, creed, icons, rituals, and taboo boundaries.

### Step 4 — Map status and factions
Determine how legitimacy is distributed and where internal divergence exists.

### Step 5 — Assess trajectory
Place the culture on a lifecycle and explain what is changing.

### Step 6 — Translate into structured intelligence
Produce the eight-section document in a form that can feed T4, T6, and later strategic work.

---

## 27. Quality Standard / Acceptance Criteria

The Culture Map should be considered acceptable only if all of the following are true:

1. The culture’s creation story is clearly articulated.
2. The core creed is expressed in the community’s own logic.
3. Positive and negative icons are identified.
4. Rituals are mapped with their social function, not merely listed.
5. Sacred and profane boundaries are explicit.
6. The social hierarchy is mapped clearly.
7. Sub-tribes are identified where strategically meaningful.
8. The culture’s trajectory is assessed.
9. Community-native evidence outweighs outsider description.
10. The document provides actionable cultural intelligence without collapsing into branding advice or product ideation.

---

## 28. Failure Modes and Misuse Warnings

Common failure modes include:

### 28.1 Stereotype drift
Reducing the ecosystem to clichés rather than observed cultural patterns.

### 28.2 Outsider overreach
Using journalistic or analyst framing in place of the community’s own language.

### 28.3 Symbol blindness
Missing icons, rituals, or taboos that materially shape belonging.

### 28.4 Status blindness
Ignoring how hierarchy and legitimacy actually work.

### 28.5 Over-fragmentation
Inventing excessive sub-tribes where the strategic difference is trivial.

### 28.6 Product leakage
Using T1 to smuggle brand or feature decisions into what should remain cultural analysis.

### 28.7 Static reading
Treating culture as fixed when it is visibly evolving.

## 30. Relationship to Agentic Research Systems

The Culture Map should be structured so that agents can assist with gathering and organizing cultural material without reducing cultural interpretation to keyword extraction.

This means:

- fields should separate belief, ritual, icon, taboo, hierarchy, and faction data clearly
- source provenance should remain explicit
- community phrasing should be preserved where analytically useful
- the final cultural interpretation should remain reviewable rather than fully automated

An agent may help surface patterns.
The operating system must still judge what those patterns mean culturally.

---

## 31. Versioning Notes

The Culture Map Standard should evolve when any of the following occur:

- repeated work reveals missing cultural dimensions that recur across ecosystems
- faction mapping consistently proves too coarse or too granular
- trust or language work exposes gaps in how T1 captures symbolic meaning
- outcome learning under T19 shows that cultural misreads were not detectable early enough
- fast-changing communities require stronger trajectory and refresh integration

Version updates should record what cultural-intelligence weakness prompted the revision.

---

## 32. Final Standard Statement

An ecosystem is not only a structure of money and incentives. It is also a structure of meaning.

The Culture Map exists to reveal the beliefs, boundaries, symbols, rituals, and hierarchies that make that meaning legible. It is the difference between seeing a market as a set of transactions and seeing it as a living social world.

Without T1, the operating system can describe the terrain.
With T1, it can understand how the people on that terrain understand themselves.
