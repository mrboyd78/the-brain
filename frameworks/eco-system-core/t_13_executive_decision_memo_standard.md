# T13 — Executive Decision Memo Standard

## Document Status
**System:** Market-Intelligence Operating System  
**Layer:** Companion Decision Layer  
**Designation:** T13  
**Document Type:** Standard  
**Status:** Canonical Draft  
**Purpose Class:** Decision Compression / Executive Briefing / Strategic Gating

---

## 1. Purpose

The **Executive Decision Memo** is the authoritative decision-compression artifact for the Market-Intelligence Operating System.

Its function is to translate the full analytical stack into a concise, decision-grade briefing that enables a founder, operator, investor, strategist, or leadership group to make a clear next decision without rereading the entire research corpus.

The memo exists to answer a practical problem: rigorous analysis often becomes too large, too distributed, or too conceptually dense to drive timely action. The Executive Decision Memo preserves rigor while compressing complexity.

Its purpose is not to simplify the market into slogans. Its purpose is to make the consequences of the analysis legible at the point of decision.

---

## 2. Strategic Role in the Operating System

The Market-Intelligence Operating System is designed to move from terrain understanding to deeper intelligence to strategic entry logic. That produces substantial analytical depth, but depth alone does not create an actionable decision.

The Executive Decision Memo serves seven strategic roles:

1. **Compression** — reduces the full research stack into the smallest form that still preserves decision integrity.
2. **Decision clarity** — defines the actual decision being asked for.
3. **Strategic gating** — determines whether the system is recommending advance, defer, redirect, or reject.
4. **Uncertainty preservation** — carries forward critical assumptions, contradictions, and no-go conditions rather than stripping them away.
5. **Prioritization** — makes the most important findings and vectors visible in ranked form.
6. **Alignment** — ensures different readers are reacting to the same distilled judgment.
7. **Handoff** — provides the clean bridge from analysis into concept conversion, validation, or portfolio allocation.

This document is therefore a strategic decision instrument, not a substitute for the full analysis.

---

## 3. What This Document Is

This document is:

- a decision-grade summary of the analytical stack
- a structured recommendation memo
- a gating mechanism between analysis and next action
- a compression artifact that preserves uncertainty and evidence quality
- a boardroom-usable strategic brief

---

## 4. What This Document Is Not

The Executive Decision Memo is **not**:

- a generic summary
- a pitch deck in prose form
- a motivational narrative
- a replacement for EA or T1–T10
- a place to introduce brand-new analysis
- a rhetorical sales document designed to force consensus
- a product brief

It should not create false certainty by omitting constraints, caveats, or evidence quality issues.

---

## 5. When to Commission or Use It

The memo should be created when the analytical work has matured enough to support a real decision, and before the organization commits to concept generation, validation investment, platform pursuit, or broader strategic allocation.

It becomes mandatory whenever any of the following are true:

- a go / no-go decision is imminent
- an ecosystem or platform must be prioritized against alternatives
- T10 has produced ranked entry vectors
- a concept-conversion effort is being considered
- leadership needs a decision artifact rather than a research archive
- the analysis will influence capital, product, partnership, or market-entry commitments

The memo should not be drafted too early. Compression before the analysis is mature will degrade judgment.

---

## 6. Mandatory vs Conditional Use

**Mandatory** for:

- ecosystem decisions that influence product or capital allocation
- post-T10 strategic review
- selection among multiple vectors, platforms, or ecosystems
- handoff into T14 Vector-to-Concept Conversion
- portfolio prioritization using T18

**Conditionally recommended** for:

- smaller internal reviews where the full analytical stack is already shared and decision-makers are close to the work
- exploratory work that is still too immature for recommendation

Where stakes are meaningful, T13 should be treated as required.

## 8. Outputs Produced

The memo produces the following outputs:

- a clearly defined decision statement
- a bounded recommendation
- ranked strategic options or vectors
- the decisive reasons supporting the recommendation
- critical assumptions and blocking conditions
- no-go criteria
- next action pathway
- an executive-level record of what the system was recommending at that time

These outputs should enable movement without severing the link to the deeper research base.

## 9. Position in Sequencing Logic

T13 should be produced after evidence and analysis are mature, after assumptions have been surfaced in T12, and before concept conversion or validation planning proceeds.

Recommended sequence:

1. scope defined
2. T11 Evidence Ledger active
3. EA and selective T1–T10 completed to decision-ready level
4. T12 Assumption & Unknowns Register populated
5. **T13 Executive Decision Memo prepared**
6. decision gate reached
7. if advancing, T14 Vector-to-Concept Conversion begins
8. if validation needed, T15 is commissioned
9. if not advancing, the memo records defer / redirect / reject logic for future reference

T13 is the operating system’s primary decision gate artifact.

## 11. Core Analytical Questions

The memo should allow an executive reader to answer the following questions quickly:

1. What decision is being asked for?
2. What is the recommendation?
3. Why is that recommendation justified?
4. What alternatives were considered and why were they not selected?
5. What assumptions or unknowns could overturn the recommendation?
6. What no-go conditions would force a pause or rejection?
7. How time-sensitive is this decision?
8. What should happen next if the recommendation is accepted?
9. What should happen next if the recommendation is not accepted?
10. How confident should the decision-maker be in this recommendation?

## 12. Required Structure of the Memo

The Executive Decision Memo should contain the following core sections.

### 12.1 Header
- ecosystem / project name
- memo version
- date
- owner / author
- decision horizon
- status

### 12.2 Decision Statement
A precise statement of the decision being requested.

### 12.3 Recommendation
A concise statement of the recommended path.

### 12.4 Why This Recommendation
A compressed explanation of the decisive findings and strategic logic.

### 12.5 Options Considered
A ranked set of alternatives, including why the selected option outranks them.

### 12.6 Critical Assumptions and Unknowns
A focused carry-forward of the most important T12 items.

### 12.7 Risks, Constraints, and No-Go Conditions
The conditions under which the recommendation should not proceed.

### 12.8 Timing Considerations
A statement of urgency, sequencing, windows, or reasons to defer.

### 12.9 Recommended Next Step
The immediate next action if the decision is accepted.

### 12.10 Evidence and Confidence Note
A concise statement of evidence quality, freshness, and confidence boundary.

### 12.11 Appendices or References
Pointers back to the deeper documents for audit or deeper review.

## 14. Decision Category Framework

Each memo must state what kind of decision it is asking for.

Recommended categories:

- **Advance** — proceed into the next structured step
- **Defer** — hold due to timing, evidence, or constraint conditions
- **Redirect** — change ecosystem, vector, segment, or interface focus
- **Reject** — do not proceed
- **Compare further** — additional targeted analysis is needed before selection

The category should be explicit.

---

## 15. Recommendation Logic Standard

A strong recommendation must state not only what to do, but why the selected path outranks alternatives.

This requires the memo to distinguish between:

- decisive reasons
- supporting reasons
- contextual reasons
- unresolved reasons that remain under uncertainty

The recommendation should be based on the strongest strategic logic revealed by the full stack, not on what sounds simplest or most emotionally appealing.

---

## 16. Options Considered Standard

The memo must show that alternatives were considered in a disciplined way.

Recommended fields for each option:

- option name
- rank
- strategic attractiveness
- access feasibility
- trust difficulty
- timing fit
- capital capture logic
- key reason not selected

The memo should not pretend the chosen recommendation was the only conceivable path.

---

## 17. Critical Assumptions and Unknowns Standard

The memo must include only the most decision-relevant assumptions and unknowns from T12.

These should be selected based on:

- ability to overturn the recommendation
- relevance to next-step execution
- severity or blocking status
- closeness to irreversible commitment

The memo should not reproduce the entire T12 register.
It should carry forward only the assumptions that matter most at decision time.

---

## 18. No-Go Conditions Standard

The memo must explicitly state the conditions under which the recommendation should not proceed.

Recommended no-go condition categories:

- platform access failure
- evidence deterioration or contradictory update
- monetization logic too speculative
- trust barrier too severe
- timing window no longer favorable
- validation threshold not met
- key assumption fails or remains unresolvable
- execution burden exceeds viable capacity

This prevents the memo from functioning as a one-way advocacy device.

---

## 19. Timing Considerations Standard

The memo must address timing directly.

Recommended timing questions:

- Is this a now decision, a later decision, or a monitor-only situation?
- What creates urgency?
- What creates reason to wait?
- What decays if the decision is delayed?
- What may improve if the decision is delayed?

Timing should reflect T7 and T16 where relevant, but in compressed form.

---

## 20. Confidence Handling

The memo must not collapse evidence quality, freshness, and assumption burden into vague confidence language.

Recommended confidence framing:

- **High confidence recommendation** — strong evidence, manageable uncertainty, low contradiction burden
- **Moderate confidence recommendation** — good directional basis, but important uncertainties remain
- **Provisional recommendation** — a path is favored, but major assumptions or time-sensitive evidence require caution
- **Low confidence recommendation** — not ready for commitment; likely defer, compare further, or reject

Confidence should reflect evidentiary condition, not executive appetite.

---

## 21. Evidence Note Standard

The memo must include a short evidence note covering:

- overall evidence strength
- notable freshness risks
- presence of unresolved contradictions
- whether critical conclusions are heavily inferential

This ensures that the memo remains connected to T11 rather than operating as pure narrative.

---

## 22. Recommended Next-Step Standard

The memo must end with a clear immediate next step.

Recommended next-step pathways:

- commission T14 for a selected vector
- commission T15 for validation on a constrained concept or assumption set
- perform targeted additional research on one blocking issue
- move into portfolio comparison via T18
- defer and monitor under T16
- close the opportunity and archive under reject logic

The memo should not end in analytical ambiguity.
It must specify what operational move follows from the recommendation.

---

## 23. Required Usage Rules

The following rules are mandatory.

### Rule 1 — One memo, one real decision
The memo must state the exact decision being requested.

### Rule 2 — No false compression
Do not omit critical assumptions, contradictions, or no-go conditions in the name of brevity.

### Rule 3 — No new analysis smuggled into the memo
The memo compresses existing analysis. It does not invent conclusions on the fly.

### Rule 4 — No advocacy masquerading as judgment
The memo should not be written to force a predetermined answer.

### Rule 5 — No unsupported ranking language
If an option is ranked first, the strategic basis for that ranking must be visible.

### Rule 6 — No next-step ambiguity
The memo must indicate what should happen immediately if the decision is accepted.

### Rule 7 — No uncertainty stripping
T12 must be reflected honestly.

---

## 24. Recommended Operating Procedure

### Step 1 — Define the exact decision
Clarify whether the memo is about ecosystem selection, vector selection, platform access, timing, concept advance, or rejection.

### Step 2 — Extract the decisive findings
Identify the smallest set of analytical conclusions that materially determine the recommendation.

### Step 3 — Rank the options
Show what alternatives were considered and how they compare.

### Step 4 — Pull critical assumptions from T12
Carry forward only the items that matter at this decision gate.

### Step 5 — Define no-go conditions
State what would make the recommendation invalid or unsafe to pursue.

### Step 6 — Write the recommendation and next step
Make the decision and the immediate operational implication explicit.

### Step 7 — Attach evidence and confidence note
Ensure the memo remains grounded in T11 and bounded by T12.

### Step 8 — Use the memo as the decision record
Archive it as the formal statement of what was recommended and why.

---

## 25. Quality Standard / Acceptance Criteria

The Executive Decision Memo should be considered acceptable only if all of the following are true:

1. The decision being requested is explicit.
2. The recommendation is clear and bounded.
3. The decisive reasons are visible and traceable to the broader analytical stack.
4. Relevant alternatives were considered and ranked.
5. Critical assumptions and blocking conditions are visible.
6. Timing and urgency are addressed directly.
7. The next action is operationally clear.
8. The memo preserves evidence quality and confidence boundaries.
9. A decision-maker can act from the memo without being misled about uncertainty.

---

## 26. Failure Modes and Misuse Warnings

Common failure modes include:

### 26.1 Summary without decision
A compact recap of analysis is not a decision memo if it never states what must be decided.

### 26.2 Advocacy drift
If the memo is written to “sell” one option rather than judge it, strategic discipline degrades.

### 26.3 Uncertainty laundering
Stripping out assumptions and contradictions to make the memo sound cleaner makes the system less trustworthy.

### 26.4 Overcompression
If important distinctions between options are lost, the memo may create a false impression of clarity.

### 26.5 No-go omission
A recommendation without stop conditions is incomplete.

### 26.6 Next-step blur
If the memo ends with vague language rather than a clear downstream action, it fails as a gating artifact.

### 26.7 Executive sanitization
If the memo becomes polished beyond usefulness and ceases to reflect actual evidence quality, it has become theater.

---

## 27. Source and Evidence Expectations

The memo should not cite every underlying source directly, but it must be tethered to T11 and T12.

At minimum it should reference:

- the analytical documents synthesized
- the evidence quality condition
- the critical assumption set
- any unresolved contradiction that could materially affect the recommendation

The memo is not a substitute for evidence traceability.
It is a compression layer built on top of evidence governance.

## 29. Relationship to Agentic Research Systems

The Executive Decision Memo should be designed so that agents can draft it, but not so that judgment is surrendered to automation.

This means:

- decision categories should be standardized
- options and ranks should be discrete and comparable
- assumption IDs and no-go conditions should be stable references
- evidence and confidence notes should be drawn from governed upstream artifacts
- the final recommendation should remain reviewable by a human decision-maker

An agent may compress the stack.
The operating system must still govern whether the compression is faithful.

---

## 30. Versioning Notes

The Executive Decision Memo Standard should evolve when any of the following occur:

- decision-makers repeatedly misread the memo’s level of confidence
- important no-go conditions are routinely omitted
- memo structure proves too long or too thin for actual strategic use
- portfolio comparison requires tighter normalization across memos
- learning capture shows that decisive reasons were poorly chosen or misleadingly ranked
- post-T10 workflows require a more explicit bridge into T14 and T15

Version updates should record what decision-quality weakness prompted the change.

---

## 31. Final Standard Statement

A rigorous market-intelligence system needs more than analysis. It needs a disciplined way to decide.

The Executive Decision Memo exists to compress a deep research stack into a form that supports action without amputating uncertainty, evidence quality, or strategic nuance.

Without T13, the operating system can produce insight.
With T13, it can produce decision-ready judgment.
