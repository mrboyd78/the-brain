# T14 — Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief Standard

## Document Status
**System:** Market-Intelligence Operating System  
**Layer:** Companion Conversion Layer  
**Designation:** T14  
**Document Type:** Standard  
**Status:** Canonical Draft  
**Purpose Class:** Strategy-to-Concept Translation / Opportunity Structuring / Post-T10 Conversion Control

---

## 1. Purpose

The **Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief** is the authoritative translation artifact for converting a selected strategic entry vector into one or more bounded concept candidates.

Its function is to ensure that the operating system does not jump directly from market strategy into loose ideation, feature lists, or product enthusiasm. Instead, it creates a disciplined bridge from strategic logic to concept formation.

The brief exists to answer a recurring problem: a strong strategic vector often degrades once teams begin “brainstorming the product.” The original market logic becomes diluted, overwritten by preference, or replaced by generic solutioning. T14 prevents that drift.

Its purpose is to preserve fidelity between:

- the market forces identified upstream
- the entry vector selected in T10 / T13
- the concept candidates explored downstream

---

## 2. Strategic Role in the Operating System

The Market-Intelligence Operating System is designed to stop at ranked strategic entry vectors before build-stage product thinking begins. That discipline is necessary, but it creates a handoff challenge: once the system has identified where to enter, someone still has to define what to build or offer.

T14 serves eight strategic roles:

1. **Translation** — converts strategic vectors into concept-ready form.
2. **Lineage preservation** — ensures concepts can be traced back to strategic rationale.
3. **Boundary setting** — prevents concept sprawl and premature feature inflation.
4. **Constraint carrying** — preserves trust, timing, platform, monetization, and access realities identified upstream.
5. **Variant generation** — allows multiple concept pathways to emerge from a single vector without losing discipline.
6. **Validation readiness** — structures concepts so they can flow into T15.
7. **Strategic coherence** — keeps concept logic aligned with the ecosystem rather than internal preference.
8. **Decision accountability** — creates a record of how a strategic judgment became an actionable concept candidate.

T14 is therefore the operating system’s controlled translation layer between strategy and validation.

## 3. What This Document Is

This document is:

- a conversion brief from vector to concept
- a strategic translation artifact
- a constraint-preserving handoff document
- a bounded concept-formation standard
- a precursor to structured validation

---

## 4. What This Document Is Not

The Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief is **not**:

- a freeform product brainstorm
- a feature backlog
- a PRD
- a design brief for full implementation
- a founder vision note detached from market logic
- a marketing narrative written before the concept is validated
- a replacement for T10 or T13

It should not be used to smuggle ad hoc ideas into the pipeline without strategic lineage.

---

## 5. When to Commission or Use It

T14 should be commissioned after a strategic vector, ecosystem pathway, or ranked option has been selected for advancement and before full validation planning or product specification begins.

It becomes mandatory whenever any of the following are true:

- T10 has produced a selected vector worth exploring further
- T13 has recommended advance on a strategic path
- one or more concepts must be derived from a market opportunity rather than invented from scratch
- the organization wants concept candidates that remain faithful to upstream analysis
- T15 validation planning is about to begin

T14 should not be generated before strategic selection is mature.

Premature conversion invites incoherent concept formation.

---

## 6. Mandatory vs Conditional Use

**Mandatory** for:

- any effort moving from T10 / T13 into concept exploration
- any concept path intended to feed T15 validation
- any ecosystem opportunity where platform access, trust, monetization, or timing constraints materially shape concept design
- any multi-concept comparison effort derived from a single vector

**Conditionally recommended** for:

- very early idea generation where no strategic vector has yet been selected
- low-stakes creative exploration not yet tied to the operating system’s formal decision path

Where concept work is meant to remain disciplined, T14 should be treated as required.

## 7. Inputs Required

The Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief requires the following inputs:

### 7.1 Strategic Inputs
- T10 ranked strategic entry vectors
- T13 Executive Decision Memo
- selected ecosystem, platform, interface, or segment pathway
- core leverage logic from upstream analysis

### 7.2 Analytical Inputs
- relevant EA conclusions
- relevant T1–T9 findings, especially trust, timing, risk, capital flow, participant decision structure, and competitive topology
- force dynamics and friction points being targeted

### 7.3 Governance Inputs
- T11 Evidence Ledger
- T12 Assumption & Unknowns Register
- decision-critical assumptions and blocking conditions
- unresolved contradictions or platform constraints

### 7.4 Operating Inputs
- resource context
- intended validation scope
- acceptable concept complexity at this stage
- whether the path is commercial software, service layer, workflow tool, ecosystem extension, or other form

## 8. Outputs Produced

T14 produces the following outputs:

- a clearly stated source vector and its market rationale
- one or more bounded concept candidates derived from that vector
- concept logic tied to ecosystem forces and participant needs
- constraints that must govern concept shape
- explicit value hypotheses
- trust and access requirements carried into concept design
- initial monetization logic
- a structured bridge into T15 validation planning

These outputs should preserve strategic clarity while making controlled concept generation possible.

---

## 9. Position in Sequencing Logic

T14 should be produced after strategic selection and before validation design or full product specification.

Recommended sequence:

1. scope defined
2. T11 active
3. EA and selective T1–T10 completed
4. T12 uncertainty surfaced
5. T13 decision memo selects or advances a vector
6. **T14 Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief produced**
7. concept candidates assessed for coherence and constraint fit
8. T15 validation program designed against the chosen concept pathway and assumptions
9. only after validation should fuller product specification proceed

T14 therefore functions as the disciplined bridge between strategy and pre-build experimentation.

## 10. Relationship to Other Documents

### Upstream Relationships
T14 depends especially on:

- **T10** for the selected entry vector
- **T13** for the decision logic that authorized movement
- **Ecosystem Analysis** and **T1–T9** for the structural and strategic rationale behind the vector
- **T11** for evidence grounding
- **T12** for assumption visibility and boundary discipline

### Downstream Relationships
T14 directly supports:

- **T15 Validation & Experiment Design Standard** by structuring what exactly will be tested
- implementation-stage product briefs or PRDs by providing a strategically bounded concept frame
- **T19 Learning Capture Memo** by documenting how the concept originated and why
- portfolio comparison or opportunity review when multiple concepts are being generated from different vectors

## 11. Core Analytical Questions

The brief should allow a reviewer to answer the following questions quickly:

1. What strategic vector is this concept derived from?
2. Why is that vector attractive in the first place?
3. What force, friction, or leverage point is the concept meant to exploit or resolve?
4. Which participant, segment, interface, or platform is it targeting?
5. What are the viable concept variants that remain faithful to the vector?
6. What value hypothesis does each concept candidate embody?
7. What constraints must shape the concept from the beginning?
8. Which assumptions are embedded in the concept logic?
9. Why does this concept deserve validation before full specification?
10. How would the concept fail if the vector-to-concept translation is wrong?

## 12. Required Structure of the Brief

The Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief should contain the following core sections.

### 12.1 Header
- ecosystem / project name
- brief version
- date
- owner / author
- source vector ID
- status

### 12.2 Source Vector Summary
A concise explanation of the selected vector and why it was chosen.

### 12.3 Conversion Objective
A statement of what the concept generation effort is trying to produce.

### 12.4 Force / Friction Target
The specific ecosystem pressure, participant friction, or leverage point the concept is meant to address.

### 12.5 Target Participant / Segment / Interface
The audience and interaction surface being targeted.

### 12.6 Concept Translation Logic
A disciplined explanation of how the vector becomes a concept candidate.

### 12.7 Concept Candidates
One or more bounded candidate concepts derived from the same vector.

### 12.8 Value Hypotheses
The specific value claims each candidate is making.

### 12.9 Constraint Set
The constraints that must govern concept formation.

### 12.10 Embedded Assumptions and Risks
The most relevant T12 assumptions and failure exposures carried into the concept.

### 12.11 Initial Monetization and Access Logic
A first-pass view of how the concept could capture value and whether it can access the market surface required.

### 12.12 Recommended Validation Direction
A statement of which concept candidate or hypothesis should move into T15.

### 12.13 Supporting References
Pointers to T10, T13, T11, T12, and relevant upstream analytical documents.

## 14. Source Vector Summary Standard

The brief must begin with a precise description of the selected vector.

This should include:

- the vector itself
- why it outranked alternatives
- the strategic logic underneath it
- what ecosystem condition makes it attractive now
- what forms of value capture it implies

The brief should never assume the reader remembers the T10 logic.
It must restate enough of the vector to make the conversion intelligible.

---

## 15. Conversion Objective Standard

Each T14 brief must state what kind of concept generation is occurring.

Recommended objective categories:

- generate one concept candidate for focused validation
- generate multiple bounded variants from one vector
- test different delivery forms for the same value logic
- compare platform-native versus platform-adjacent concept expressions
- define a minimum viable concept for assumption testing

Without a clear conversion objective, T14 risks becoming unfocused ideation.

---

## 16. Force / Friction Target Standard

The brief must specify the market pressure being targeted.

Recommended categories:

- participant pain or coordination friction
- trust deficit
- timing mismatch
- access bottleneck
- workflow inefficiency
- monetization leakage
- platform gap
- adoption barrier
- decision burden
- capital or attention misallocation

This section is essential because concepts should be shaped by real ecosystem pressure, not by abstract novelty.

---

## 17. Concept Translation Logic Standard

The brief must explicitly explain how the vector becomes a concept.

This requires the author to state:

- what the vector implies must exist or be delivered
- what form the intervention might take
- why that form is coherent with the ecosystem’s structure
- what forms were rejected and why
- why the candidate concept is a faithful expression of the vector rather than a random product idea

This is one of the most important sections of T14.

It is the safeguard against strategic drift.

---

## 18. Concept Candidate Standard

Each concept candidate should be expressed in bounded form.

At minimum, each candidate should define:

- the primary user or actor
- the job or friction being addressed
- the mechanism of value delivery
- the interface or delivery surface
- the core differentiating logic
- the concept form (tool, extension, workflow layer, analytics layer, service wrapper, marketplace app, etc.)
- what is deliberately excluded at this stage

Candidates should be specific enough to validate and compare, but not yet expanded into full product plans.

---

## 19. Value Hypothesis Standard

Each candidate must include one or more explicit value hypotheses.

Recommended fields:

- value delivered to whom
- why the value matters now
- why existing alternatives underperform
- what outcome would cause the target participant to care
- what behavior or adoption signal would indicate the hypothesis may be true

A concept without a clear value hypothesis is not ready for T15.

---

## 20. Constraint Set Standard

T14 must preserve the constraints identified upstream.

Recommended constraint domains:

- platform access constraints
- policy or review constraints
- trust acquisition constraints
- timing constraints
- monetization constraints
- operational complexity constraints
- required integration constraints
- adoption-friction constraints
- evidence or assumption constraints

Constraints should shape the concept from the beginning rather than being discovered too late.

---

## 21. Embedded Assumptions and Risks Standard

The brief must carry forward only the assumptions and risks that materially shape the concept.

Recommended categories:

- demand assumption
- behavior-change assumption
- access assumption
- monetization assumption
- trust assumption
- implementation / delivery assumption
- validation-design risk

These should be drawn from T12, not invented casually inside T14.

---

## 22. Initial Monetization and Access Logic Standard

T14 should include an early but disciplined view of how the concept could capture value.

This should address:

- who pays or what economic event creates value
- whether the concept fits the platform’s monetization pathways
- whether pricing would likely attach to workflow, compliance, convenience, revenue lift, cost reduction, or some other mechanism
- what access path exists to reach the target user or buyer

This section should remain provisional.
T14 is not the final pricing document.

---

## 23. Recommended Validation Direction Standard

The brief must end with a clear recommendation for what should be tested next.

Recommended outputs:

- select one concept candidate for focused validation
- select two candidates for comparative validation
- select one core assumption for direct testing before concept comparison
- defer concept testing until one blocking issue is resolved

T14 should not terminate in conceptual ambiguity.
It must point toward structured next action.

---

## 24. Required Usage Rules

The following rules are mandatory.

### Rule 1 — No concept without vector lineage
Every concept candidate must be explicitly traceable to a selected vector.

### Rule 2 — No freeform ideation drift
T14 is not an invitation to invent unrelated ideas.

### Rule 3 — No constraint stripping
Platform, trust, access, timing, and monetization realities must remain visible.

### Rule 4 — No feature sprawl
The brief must stay at concept level, not backlog level.

### Rule 5 — No validation handoff without explicit hypotheses
A concept must contain stated value and assumption logic before moving into T15.

### Rule 6 — No pretending all variants are equal
The brief should indicate which candidate or pathway appears strongest and why.

### Rule 7 — No conversion without upstream maturity
T14 should not be used to replace unfinished strategic analysis.

---

## 25. Recommended Operating Procedure

### Step 1 — Confirm the selected vector
Verify that T10 / T13 has clearly identified the path being advanced.

### Step 2 — Restate the strategic logic
Capture the core force, friction, and value-capture rationale behind the vector.

### Step 3 — Define the conversion objective
Clarify whether the goal is one candidate, multiple variants, or comparative concept shaping.

### Step 4 — Generate bounded concept candidates
Develop concept options that remain faithful to the vector and constraint set.

### Step 5 — Extract value hypotheses and embedded assumptions
Define what each candidate is really claiming about the market.

### Step 6 — Carry forward critical constraints
Ensure trust, timing, access, platform, and monetization conditions remain visible.

### Step 7 — Recommend a validation pathway
Identify what should move next into T15.

### Step 8 — Archive the conversion logic
Preserve the document as the formal record of how strategy became concept.

---

## 26. Quality Standard / Acceptance Criteria

The Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief should be considered acceptable only if all of the following are true:

1. The selected vector is clearly identified and explained.
2. Concept candidates are traceable to upstream strategy.
3. The force or friction being targeted is explicit.
4. Concept candidates are bounded and distinct.
5. Value hypotheses are clearly stated.
6. Critical constraints remain visible.
7. Material assumptions and risks are carried forward from T12.
8. The brief provides a clear recommendation for next-step validation.
9. A reviewer can see why the concept exists, not just what it is.

---

## 27. Failure Modes and Misuse Warnings

Common failure modes include:

### 27.1 Ideation masquerading as conversion
If the brief drifts into generic product brainstorming, lineage is lost.

### 27.2 Feature inflation
Adding too much detail too soon makes the concept harder to test and easier to distort.

### 27.3 Strategy amnesia
If the vector rationale is no longer visible, T14 has failed its core function.

### 27.4 Constraint blindness
Ignoring platform, trust, timing, or monetization limits at the concept stage creates downstream waste.

### 27.5 Weak value hypotheses
If the concept cannot clearly state why anyone would care, it is not ready for validation.

### 27.6 False optionality
Producing many variants with no meaningful distinction or ranking creates noise rather than structured choice.

### 27.7 Premature certainty
T14 should shape candidates, not declare product-market fit before testing.

## 28. Source and Evidence Expectations

T14 should be anchored in upstream governed artifacts rather than freehand speculation.

At minimum, the brief should reference:

- the source vector and related T10 logic
- the T13 decision basis
- the T12 assumptions that materially shape the concept
- the T11 evidence condition where relevant
- any platform or policy evidence that constrains delivery form

The brief is a translation layer, not a detachment from evidence discipline.

## 30. Relationship to Agentic Research Systems

The Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief should be designed so that agents can generate disciplined first drafts without dissolving the strategic chain of custody.

This means:

- vector IDs and decision memo IDs should be stable references
- concept candidates should be structured and comparable
- constraint fields should be discrete where practical
- assumptions should link back to T12 IDs
- the recommended validation direction should be explicit enough to hand into T15

An agent may help produce the brief.
The operating system must still enforce fidelity between vector and concept.

---

## 31. Versioning Notes

The Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief Standard should evolve when any of the following occur:

- repeated conversion work shows that concept drift is still occurring
- T15 reveals that concept candidates are arriving too broad or too vague to validate well
- platform ecosystem work requires more explicit access or compliance translation fields
- monetization logic repeatedly appears too late in the concept process
- learning capture shows that the chosen concept form was not actually faithful to the source vector
- multi-concept comparison requires tighter standardization across briefs

Version updates should record which conversion weakness prompted the revision.

---

## 32. Final Standard Statement

A strategic vector is not yet a product concept.
But a product concept that cannot trace its logic back to a strategic vector is already drifting.

The Vector-to-Concept Conversion Brief exists to preserve strategic fidelity while allowing bounded concept formation.

Without T14, the operating system can identify where to enter.
With T14, it can translate that insight into what may deserve to exist.
