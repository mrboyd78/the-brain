# T3 — Participant Decision Architecture Standard

## Document Status

---

## 1. Purpose

The **Participant Decision Architecture** is the authoritative decision-process artifact for the Market-Intelligence Operating System.

Its function is to map how each participant type inside an ecosystem evaluates options, processes information, responds to influence, encounters objections, applies shortcuts, and ultimately commits to action or permanent non-decision.

This document exists because knowing that an ecosystem participant has a need is not the same as knowing how that participant decides. A trigger may create urgency. A culture may define belonging. But the actual path from awareness to commitment still runs through a decision process — and that process is often patterned, social, emotionally burdened, and shortcut-driven.

T3 exists to make that decision architecture explicit so downstream sales design, pricing logic, onboarding, content staging, and objection handling can be grounded in how choices are actually made inside the ecosystem rather than how rational decision theory says they should be made.

---

## 2. Strategic Role in the Operating System

The Ecosystem Analysis identifies participants. T2 identifies what makes them act. T3 explains how they translate prompts, pressures, and information into commitment.

T3 serves nine strategic roles:

1. **Decision pathway mapping** — reveals the sequence participants move through from awareness to commitment.
2. **Information design grounding** — identifies what information matters, in what order, and in what format.
3. **Influence interpretation** — distinguishes who meaningfully shifts a participant’s decision and who merely creates noise.
4. **Timeline realism** — clarifies whether decisions are impulsive, considered, committee-based, or habit-driven.
5. **Objection intelligence** — surfaces the recurring barriers that block movement at each stage.
6. **Heuristic exposure** — identifies the shortcuts participants use when they do not fully evaluate options.
7. **Segment differentiation** — prevents a single decision model from being falsely applied across all participant types.
8. **Strategy translation** — feeds downstream pricing, onboarding, content sequencing, positioning, and trust work.
9. **Entry-vector support** — gives T10 a more realistic basis for segment selection and communication logic.

T3 is therefore the operating system’s participant-level decision-intelligence layer.

---

## 3. What This Document Is

This document is:

- a structured map of decision processes by participant type
- a cognitive and social commitment-pathway analysis
- an objection and influence architecture document
- a downstream input to pricing, onboarding, messaging, and content sequencing work
- a participant-specific complement to leverage and culture analysis

---

## 4. What This Document Is Not

The Participant Decision Architecture is **not**:

- a generic buyer journey
- a universal funnel template applied to all ecosystems
- a marketing persona document
- a feature prioritization memo
- a list of preferences without stage logic
- a speculative sales playbook disconnected from evidence

It should not flatten different participant types into one decision model or assume that a visible need automatically implies a predictable buying path.

---

## 5. When to Commission or Use It

T3 should be commissioned once the Ecosystem Analysis has defined distinct participant types and once T2 or other evidence makes it important to understand how those participants convert awareness or urgency into actual commitment.

It becomes mandatory whenever any of the following are true:

- sales, onboarding, pricing, or content-sequencing decisions are approaching
- segment selection depends on whether one participant type is easier or harder to move than another
- T10 Entry Vector Analysis will be produced
- there is material uncertainty about where decisions stall, who influences them, or what objections dominate
- the ecosystem contains multiple participant types with likely different decision logics

T3 is strongest when produced after T2, because trigger knowledge helps clarify when and why decision pathways compress or stall. fileciteturn9file0

---

## 6. Mandatory vs Conditional Use

**Mandatory** for:

- ecosystems with multiple distinct participant types
- opportunities where buying, switching, or trial behavior varies meaningfully by segment
- any effort that will later produce T10 Entry Vector Analysis
- any downstream work involving pricing psychology, onboarding flow design, objection handling, or content-by-stage logic

**Conditionally recommended** for:

- simple ecosystems with only one participant type and a trivial decision process
- early scans where segment-specific commitment pathways are not yet decision-relevant

Where segment-level decision complexity matters, T3 should be treated as required.

## 11. Core Analytical Questions

T3 should allow a reviewer to answer the following questions quickly:

1. Where does each participant type get decision-relevant information?
2. In what sequence do they evaluate options?
3. Who actually influences their choice?
4. What kind of social proof matters most?
5. How quickly do they decide, and what compresses or extends that timeline?
6. What objections appear at each stage?
7. Which objections are rational versus emotional?
8. What heuristics dominate when they cannot or will not evaluate fully?
9. What signals cause abandonment or permanent non-decision?
10. How do decision patterns differ meaningfully across participant types?

## 12. Required Structure of the Document

One complete Decision Architecture must be produced for **each participant type** identified in the Ecosystem Analysis. If the ecosystem has four participant types, the document contains four parallel analyses. fileciteturn8file3

Each participant analysis must contain the following five sections.

### 12.1 Section 1 — Information Architecture
**Governing question:** Where does this participant get the information that informs their decisions?

This section must document:

- primary information sources ranked by trust and frequency of use
- information-format preferences
- information sequence, such as what is checked first, second, and third
- information vetoes, meaning pieces of information that kill the decision immediately

The goal is to map not just sources, but the participant’s order of operations. fileciteturn8file7

### 12.2 Section 2 — Influence Chain
**Governing question:** Who influences this participant’s decisions, and in what sequence?

This section must document:

- primary influencers the participant consults or observes
- authority weight for each influencer type
- social-proof mechanics that are most persuasive
- anti-influences, meaning endorsements or associations that actually damage adoption

This section should distinguish visible attention from actual decision-shifting authority. fileciteturn8file7

### 12.3 Section 3 — Decision Timeline
**Governing question:** How long does this participant take to decide, and what can compress or extend that timeline?

This section must document:

- baseline decision speed
- compression factors, including those linked to T2 triggers
- extension factors that introduce delay
- abandonment threshold, meaning when delay becomes permanent non-decision

This section should reveal whether time is a soft variable or a hard strategic constraint. fileciteturn8file7

### 12.4 Section 4 — Objection Catalogue
**Governing question:** What objections arise at each decision stage, and what resolves them?

For each participant type, objections must be organized by decision stage:

- awareness-stage objections
- consideration-stage objections
- decision-stage objections
- post-decision objections

For each objection, document:

- the objection in the participant’s own language
- what evidence or experience resolves it
- whether the objection is rational or emotional

The catalogue must distinguish information gaps from trust or identity barriers. fileciteturn8file7

### 12.5 Section 5 — The Decision Heuristic
**Governing question:** When this participant doesn’t have time or energy for full evaluation, what shortcut do they use?

This section must identify the dominant heuristics used by the participant, such as:

- authority heuristic
- social proof heuristic
- price-as-quality heuristic
- default heuristic
- risk-aversion heuristic

The section must identify which heuristic dominates for each participant type and why. fileciteturn8file7

## 14. Information Architecture Standard

This section must reveal the participant’s information stack, not just a list of sources.

It should identify:

- which sources the participant trusts most
- which sources they actually use most often
- how format preference changes the credibility of a message
- what sequence they follow during real evaluation
- what immediate vetoes end evaluation regardless of other positives

The distinction between trusted information and consumed information should be preserved where they differ.

---

## 15. Influence Chain Standard

This section must map the social and informational chain around the participant’s decision.

It should identify:

- who the participant consults directly
- who they observe without consulting
- which endorsements meaningfully shift the outcome
- which associations create backlash or mistrust

Authority weight should be ranked, not merely described. The point is to show which influences actually move the participant. fileciteturn8file7

---

## 16. Decision Timeline Standard

The timeline section must distinguish between the participant’s default pace and the conditions that distort it.

Recommended baseline categories:

- impulse (minutes)
- considered (days to weeks)
- enterprise (weeks to months)

Compression factors should explicitly reference T2 where urgency triggers exist. Extension factors should include internal approval, budget timing, evaluation burden, switching cost, or social-risk concerns where relevant. fileciteturn8file7

---

## 17. Objection Catalogue Standard

The objection catalogue is one of the most operationally valuable parts of T3.

It must:

- organize objections by stage rather than lumping them together
- state objections in participant-native language where possible
- identify what resolves each objection
- distinguish rational objections from emotional objections

Rational objections are usually resolved with information, proof, compatibility, or economics.
Emotional objections are usually resolved with trust, social proof, status safety, or lived reassurance. fileciteturn8file7

---

## 18. Decision Heuristic Standard

This section must identify the shortcut logic participants use under cognitive or time pressure.

Most participants do not evaluate fully every time. T3 must therefore document which heuristic dominates when decision energy is low.

Recommended heuristic families include:

- authority
- social proof
- price-as-quality
- default / platform recommendation
- risk-aversion

Where possible, the analysis should explain when the participant shifts from full evaluation to heuristic use. fileciteturn8file7

---

## 19. Research Methodology Standard

The Participant Decision Architecture should follow a participant-level decision-mapping methodology.

Recommended minimum protocol:

### 19.1 Decision Evidence Scan
Collect participant-native evidence showing how options are compared, discussed, accepted, or rejected.

### 19.2 Recommendation Behavior Review
Analyze recommendation requests, peer advice patterns, reviews, and self-reported switching logic.

### 19.3 Objection Mining
Read high-friction threads, support complaints, review negatives, and hesitation language to identify stage-specific objections.

### 19.4 Heuristic Detection
Look for repeated shortcuts in phrasing, such as deference to authority, price shorthand, default choices, or visible crowd-following.

The methodology should prioritize actual decision behavior over idealized rational models.

---

## 20. Source and Evidence Expectations

The Participant Decision Architecture must remain evidence-based even when parts of the journey are partly inferred.

Source expectations:

- use participant-native material where possible
- distinguish observed decision patterns from inferred ones
- preserve participant phrasing for objections where analytically useful
- use repeated patterns before declaring a dominant heuristic or influence chain when feasible
- flag insufficient data explicitly rather than masking it with generic assumptions

Primary community and participant expression should be preferred over abstract marketing theory. fileciteturn9file0

---

## 21. Length Calibration

Recommended target length: **1,200–1,800 words per participant type**.  
For a four-participant ecosystem, the full document target is **4,800–7,200 words**.  
Maximum: **10,000 words total**. fileciteturn8file7

## 22. Required Usage Rules

The following rules are mandatory.

### Rule 1 — One architecture per participant type
Each participant type identified in the Ecosystem Analysis must receive a complete five-section analysis. fileciteturn8file3

### Rule 2 — No generic buyer-journey substitution
T3 must reflect ecosystem-specific decision logic rather than imported funnel assumptions.

### Rule 3 — No trigger blindness
Decision-timeline analysis must incorporate T2 compression and urgency logic where relevant. fileciteturn9file0

### Rule 4 — No objection flattening
Objections must be staged and classified, not collapsed into a single list.

### Rule 5 — No rationality overstatement
Heuristics must be treated as real decision drivers where evidence suggests shortcut behavior.

### Rule 6 — No product contamination
T3 may not contain product names, feature lists, or build plans. fileciteturn9file0

### Rule 7 — No hidden data gaps
If a section lacks sufficient evidence, it must be flagged explicitly and may recommend interviews, surveys, or community observation to fill the gap. fileciteturn8file7

## 23. Recommended Operating Procedure

### Step 1 — Anchor in the participant map
Use the Ecosystem Analysis to define the participant types requiring analysis.

### Step 2 — Import trigger context from T2
Clarify what conditions cause each participant to enter active decision mode.

### Step 3 — Map information and influence structure
Identify who informs the decision, in what order, and with what authority.

### Step 4 — Map timeline and abandonment dynamics
Clarify how quickly the participant decides and what lengthens or compresses the process.

### Step 5 — Catalogue objections by stage
Capture recurring objections in participant-native language and identify what resolves them.

### Step 6 — Identify dominant heuristics
Determine what shortcut logic takes over when the participant does not fully evaluate.

### Step 7 — Repeat for each participant type
Produce a complete architecture for every participant type in the ecosystem.

---

## 24. Quality Standard / Acceptance Criteria

The Participant Decision Architecture should be considered acceptable only if all of the following are true:

1. Every participant type has a complete five-section analysis.
2. Information sources are ranked and sequenced, not merely listed.
3. Influence chains identify real decision-shifting actors and anti-influences.
4. Decision timelines distinguish baseline speed, compression factors, extension factors, and abandonment threshold.
5. Objections are organized by stage and classified as rational or emotional.
6. Dominant heuristics are identified clearly.
7. T2 trigger logic is reflected where relevant.
8. Evidence and inference are distinguished where needed.
9. Data gaps are explicitly flagged when present.
10. The document gives downstream work a realistic picture of how each participant type commits to action.

---

## 25. Failure Modes and Misuse Warnings

Common failure modes include:

### 25.1 Generic funnel drift
Using standard buyer-journey language without ecosystem-specific evidence.

### 25.2 Segment collapse
Treating all participant types as though they decide in the same way.

### 25.3 Trigger omission
Ignoring the urgency or switching conditions already identified in T2.

### 25.4 Objection vagueness
Stating generic concerns like “trust” or “price” without stage, language, or resolution path.

### 25.5 Heuristic blindness
Assuming participants evaluate rationally when they clearly use shortcuts.

### 25.6 Social-proof simplification
Treating all social proof as interchangeable rather than source- and context-dependent.

### 25.7 Evidence thinness
Making strong claims about participant psychology from weak or one-off observations.

## 27. Relationship to Agentic Research Systems

The Participant Decision Architecture should be structured so that agents can assist with gathering participant-native decision evidence without mistaking extraction for interpretation.

This means:

- each participant type should have a stable block structure
- information, influence, timeline, objections, and heuristics should be separated cleanly
- participant-native language should be preserved where useful
- inferred patterns should remain distinguishable from directly observed ones
- explicit data gaps should be recordable rather than hidden

An agent may help surface decision patterns and stage-specific objections.
The operating system must still judge what the participant’s decision logic actually is.

---

## 28. Versioning Notes

The Participant Decision Architecture Standard should evolve when any of the following occur:

- repeated work reveals missing decision-pattern categories or heuristic families
- downstream validation under T15 shows that objections were poorly staged or incorrectly classified
- trust analysis under T6 exposes missing influence-chain detail
- T19 learning shows that segment selection failed because the wrong participant decision model was assumed
- platform ecosystems under T17 require stronger treatment of platform-mediated default heuristics or review-based vetoes

Version updates should record what decision-intelligence weakness prompted the revision.

---

## 29. Final Standard Statement

A participant does not move from need to commitment in a straight line. They move through information, influence, hesitation, shortcuts, and social risk.

The Participant Decision Architecture exists to make that path visible for each participant type in the ecosystem. It is the difference between knowing that someone should act and understanding how they actually decide.

Without T3, the operating system can identify who matters and what pressures exist.
With T3, it can understand how those people actually choose.
