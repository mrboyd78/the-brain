# Strategic Timing and Market Timing Deep Research Prompts

These prompts are designed for deep research on strategic timing, market timing, readiness windows, category maturity, adoption timing, and sequence advantage conducted entirely through public information and LLM-assisted analysis, without interviews or surveys.

Each prompt is copy-and-paste ready and includes:
- master operating instructions
- a rigor add-on
- a consistent output format

## Prompt 1 — Is This Product Category Too Early, Too Late, or Well-Timed?

```text
Conduct deep research using only publicly available information. Do not use interviews, surveys, invented examples, or unsupported speculation. Use current sources where recency matters. Distinguish clearly between:
1. verified facts,
2. reasoned inference,
3. uncertain or contested conclusions.

For every major claim, cite sources. Prioritize primary sources, official product pages, documentation, pricing pages, marketplace listings, public case studies, review ecosystems, credible industry research, comparison content, investor materials when relevant, changelogs, and high-quality operator commentary when directly relevant.

Your task is not merely to summarize trends. Your task is to identify commercially meaningful timing signals, category-readiness indicators, adoption constraints, sequencing logic, competitive windows, and strategic implications.

For each section of the answer, include:
- what is directly evidenced,
- what appears to be changing in a way that affects timing,
- what the market may be underestimating,
- what this implies for a founder deciding whether to enter now, wait, narrow, or avoid.

Where evidence is weak, speculative, or unstable, say so explicitly.

Now conduct deep research on the question: “Is the category of [INSERT PRODUCT OR CATEGORY] too early, too late, or well-timed?”

Analyze:
- buyer readiness,
- workflow readiness,
- infrastructure maturity,
- ecosystem maturity,
- competitive crowding,
- pricing tolerance,
- trust and governance readiness,
- platform support or hostility,
- implementation burden,
- macro trend alignment,
- signs of commoditization,
- signs of unmet demand persisting.

Then produce:
- a category-specific timing analysis,
- the strongest arguments that the timing is favorable,
- the strongest arguments that the timing is poor or dangerous,
- a framework for distinguishing real timing advantage from hype or lagging entry,
- a final recommendation on whether the category is early, late, or appropriately timed.

Before finalizing the answer, perform an explicit internal adversarial review of your own conclusions. Identify at least five ways your reasoning may be weak, incomplete, biased, overly optimistic, or too dependent on limited public evidence or unstable market conditions. Then revise the answer accordingly.

Use this exact output structure:
1. Executive Thesis
2. What the Evidence Shows
3. Why the Timing Appears Early, Late, or Well-Timed
4. Weak or Misleading Timing Narratives
5. Strategic Implications for a Founder
```
