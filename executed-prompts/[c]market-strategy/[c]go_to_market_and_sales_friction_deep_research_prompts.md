# Go-to-Market and Sales-Friction Deep Research Prompts

These prompts are designed for deep research on go-to-market design, sales friction, conversion blockers, trust requirements, and buying-process reality conducted entirely through public information and LLM-assisted analysis, without interviews or surveys.

Each prompt is copy-and-paste ready and includes:
- master operating instructions
- a rigor add-on
- a consistent output format

---

## Prompt 1 — What Actually Blocks Buyers From Purchasing in This Category?

```text
Conduct deep research using only publicly available information. Do not use interviews, surveys, invented examples, or unsupported speculation. Use current sources where recency matters. Distinguish clearly between:
1. verified facts,
2. reasoned inference,
3. uncertain or contested conclusions.

For every major claim, cite sources. Prioritize primary sources, official product pages, pricing pages, security pages, procurement docs, implementation guides, marketplace listings, review ecosystems, public case studies, credible industry research, comparison content, and high-quality operator commentary when directly relevant.

Your task is not merely to summarize. Your task is to identify commercially meaningful buying friction, trust barriers, implementation fears, approval bottlenecks, proof requirements, conversion blockers, and strategic implications.

For each section of the answer, include:
- what is known,
- what appears to be changing,
- what the market may be underestimating,
- what this implies for a founder designing go-to-market and sales conversion.

Where evidence is weak, say so explicitly.

Now conduct deep research on the question: “What actually blocks buyers from purchasing products in the category of [INSERT PRODUCT OR CATEGORY]?”

Analyze blockers such as:
- unclear ROI,
- implementation fear,
- security and compliance concerns,
- vendor trust gaps,
- pricing confusion,
- weak proof,
- unclear differentiation,
- workflow disruption risk,
- internal approvals,
- procurement friction,
- team adoption anxiety,
- unclear onboarding expectations,
- switching risk.

Then produce:
- a category-specific purchase-friction analysis,
- the strongest blockers to adoption,
- the weakest or most overrated friction assumptions,
- a framework for distinguishing superficial hesitation from true buying resistance,
- a final recommendation for what a new entrant must solve to convert buyers.

Before finalizing the answer, perform an explicit internal adversarial review of your own conclusions. Identify at least five ways your reasoning may be weak, incomplete, biased, overly optimistic, or too dependent on limited public evidence. Then revise the answer accordingly.

Use this exact output structure:
1. Executive Thesis
2. What the Evidence Shows
3. What Actually Blocks Buyers in This Category
4. Weak or Overrated Sales-Friction Assumptions
5. Strategic Implications for a Founder
6. Risks, Counterarguments, and Uncertainty
7. Final Recommendations
8. Source List
```
